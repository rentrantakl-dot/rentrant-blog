Rent increases feel confronting, especially when housing costs are already stretched. The good news is that New Zealand law gives tenants meaningful protection here — your landlord cannot simply decide to raise the rent and expect you to absorb it on short notice. There are clear rules, and breaking them means the increase has no legal effect.

### 1. 90 days written notice — no exceptions

Your landlord must give you at least 90 days notice before a rent increase takes effect. That notice period is not negotiable and there are no exceptions in the law. If your landlord hands you a letter on 1 April saying rent goes up on 1 May, that increase is not valid no matter how reasonable the new amount might be.

### 2. Rent can only go up once every 12 months

Your landlord can only increase your rent once in any 12-month period. If your rent was raised in October last year, you cannot legally be issued another increase until October this year — even with 90 days notice. Two increases in 12 months is a breach of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, full stop.

### 3. The notice must be in writing

A text message, a phone call, a conversation at the door — none of these are valid. The notice must be in writing, and it must be delivered in a way that meets the Act's requirements: by hand, by post, or by email if you have agreed to receive notices that way. If you have not agreed to email notices in writing, your landlord cannot rely on email alone.

### 4. The notice must state the new amount and the date it takes effect

A vague notice saying "rent will be going up soon" is not a valid notice. The written notice must specify exactly what the new rent will be and exactly when it takes effect. If either piece of information is missing, the notice is defective and the increase does not apply until a valid notice is issued.

### 5. Fixed-term tenants have extra protection

If you are in a fixed-term tenancy — one that runs to a set end date — your landlord generally cannot raise your rent during that fixed term at all. The only exception is if the tenancy agreement itself includes a clause specifically allowing rent increases during the fixed term. If your agreement is silent on the matter, your rent is locked until the fixed term ends. Read your agreement carefully before signing.

### 6. The 12-month clock starts from when the last increase took effect

This catches some tenants off guard. The 12-month gap is measured from the date the previous increase actually took effect — not the date the landlord sent the notice, and not the date the tenancy started. If your last increase took effect on 15 November, the earliest a new increase can take effect is 15 November the following year. Count from the effect date, not the notice date.

### 7. You can challenge an increase at the Tenancy Tribunal

If your landlord follows all the procedural rules but the new rent is unreasonably above the market rate for comparable properties in your area, you can apply to the Tenancy Tribunal to have it reviewed. The Tribunal can set the rent back to a reasonable level. This is not a tool to avoid any increase at all — the Tribunal looks at comparable market rents — but it does provide a check against opportunistic overreach. Gather evidence of comparable rents before applying.

If your landlord has issued a notice that does not follow these rules, the increase does not apply. You are not obligated to pay the higher amount until a valid notice has been issued and the full 90-day period has passed. Keep every notice you receive in writing and note the dates. If you need to challenge anything, that paper trail is your evidence.
