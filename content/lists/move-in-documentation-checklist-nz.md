Move-in day is hectic. You have keys, a van, and a dozen things to sort out. Documentation is the last thing you feel like doing. Do it anyway. Everything you skip on day one is a potential deduction from your bond on the way out. The inspection report and your photos are your only protection against being charged for damage that was already there when you arrived.

Do all of this before you bring in a single piece of furniture. Once boxes are in and you are living there, it is much harder to prove the baseline condition of anything.

**Before you even get to move-in day, make sure you checked all the right things before signing.** [Our signing checklist covers what to look for before you commit to a tenancy](/posts/practical-nz-renter-checklist-2026/).

### 1. Photograph every room systematically

Walk through each room in order — walls, floors, ceilings, built-in wardrobes, window frames. Take photos in landscape orientation so context is visible, and make sure the timestamp function on your phone is on. Do not skip the hallways, laundry, garage, or any outdoor areas included in your tenancy. More is more here.

### 2. Record all existing damage on the inspection report

Your landlord should provide a property inspection report. Every mark, scuff, stain, chip, or crack you can see should go on that report. Sign it, make sure your landlord or property manager signs it, and keep your copy. If the inspection report does not reflect what you are seeing, add your own notes and photograph the discrepancy.

### 3. Test every lock

Check every external door lock and every window latch. If any outbuildings, sheds, or garages are included in your tenancy, test those too. A door that does not lock properly on move-in day is both a security problem and a potential Healthy Homes breach — landlords are required to provide adequate security. Report any failures in writing on the same day.

### 4. Test the smoke alarms

Your landlord is legally required to ensure smoke alarms are working before you move in. Press the test button on every alarm and note the result. If an alarm is missing or not functioning, notify your landlord in writing immediately. Smoke alarms are not optional and the obligation to install and maintain them sits with the landlord.

### 5. Run every tap and check under sinks

Turn every tap on and off, check the water pressure, and look under every sink for signs of leaking, water damage, or mould on the cabinetry. Flush every toilet and check that it refills properly. Active leaks are urgent repairs — get them on the record on day one.

### 6. Test all included appliances

If the tenancy includes an oven, hob, extractor fan, dishwasher, heat pump, or any other appliance, test each one. Turn on every burner, run the extractor, cycle the heat pump through heating and cooling. Any appliance that does not function on arrival goes on the inspection report as a pre-existing fault — that way you cannot be charged for it later.

### 7. Check the hot water temperature

Under Healthy Homes Standards, hot water at the tap must reach at least 55°C. You can test this with a basic cooking thermometer — run the hot tap for a minute and measure. If the water is not reaching that temperature, the hot water system either needs adjustment or repair. Put the result in writing to your landlord.

### 8. Photograph the meter readings

On your first day, photograph the electricity meter, gas meter (if applicable), and water meter. These are the baseline readings that your billing starts from. Disputes about utility costs are rare but they do happen, and a timestamped photo of the meter on day one resolves them instantly.

### 9. Note the condition of carpet, curtains, and blinds

Pre-existing stains, worn patches, faded curtains, broken blind cords — all of these need to be on the record. Carpet condition is one of the most common sources of bond disputes. If the carpet was already stained in the corner of the bedroom when you moved in, you need a photo dated to day one or you will likely pay for it at the end.

### 10. Check the Healthy Homes compliance statement

Your landlord must provide a Healthy Homes compliance statement with or before the start of the tenancy. It should state whether the property meets the heating, insulation, ventilation, moisture, and draught standards. Walk through the property and check that what you can physically see matches what the statement says. If there are obvious gaps — no extractor in the bathroom, no fixed heater in the lounge — note them and raise them in writing.

### 11. Send a written summary of issues to your landlord within 48 hours

A photo on your phone only helps you if the landlord knows about the issue and there is a record of them being told. Within 24 to 48 hours of moving in, send a written summary of everything you found — email is fine — and keep a copy. This creates a timestamped paper trail that is very difficult to dispute later.

### 12. Back everything up off your phone

Your phone can be lost, stolen, or broken. Upload your photos to cloud storage the same day — Google Photos, iCloud, Dropbox, wherever — and make sure the backup actually completed. A photo that only exists on a broken phone is no protection at all. The same goes for the signed inspection report: scan it or photograph it and store it somewhere you can access without the original.

---

None of this takes more than an hour, and it protects weeks of bond money. A thorough move-in record is the single most practical thing you can do as a renter.
