New Zealand landlords are not just contractually obligated to maintain rental properties — they are legally required to under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986 and the Healthy Homes Standards. Many tenants do not know exactly what is covered or how quickly a landlord must act. These are the ten things that landlords have no legal discretion to ignore, along with the timeframes that apply.

**For a full picture of what landlords owe you across every aspect of a tenancy,** [read our guide to landlord obligations in NZ](/posts/landlord-obligations-nz/).

### 1. Urgent repairs — 24 hours or less

Urgent repairs include anything that threatens safety, makes the property uninhabitable, or cuts off an essential service: burst or blocked pipes, gas leaks, electrical faults, no running water, and broken door or window locks that compromise security. Your landlord must respond immediately, or within 24 hours at the outside. This is not a 14-day situation — if they cannot act themselves, they must arrange a contractor. Document when you notified them and how.

### 2. Non-urgent repairs — 14 days from written notice

For repairs that are necessary but not urgent — a leaking tap, a broken rangehood, a damaged fence — the standard is a reasonable time. In practice, Tenancy Tribunal decisions treat 14 days as the outer limit for most non-urgent repairs after you have given written notice. Always put your repair requests in writing and keep a copy. Verbal requests are very hard to rely on if the matter goes to the Tribunal.

### 3. Fixed heating in the main living room

Under Healthy Homes Standards, the main living room must have a fixed heater capable of heating the room to at least 18°C when the temperature outside is 1°C. Plug-in electric heaters do not qualify — the heater must be fixed and appropriately sized for the room. Heat pumps, wood burners, and fixed electric panel heaters can all qualify if they meet the capacity requirement. If your lounge does not have a qualifying heater, your landlord is in breach.

### 4. Ceiling and underfloor insulation

Insulation must meet the 2008 building code as a minimum standard, with higher requirements in newer builds. Your landlord cannot remove existing insulation. If the insulation is damaged, missing, or below standard, they are required to bring it up to the required level. The Healthy Homes compliance statement your landlord provides should disclose the insulation status — check it against what you can actually see or access.

### 5. Extractor fans in kitchen and bathroom

Every kitchen must have an extractor fan or range hood that vents to the outside. Every bathroom and toilet must also have ventilation meeting the standard — either an extractor fan ducted outside, or a window that opens. A window alone does not satisfy the kitchen ventilation requirement. Extractor fans that vent into the ceiling cavity rather than outside also do not comply. These requirements exist because moisture is one of the primary causes of mould in NZ rentals.

### 6. Adequate drainage — no ponding water

Your landlord must ensure the property has effective drainage for groundwater, stormwater, and surface water. If water pools in the garden, under the house, or against the foundation after rain, that is a drainage problem your landlord must fix. Persistent dampness under the floor can damage insulation, contribute to mould, and create health problems. Report it in writing and give your landlord a reasonable time to address it.

### 7. Draught stopping

Landlords must fill unreasonable gaps in walls, ceilings, floors, and around windows and doors that allow draughts into the living areas. This does not mean making a house perfectly airtight — it means blocking gaps that are large enough to affect the temperature or habitable comfort of the space. Fireplaces that are not used must also be blocked. If your property has obvious draught problems, raise them in writing as a Healthy Homes compliance matter.

### 8. Hot water at 55°C

The hot water system must deliver water at a temperature of at least 55°C at the tap. This temperature is required to prevent the growth of legionella bacteria in the hot water system. If your hot water is consistently lukewarm, the cylinder temperature may need adjustment or the system may need repair. Check it with a thermometer — run the hot tap for a minute before measuring. Below 55°C is a Healthy Homes breach, not just an inconvenience.

### 9. Smoke alarms on every level

Landlords must install working smoke alarms on every level of the property. Ionisation or photoelectric alarms both comply. The landlord is responsible for installing them and for ensuring they are in working order at the start of each new tenancy. Tenants are responsible for not tampering with them and for replacing batteries in long-life battery alarms. If an alarm is missing, expired, or not working on arrival, report it in writing immediately — this is both a Healthy Homes matter and a basic safety issue.

### 10. Bond lodgement with Tenancy Services

This one surprises people. If you paid a bond, your landlord is legally required to lodge it with Tenancy Services within 23 working days of receiving it. The bond does not sit with the landlord — it goes into a government-held account. You should receive a receipt from Tenancy Services confirming your bond has been lodged. If you have not received that confirmation within a month of paying, check directly on the Tenancy Services website using your name and address. An unlodged bond is a breach of the Act.

---

If your landlord is not meeting any of these obligations, the first step is always to raise it in writing. Tenancy Tribunal applications carry more weight when there is a clear record of you notifying the landlord and them failing to act.
