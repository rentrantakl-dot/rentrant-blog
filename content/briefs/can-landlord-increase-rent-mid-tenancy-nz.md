Yes — but in New Zealand, only once every 12 months, only with **60 days' written notice**, and on a fixed-term tenancy only if your agreement actually allows it. If your landlord has bumped the rent more often than that, the increase isn't enforceable.

---

## The 12-Month Rule

Since 12 August 2020, NZ landlords can only raise the rent **once every 12 months**. The clock starts from the beginning of the tenancy, or from the date of the last increase — whichever is more recent.

This applies whether you're on a periodic or fixed-term tenancy. If your landlord increased the rent ten months ago and tries again now, that second increase is invalid. You don't have to pay it.

The full rule is set out in [section 24 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html).

---

## The 60-Day Notice Rule

A rent increase notice has to be in writing. Email or text is fine, as long as it's clear. The notice must include:

- The new rent amount
- The date the increase takes effect (at least 60 days from when you receive the notice)

If your landlord tells you over the phone or hands you a notice with only 30 days' warning, the increase isn't enforceable on the stated date. They have to start again with a fresh, compliant notice.

---

## Fixed-Term vs Periodic Tenancies

The 12-month and 60-day rules apply to both, with one important catch:

- **Periodic tenancy:** Landlord can issue a rent increase notice at any time, subject to the 12-month and 60-day rules.
- **Fixed-term tenancy:** Rent can only be increased if the agreement specifically includes a clause allowing it. If your fixed-term agreement is silent on rent increases, your rent stays the same for the entire term.

Always check the agreement before paying an increase on a fixed-term tenancy.

---

## How to Challenge an Increase

If the new rent is substantially above market rent for comparable properties nearby, you can apply to the [Tenancy Tribunal](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/disputes/) within **6 months** of the increase taking effect.

Use the [Tenancy Services market rent tool](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/market-rent/) to check the going rate for similar properties in your suburb. The tool draws on actual bond lodgement data — it's the same source the Tribunal looks at.

The application fee is $28. If your landlord has breached the notice rules (wrong format, less than 60 days, or within 12 months of the last increase), the increase can be set aside without needing the market-rent argument at all.
