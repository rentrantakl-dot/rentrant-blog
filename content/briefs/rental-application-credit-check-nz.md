Yes, NZ landlords can run credit checks on rental applicants. You must give written consent first, and they cannot charge you a fee for it — credit check fees are prohibited under [section 17 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM94278.html) as unlawful "key money."

---

## What a credit check actually shows

A credit report gives a landlord a snapshot of your financial history: payment defaults, court judgments against you, accounts in collections, and a record of how many credit enquiries have been made. It's a financial picture — not a character assessment.

It does **not** show:
- Your ethnicity or national origin
- Your employment status
- Your Tenancy Tribunal history (that's a separate search entirely)

Not every landlord runs one. Private landlords often skip it; larger property management companies are more likely to make it standard. Either way, the same rules apply to both.

---

## Written consent is required

Before accessing your credit report, a landlord must have your **written consent** — required under the [Credit Reporting Privacy Code](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tools/guidance/business-guidance/credit-reporting-guidance/). Most rental application forms include a consent clause near the signature section. Read it before you sign.

Running a credit check without consent is a breach of the Privacy Act 2020. If that happens, you can file a complaint with the [Privacy Commissioner](https://www.privacy.org.nz/).

---

## They cannot charge you for it

Application fees and credit check fees are **prohibited under section 17 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986** as "key money." The landlord absorbs this cost — not you. If someone asks you to pay, decline. You can report it to [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/letting-fees-and-application-fees/).

---

## Tribunal records are a separate search

This surprises a lot of renters. Landlords can search the Tenancy Tribunal orders database by your name at [forms.justice.govt.nz/search/TT](https://forms.justice.govt.nz/search/TT/) — no credit check required, no consent needed. It's a public record.

The online database covers approximately the last three years of published decisions. If you have a past Tribunal case in that window, it's better to mention it briefly in your cover letter than to hope the landlord doesn't find it.

| Check | What it finds | Consent required? |
|---|---|---|
| Credit check | Financial defaults, court judgments | Yes — written consent |
| Tribunal search | Past tenancy disputes, bond cases | No — public record |
| Reference check | Character, payment history, property condition | No |

---

## If your credit history has problems

If you have defaults on your file, a short note in your cover letter — explaining the context and what's changed — beats saying nothing. Landlords are more likely to look past a single historic default with an honest explanation than to reject without one. Credit file retention periods are defined by the [Credit Reporting Privacy Code](https://www.privacy.org.nz/tools/guidance/business-guidance/credit-reporting-guidance/).
