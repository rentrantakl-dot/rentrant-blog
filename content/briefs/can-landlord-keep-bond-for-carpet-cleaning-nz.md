No. In most cases your landlord cannot keep your bond for carpet cleaning. They can only deduct from your bond for damage that goes **beyond fair wear and tear** — and carpet cleaned from ordinary use doesn't meet that threshold.

---

## What Fair Wear and Tear Means for Carpets

Fair wear and tear is the gradual deterioration you'd expect from someone actually living in a property. For carpets, that includes:

- Flattened pile in hallways and high-traffic areas
- Minor fading from sunlight
- Light, general soiling from everyday foot traffic
- Small indentations where furniture sat

None of these are damage. A landlord cannot charge you for them.

What *can* legitimately be deducted: stains from spills that weren't cleaned up, burns, pet damage, or carpet left in a significantly filthy state that goes beyond what normal living would produce.

For a full breakdown of what counts and what doesn't across the whole property, see [What Counts as Fair Wear and Tear in NZ Rentals](/briefs/fair-wear-and-tear-nz-rentals/).

---

## Lease Clauses Requiring Professional Cleaning Are Void

Under s45A of the Residential Tenancies Act (amended 2020), any clause in your tenancy agreement that requires you to professionally clean the carpets at the end of the tenancy is **void** — unless the property was professionally cleaned immediately before your tenancy began. Even then, you can only be required to return the carpets to the same standard, not necessarily pay for professional cleaning.

This means landlords cannot enforce blanket "must have carpets professionally cleaned on exit" clauses. If yours has one, it has no legal effect.

---

## The Evidence Problem for Landlords

To succeed at the Tenancy Tribunal on a carpet claim, a landlord has to prove the carpet was in better condition at the **start** of your tenancy than when you left. That means a signed inspection report, photos taken before you moved in, and evidence of the carpet's age and original condition.

If there's no move-in inspection report, or the landlord can't produce it, the Tribunal regularly finds in the tenant's favour. The burden of proof sits with the landlord, not you.

This is why your move-in inspection matters. Take photos on day one — date-stamped, every room, every stain that was already there.

---

## If Your Landlord Is Disputing the Bond

You don't have to accept a deduction you disagree with. Apply to have the bond refunded through Tenancy Services at tenancy.govt.nz — it costs nothing and the process is straightforward online.

Once you apply, the landlord has to file their own claim for deductions. If they do, it goes to the Tribunal. Bring your move-in photos, inspection report, and any written communication. The Tribunal sees carpet disputes constantly and applies consistent standards.

> **Key point:** A claim of "we always get carpets professionally cleaned at the end of every tenancy" is not a legal basis for a bond deduction — and any lease clause requiring it is void under s45A of the Residential Tenancies Act. The condition has to have actually deteriorated beyond fair wear and tear during your tenancy.
