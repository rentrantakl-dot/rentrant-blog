Yes — but only with written notice each time. Even once either party has served notice to end the tenancy, the flat's still your home. The landlord's right to show it doesn't override your right to live in it properly.

---

## What the Law Says

Under [section 48 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html#DLM95220), a landlord may enter to show the property to prospective tenants — but only after giving you at least **24 hours' written notice**. The notice must state the reason and when they'll be coming.

That means:

| Notice type | Valid? |
|---|---|
| Written, 30 hours' notice, reason stated | Yes |
| Text message, 28 hours' notice, reason stated | Yes |
| Verbal mention the day before | No |
| "I'll be showing the flat on all Saturday mornings for the next month" | No |
| Same-day message asking to pop in | No (unless you agree) |

A landlord cannot give one notice and treat it as covering all future viewings. Each visit needs its own.

---

## During a Notice Period

It doesn't matter who served notice first. Until the tenancy actually ends, the entry rules are the same. Your landlord can start showing the flat once notice is in — but only with proper notice for each viewing, at a reasonable time.

If viewings start happening daily, or seem timed to make you uncomfortable rather than to find a new tenant, that's worth noting. Under [section 38 of the RTA](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html#DLM94943), you have a right to quiet enjoyment. Excessive viewings used as pressure tactics can be challenged at the Tenancy Tribunal.

---

## What to Do If Your Landlord Ignores the Rules

Write to them (text or email is fine) and name the issue: "You entered on [date] without the required 24 hours' written notice." Keep a record. If it continues, [apply to the Tenancy Tribunal](https://www.justice.govt.nz/tribunals/tenancy-tribunal/) — the fee is $28, and unlawful entry is a straightforward claim.
