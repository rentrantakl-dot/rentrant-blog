From 16 April 2026, New Zealand has clear legal rules around methamphetamine contamination in rental properties. Before these regulations, there was no binding legal framework — landlords and tenants navigated inconsistent guidance with no enforceable thresholds. The [Residential Tenancies (Managing Methamphetamine Contamination) Regulations 2026](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/properties-affected-by-meth/new-meth-regulations-as-of-april-2026/) change that.

---

## The Two Thresholds That Now Matter

The regulations set two contamination levels, each with different consequences:

| Level | Reading | What it triggers |
|---|---|---|
| Contaminated | Above 15µg/100cm² | Professional decontamination required |
| Uninhabitable | Above 30µg/100cm² | Either party may end the tenancy |

**At 15µg/100cm²:** The landlord must arrange and fund professional decontamination. After the work is done, the property must be re-tested and confirmed below the threshold before it can be re-let. A landlord cannot lease a property they know is contaminated above this level.

**At 30µg/100cm²:** The property is considered uninhabitable. A tenant can give just **2 days' notice** to leave. A landlord wishing to end the tenancy must give **7 days' notice**.

---

## When Must a Landlord Test?

Professional testing is mandatory in two situations:

1. Police or a local council notify the landlord that meth was manufactured on the property
2. A screening assessment comes back above **15µg/100cm²**

Landlords must share test results with all tenants within **7 days** of receiving them — whether the news is good or bad.

---

## Your Rights If Contamination Is Found

If professional testing confirms contamination below the 30µg uninhabitable threshold, the landlord is responsible for decontaminating the property as soon as practicable. You're entitled to discuss a rent reduction while the work is being done. If only a minor, physically separate area of the property is affected, the regulations require a rent reduction to reflect the loss of access to that part.

If you and your landlord can't agree on arrangements, either party can apply to the [Tenancy Tribunal](https://www.justice.govt.nz/tribunals/tenancy-tribunal/).

---

## What If the Tenant Caused It?

If a tenant caused the contamination — through manufacture or use — the liability picture changes. That's a breach of both the [Misuse of Drugs Act 1975](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1975/0116/latest/whole.html) and the tenancy agreement. A landlord can apply to the Tenancy Tribunal to recover costs, though the cap on what can be recovered from a tenant is **$1,800**.
