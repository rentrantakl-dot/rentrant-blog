You have to leave the place "reasonably clean and tidy" — but you don't have to pay for professional cleaning unless the property was professionally cleaned immediately before you moved in. That's the rule under [section 40 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html#DLM95049).

Anything else is just landlord preference.

---

## What "Reasonably Clean" Actually Means

[Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/ending-a-tenancy/cleaning-and-damage/) describes the standard as the condition a reasonable person would expect — not perfect, not professional-grade. Practically, that's vacuumed floors, wiped surfaces, oven and rangehood cleaned of grease, bathroom scrubbed, rubbish out, and the fridge defrosted if you used it.

It does not mean steam-cleaned carpets or showroom finish. The benchmark is the next tenant being able to move in without having to clean first.

---

## Professional-Cleaning Clauses Are Mostly Void

Under [section 45A of the Residential Tenancies Act](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html#DLM95058) (as amended in 2020), any clause in a tenancy agreement that requires the tenant to professionally clean the property at the end of the tenancy is **void** — unless the property was professionally cleaned immediately before the tenancy began.

Even when that exception applies, you only have to return the property to the same standard you got it in. You don't have to use a professional cleaner to get there.

If your tenancy agreement has a blanket "professional cleaning required on exit" clause, it has no legal force on its own. The same logic applies to carpet cleaning — see [Can My Landlord Keep My Bond for Carpet Cleaning?](/briefs/can-landlord-keep-bond-for-carpet-cleaning-nz/) for the full breakdown.

## When the Landlord Can Deduct From Your Bond

A bond deduction for cleaning only stands up at the Tenancy Tribunal if the landlord can show:

1. The property fell below the reasonably-clean standard at the end of the tenancy (photos help here)
2. They actually paid to bring it back to that standard (invoice required)
3. The cost is reasonable

Common deductions that succeed: greasy ovens left untouched, mouldy bathrooms, bins left full, rubbish dumped in the garage. Common deductions that fail: routine vacuum marks, light dust, "we just like everything professionally cleaned."

The burden of proof sits with the landlord. If they can't produce evidence the property was in better condition at the start, the deduction is hard to defend.

---

## What to Do at Move-Out

Take date-stamped photos of every room once you've cleaned, and save them somewhere you'll still have access in six months. If a cleaning deduction lands on your final bond settlement and you don't agree, don't sign the refund form — [apply to Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/disputes/) for the full refund and your landlord then has to justify the deduction. See also [How Long Does a Landlord Have to Return My Bond?](/briefs/how-long-does-landlord-have-to-return-bond-nz/) and the [fair wear and tear brief](/briefs/fair-wear-and-tear-nz-rentals/).

> **Key point:** Move-out photos are your insurance. They take ten minutes and they win Tribunal cases.
