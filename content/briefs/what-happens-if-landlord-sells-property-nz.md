Your tenancy doesn't automatically end when your landlord sells the property. In New Zealand, there are two very different outcomes depending on whether the sale requires the property to be empty — and one of them just means you carry on with a new landlord.

---

## Sale Without Vacant Possession: Tenancy Continues

If your landlord sells *without* requiring vacant possession — meaning the buyer is happy to take on an existing tenancy — nothing changes for you. The buyer steps into your landlord's shoes. Same rent, same obligations on both sides, same tenancy agreement. You don't sign anything new.

This is actually common for investment properties. The new owner is legally bound by the terms of your existing tenancy from day one.

---

## Sale With Vacant Possession: 42 Days Notice

If the sale agreement *requires* the property to be empty at settlement, your landlord must give you **at least 42 days written notice** under the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/ending-a-tenancy/ending-a-periodic-tenancy/). That notice can only be issued once the sale has gone unconditional — meaning finance approved, building inspection done, all conditions waived.

An offer on the table or a listing on Trade Me doesn't count. "We're thinking of selling" doesn't count. The unconditional date is when the 42-day clock starts.

If the sale falls through before settlement, the notice becomes void. You'd stay, and your landlord would have to issue fresh notice if they sell again.

---

## Fixed-Term Tenancies Are Protected

On a fixed-term tenancy? A sale cannot cut that short. The new owner inherits your tenancy and must see out the fixed term. Their intention to move in, renovate, or do anything else doesn't override your right to stay until the term expires.

You can agree to an early termination if you want to — say you find a better flat — but that agreement needs to be in writing and signed by both parties. Don't feel pressured into it.

---

## What to Do When Notice Arrives

42 days doesn't go far in the Auckland rental market. A few things worth doing immediately:

- Check the notice is valid — it must be in writing and state that the sale requires vacant possession
- Ask your landlord to confirm the sale is unconditional; you're entitled to that assurance
- Start looking straight away; don't assume the settlement date will push out
