Not if the appliance was included in your tenancy. Under [section 45 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html#DLM95034), your landlord must keep the premises and any provided chattels in a **reasonable state of repair**. If the fridge came with the flat and it's packed up through no fault of yours, that's their job to sort.

---

## When It's the Landlord's Problem

The key is whether the appliance is listed in your tenancy agreement — usually in a chattels schedule that specifies what's included. Common ones: stove, oven, rangehood, fridge, dishwasher, washing machine, dryer.

If it's on the list and it breaks through normal use, the landlord must repair or replace it within a reasonable time. "Reasonable" depends on how essential the appliance is — a broken stove or oven gets higher urgency than a faulty tumble dryer. Age of the appliance factors into what repair looks like: they might replace rather than repair, but "we won't do anything" is not a valid response for a listed chattel.

[Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/repairs-and-maintenance/repairs-and-maintenance-overview/) covers the landlord's maintenance obligations in more detail.

---

## When It's Your Problem

If you broke it — dropped something in the oven, overloaded the washing machine, let water get somewhere it shouldn't — the repair cost is on you. The same standard that applies to fair wear and tear on the rest of the property applies here too.

Accidental damage that wasn't your fault (a power surge, a manufacturing fault, something that failed without warning) is generally the landlord's responsibility regardless of the cause. The distinction is between negligence and bad luck.

---

## Appliances You Brought Yourself

If the appliance wasn't listed in the tenancy — you turned up with your own washing machine, or the flat never had a fridge and you bought one — it's entirely yours. The landlord has no obligation to maintain it and no right to touch it.

---

## What to Do If They Won't Fix It

1. **Put the request in writing** — email with the appliance, the fault, and the date it broke
2. **Give a clear deadline** — 7 days for something essential, 14 for less urgent
3. **Follow up once** in writing if they miss it
4. **Apply to Tenancy Services** if they still don't act: [Tenancy Services disputes page](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/disputes/)

For how urgency rules apply to other repairs, see [How Long Does a Landlord Have to Fix Hot Water in NZ?](/briefs/how-long-does-landlord-have-to-fix-hot-water-nz/) or [Heating Not Fixed Before Winter NZ](/briefs/heating-not-fixed-before-winter-nz/).
