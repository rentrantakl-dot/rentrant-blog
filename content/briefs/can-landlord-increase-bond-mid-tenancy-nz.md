Only if your rent has gone up — and only to bridge the gap between 4 weeks at the old rent and 4 weeks at the new one. Your landlord cannot demand extra bond out of the blue.

---

## The Rule

Under [section 19 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html#DLM94304), bond is capped at 4 weeks' rent. That cap applies both when the tenancy starts and whenever a top-up is requested.

A bond top-up is only lawful when:

1. Rent has increased (through the proper 12-month, 60-days-notice process), and
2. The current bond is less than 4 weeks at the new rent level

If both are true, the landlord can ask for the difference. If rent hasn't increased, there's no legal basis for the request.

**Example:** You've been paying $600/week for 18 months. Your bond is $2,400 (4 × $600). Your landlord gives you a valid rent increase to $650/week.

The new 4-week amount is $2,600. They can ask for the **$200 difference** — nothing more.

---

## What They Can't Do

| Situation | Landlord can require top-up? |
|---|---|
| Rent has increased by $50/week | Yes — up to 4 weeks at new rent |
| Rent unchanged, landlord wants more security | No |
| Bond was below 4 weeks when tenancy started | No — can only top up after a rent increase |
| Rent increased, but bond already equals 4 weeks at new rent | No — it's already at the cap |

---

## Bond Still Goes to Tenancy Services

Any additional bond must be lodged with [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/bond/) within 23 working days — same as initial bond. A landlord who pockets it without lodging has breached the Act.

If you're unsure whether the top-up request is lawful, you can check directly with Tenancy Services on 0800 836 262 before paying.
