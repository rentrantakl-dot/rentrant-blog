Your landlord can add clauses to your tenancy agreement. What they cannot do is override the Residential Tenancies Act 1986. Any clause that attempts to remove your legal rights is void — you cannot sign them away, even voluntarily.

---

## Clauses You'll See That Aren't Enforceable

| Clause | Why it's unenforceable |
|---|---|
| "Tenant must have carpets professionally cleaned on exit" | Legal requirement is reasonably clean — not professionally cleaned — unless that was the move-in standard |
| "Rent may be increased with 14 days' notice" | Rent increases require 60 days' written notice and can only occur once per 12 months |
| "No visitors after 10pm" | Unreasonable restriction on quiet enjoyment |
| "Tenant responsible for all maintenance and repairs" | Landlord cannot contract out of the duty to maintain under s.45 of the RTA |
| Application fee / letting fee / key fee | Explicitly illegal under the Residential Tenancies Act |

---

## What Landlords Can Legitimately Add

Extra clauses are allowed when they're reasonable and don't override the Act. Common legitimate additions: no smoking on premises, pet conditions, garden maintenance responsibilities, parking rules, and heat pump filter cleaning (usually every 6 months — standard practice).

---

## The Healthy Homes Compliance Statement

Your landlord must provide a signed Healthy Homes compliance statement before the tenancy starts — for all agreements commencing after 1 July 2025. It confirms the heating, insulation, and ventilation all meet the legal standard.

If it's not there when you go to sign, ask for it before you put pen to paper. A landlord who can't produce one is either non-compliant or unprepared. Either way, that's useful to know before you're locked in.

---

## Before You Sign: One Practical Thing

Walk through the property and note every pre-existing mark, stain, or damage in the signed tenancy agreement itself — not on a separate sheet. Both you and the landlord sign off on it. What's documented is protected.
