A landlord in New Zealand cannot lawfully decline your rental application because of your age. Under [section 53 of the Human Rights Act 1993](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0082/latest/DLM304632.html), it is unlawful to refuse accommodation — or to offer it on less favourable terms — on the basis of age. The protection runs from age 16 upward with no upper limit.

---

## What Landlords Can and Cannot Ask

The question that comes up most often: can a landlord ask for your date of birth?

Under the Privacy Act 2020, landlords can only collect information that is reasonably necessary to assess the application. Asking whether you are **over 18** is legitimate — it's relevant to whether you can legally enter a tenancy agreement. Asking for your full date of birth, or using age as a screening criterion, goes beyond what the law permits.

| They can ask | They cannot do |
|---|---|
| Are you over 18? | Require your full date of birth |
| Proof of income or employment | Decline based on your age |
| Rental history and references | Offer shorter lease terms because you seem young |
| Number of occupants | Advertise "over 25s only" or "no under 30s" |

---

## "No Students" Advertising

This one's murky. Advertising "no students" isn't cleanly prohibited as age discrimination — a landlord could argue they're screening for financial stability, not age. But it potentially discriminates on **employment status** (students may be on StudyLink or a student allowance), which is a protected ground under the HRA. It may also indirectly discriminate on age.

In practice, proving discrimination in rental selection is hard. Without something in writing — an ad with explicit wording, an email, a text message — it usually comes down to your word against theirs.

---

## The Exemption Worth Knowing

There's one situation where the accommodation discrimination rules don't apply: **shared accommodation where the owner lives on-site.**

Under [section 54 of the Human Rights Act 1993](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0082/latest/DLM304633.html), the discrimination prohibition does not apply when the person who owns or manages the property lives there too. If you're applying to rent a room in someone's home and they share the space with you, they can choose who they live with on any grounds they like.

Student halls and retirement villages are also exempt under section 55 — they can legally restrict intake to particular age groups.

## If You Think You've Been Discriminated Against

Two paths exist, but you can only use one:

1. **[Te Kāhui Tika Tangata (Human Rights Commission)](https://tikatangata.org.nz/)** — 0800 496 877. A free complaint process that starts with mediation. If unresolved, it can escalate to the Human Rights Review Tribunal, which can award substantial compensation.
2. **Tenancy Tribunal** — faster, but lower maximum awards for accommodation discrimination specifically.

Either way, get advice first from [Community Law NZ](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) or [Citizens Advice Bureau](https://www.cab.org.nz/). They can help you assess the strength of your evidence before you commit to a formal complaint.
