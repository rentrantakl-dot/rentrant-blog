Fair wear and tear is the gradual deterioration a property goes through from ordinary, everyday use. Your landlord **cannot charge you for it** — not from your bond, and not as a separate claim.

---

## What Counts as Fair Wear and Tear

These are things the Tenancy Tribunal consistently treats as normal and expected:

| Item | Fair wear and tear |
|---|---|
| Carpet | Flattened pile in hallways, minor fading, light general soiling — see [carpet bond disputes](/briefs/can-landlord-keep-bond-for-carpet-cleaning-nz/) |
| Walls | Small scuffs and marks from furniture, minor nail holes from pictures |
| Paint | Fading, minor discolouration from normal living |
| Doors and handles | Worn finish, slight loosening from regular use |
| Curtains | Fading from sunlight |
| Flooring | Light scratches from furniture and foot traffic |

The key question the Tribunal asks is: would a reasonable person expect this from ordinary use over this period of time? If yes, it's wear and tear. If the deterioration is worse than that, it's potentially damage.

---

## What Is NOT Fair Wear and Tear

These sit clearly in the "damage" category:

- Stains on carpet or upholstery from spills
- Burns on surfaces or carpet
- Holes or gouges in walls
- Broken fixtures (towel rails, door handles, hinges) from rough use
- Pet damage — scratches, stains, or odours
- Mould where the landlord can demonstrate their ventilation obligations under the Healthy Homes Standards were met and the cause was clearly the tenant's behaviour — though this is contested territory, and landlords who haven't installed compliant extractor fans or openable windows will struggle to pin mould on a tenant
- A property left in an unreasonably dirty state

The distinction matters because a landlord *can* legitimately deduct from your bond for these, provided they can prove the damage occurred during your tenancy.

---

## Tenancy Length Changes the Calculation

A property lived in for two years will show more wear than one occupied for three months. The Tribunal factors this in. A landlord who rents a property for five years and then claims the carpet needs replacing has a weak case — that carpet has had a full working life.

Age matters too. The Tribunal applies a depreciation approach — if a carpet had a 10-year expected lifespan and was already 8 years old when you moved in, it has roughly 20% of its value remaining. Even if you caused damage, you can only be held liable for that remaining portion, not the full replacement cost. A landlord cannot use a tenant's departure as an opportunity to replace aging items at full cost.

---

## How to Protect Yourself

The move-in inspection report is your single most important protection. Do it thoroughly, take date-stamped photos of every room, note every existing mark or stain, and make sure it's signed by both parties.

If the landlord has no evidence of the property's condition at the start of tenancy, their bond deduction claim falls apart at the Tribunal.

> **Tip:** Don't rely on the landlord's inspection report alone. Do your own walkthrough with photos before you unpack.
