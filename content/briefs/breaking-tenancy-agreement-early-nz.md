It depends on your tenancy type. **Periodic tenancy: give 21 days notice and you're done.** Fixed-term tenancy: you cannot end it early without the landlord's agreement — but you have more options than most people realise.

---

## Periodic Tenancy — Straightforward

A periodic tenancy has no fixed end date. To leave, you give your landlord **21 days written notice**. That's it. No fees, no penalties, no need for the landlord to agree.

The 21 days starts from the day the landlord receives your notice, not the day you write it. Email with a read receipt is a clean way to handle this.

---

## Fixed-Term Tenancy — More Complicated

A fixed-term tenancy runs until its end date. You cannot end it early simply by giving notice. You have three real options:

**1. Negotiate with your landlord**
Ask them to agree to an early end. Get any agreement in writing — signed by both parties. Verbal agreements are unenforceable at the Tribunal. Some landlords will agree; some will want a break fee or compensation for re-letting costs.

**2. Find a replacement tenant**
If you can find someone acceptable to the landlord, they can take over your tenancy. The landlord cannot unreasonably refuse a replacement. If they do, that shifts liability back to them.

**3. Invoke special circumstances**
The law provides specific exits regardless of tenancy type:

- **Domestic violence:** You can end the tenancy immediately with a [protection order or statutory declaration](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/ending-a-tenancy/family-violence-and-tenancies/)
- **Uninhabitable property:** If the property becomes uninhabitable (through no fault of yours), the tenancy can be terminated
- **Landlord in serious breach:** If the landlord has materially breached their obligations, you may have grounds to apply to the Tribunal to end the tenancy early

---

## If You Just Leave

Walking out of a fixed-term without an agreement is legally treated as abandonment. The landlord can apply to the Tenancy Tribunal for rent lost from your departure date until a new tenant moves in.

The key constraint on landlords: they have a **duty to mitigate**. They must actively try to re-let the property. If they list it at above-market rent, delay advertising, or turn down reasonable applicants, the Tribunal will reduce any award accordingly.

In practice, if a replacement tenant is found within a few weeks, your liability may be limited to that gap period plus reasonable re-letting costs.

---

## Before You Do Anything

Write to your landlord first. Most early exits are resolved by agreement. If yours is not, [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) has a free mediation service — use it before going to the Tribunal.
