No hot water is an urgent repair. Your landlord's legal obligation under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986 is to act **as soon as practicable** — not at their convenience, and not in weeks.

---

## What the Law Requires

Under the Residential Tenancies Act, your landlord must keep the property in a reasonable state of repair throughout your tenancy. Hot water is not optional — it's essential for basic health and hygiene.

The Healthy Homes Standards go further: landlords must provide hot water with at least **120 litres capacity**, heated to a minimum of **60°C** to prevent Legionella growth. Bath and shower outlets must have a tempering valve to limit delivery temperature. A broken hot water cylinder isn't just an inconvenience — it's a compliance failure.

There's no fixed number of days written into law. But "urgent" repairs are those that make the property uninhabitable or unsafe, and the standard the Tribunal applies is *as soon as practicable* — meaning the landlord should act immediately, not schedule it for next week.

---

## What to Do If Your Hot Water Is Out

**Step 1: Notify your landlord in writing**

Text or email so you have a timestamp. Be specific: what's wrong, when it stopped working, and that you consider it urgent. Don't just call — a phone call leaves no record.

**Step 2: Give them a short, reasonable window**

24–48 hours to arrange a plumber is a reasonable expectation for an urgent repair. The Tribunal does not accept weeks. If your landlord is unresponsive or dismissive, move to step 3.

**Step 3: Apply to the Tenancy Tribunal**

Apply for an urgent hearing at tenancy.govt.nz. Urgent applications are prioritised — hearings can be scheduled within days. The Tribunal can order your landlord to carry out the repair immediately.

> **Key point:** Keep every message you send and receive. Screenshots, emails, texts. If this ends up at the Tribunal, your paper trail is your case.

---

## What You Can Claim

If your landlord failed to fix an urgent repair within a reasonable time, the Tribunal can award:

- A **rent reduction** for the period you had no hot water
- **Exemplary damages** if the landlord knew about the problem and failed to act, or acted in flagrant disregard of your rights — up to $7,200 for landlords with fewer than 6 properties, up to $50,000 for larger landlords. These aren't awarded for ordinary delays; the conduct needs to be clearly unreasonable
