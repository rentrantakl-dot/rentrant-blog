There are two very different noise problems in a rental. The first is noisy neighbours — the kind who have a party at 2am or whose TV leaks through a thin wall. The second is structural or persistent noise that makes your home hard to live in: a plant room that hums through your bedroom floor, a building next door under construction at 7:30am every morning, or a fellow tenant of your landlord who won't turn down the music no matter how many times they're asked.

Your options — and who's legally responsible — depend almost entirely on which category your problem falls into. Getting that wrong wastes time.

---

## Your Right to Quiet Enjoyment

The implied covenant of quiet enjoyment means your landlord must not interfere with your use and enjoyment of the property, and must take reasonable steps to prevent others they control from doing so. [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/maintenance-and-inspections/damage-and-loss/quiet-enjoyment/) describes this as one of the fundamental protections tenants have in New Zealand.

What this means in practice: your landlord cannot enter without proper notice, cannot interfere with your access to the property, and cannot permit other tenants under their management to make your living situation unreasonable. The protection runs in your favour — not the landlord's.

What it doesn't mean: quiet enjoyment is not an absolute right to a silent environment. Normal neighbourhood sounds, the city, and background noise from shared living are part of renting. The standard is reasonableness — what a reasonable person in your position would find acceptable.

## When the Noise Comes From Inside Your Building

This is where your landlord's responsibility is clearest.

If you're in a multi-unit building and the noise is coming from another tenant of the same landlord — in another flat in the same complex, a unit above or below you, or an adjacent property owned by the same person — your landlord can be required to take action. Under the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM94278.html), tenants must not cause unreasonable disturbance to neighbours, and a landlord who is aware of a noisy tenant and takes no steps to address it is potentially in breach of their obligations to the affected tenant.

**What you should do:**

1. Document the noise — write down dates, times, how long it lasted, and what it was. If it's persistent, a brief log kept over two to three weeks is stronger than a single complaint.

2. Put a written complaint to your property manager or landlord. Email is best — it creates a dated record. Be specific: which unit the noise comes from if you know, what times it happens, and the effect it has on you (can't sleep, can't work from home).

3. Give them a reasonable opportunity to act. For a noise complaint between tenants, a week or two is reasonable before escalating.

4. If nothing changes, contact [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) for information and assistance. They can help you understand your options before you apply to the Tribunal.

> **Key point:** A landlord who receives a written noise complaint and does nothing about it is in a much weaker position at the Tribunal than one who tried to address it. Don't just complain verbally — put it in writing.

## When the Noise Is External

External noise — from neighbours who are nothing to do with your landlord, from construction nearby, from a bar or venue down the road — is a different situation. Your landlord is generally not responsible for noise that originates outside the property and outside their control.

This doesn't mean you have no recourse. It means the remedy is different.

**Construction noise** is subject to rules about permitted hours. Auckland Council sets permitted hours for construction and demolition work in residential areas — generally weekdays and Saturdays, not early mornings or Sundays. If construction noise is happening outside permitted hours, Auckland Council's noise control team can investigate. Check [Auckland Council's noise page](https://www.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/environment/finding-reporting-problems-environment/Pages/noise.aspx) for current guidance and contact details.

**Neighbour noise** (a completely separate household, not connected to your landlord) is handled through Auckland Council's noise control service. Noise control officers can attend, issue directions to stop the noise, and follow up with fines if directions aren't complied with.

**Venue and event noise** is similar — outside your landlord's control. If you're moving to an area near a bar, a stadium, or an entertainment strip, this is worth checking before you sign rather than after.

One exception worth knowing: if your property has been marketed as quiet or private and you discover structural features that make it significantly noisier than expected (a poorly ventilated plant room, inadequate soundproofing between floors), there may be an argument about whether the property was fit for purpose. This is a narrow argument and would need specific facts — contact [Community Law NZ](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) for advice on your specific situation.

## Auckland Council's Noise Control Team

Auckland Council operates a noise control service for unreasonable residential noise. It's available 24 hours via the council contact channels listed on [aucklandcouncil.govt.nz](https://www.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/).

When a noise control officer attends and the noise is unreasonable, they can issue a direction to the person responsible. If the noise continues after that direction, enforcement action — including fines — can follow.

Noise control is most effective when:
- The noise is happening right now and the officer can attend quickly
- There's an ongoing pattern (they can note repeat complaints against the same address)
- You're making a formal complaint, not just a one-off call

It's less effective for noise that comes and goes unpredictably, or noise that's technically within permitted times but is still genuinely intrusive. For that kind of problem, a broader approach — talking to your property manager, using Tenancy Services mediation, or in serious cases applying to the Tribunal — is more likely to get a result.

## Documenting the Problem

Whether you're dealing with internal building noise or external noise, documentation matters more than most renters realise — especially if you end up at the Tenancy Tribunal.

A basic noise log looks like this:

| Date | Time | Duration | Description | Action taken |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 14 June | 11:30pm–2:00am | ~2.5 hrs | Loud music from Unit 3, bass audible through wall | Knocked on door at midnight — noise reduced briefly |
| 17 June | 12:15am–1:30am | ~75 min | Same — music and shouting | Emailed property manager at 9am on 18 June |
| 21 June | 11:00pm–3:00am | ~4 hrs | Same pattern, louder | Called noise control — officer attended at 1am, issued direction |

A log like that — specific, dated, noting what steps you took — is strong evidence at a Tribunal hearing. "It was noisy a lot" is not.

Keep copies of all written communications with your property manager about noise. If you called rather than emailed, follow up the call with an email: "Following up on our call this morning about the noise from Unit 3..."

## When to Take It to the Tenancy Tribunal

The Tenancy Tribunal can help when:

- The noisy tenant is also renting from your landlord, you've put the complaint in writing, given a reasonable time to act, and nothing has changed
- The noise problem amounts to a breach of your right to quiet enjoyment serious enough to make the property difficult to live in
- You want a rent reduction for a period when the noise has made the property less useful to you than what you're paying for

What the Tribunal can order:
- The landlord to take action against the noisy tenant (issue a formal notice, or apply for termination if the behaviour is serious)
- A rent reduction for the period affected
- Exemplary damages if the landlord's failure to act was particularly serious — up to $7,200 for landlords with fewer than 6 rental properties, up to $50,000 for larger landlords

Applying to the Tribunal costs $20.44 (as of 2026). [The full application process is explained here](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/).

Before applying, [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) offers free mediation — a faster and less formal process that resolves many disputes without going to a full hearing. It's worth trying if the landlord has shown some willingness to engage.

## Noise You're Making Yourself

Worth mentioning: the obligations run both ways. Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, tenants must not cause unreasonable disturbance to neighbours. A landlord who receives complaints about a noisy tenant can issue a notice to remedy, and in serious or repeated cases, can apply to the Tribunal to terminate the tenancy.

This isn't said to be preachy — it's worth knowing because the rules apply to you equally. If you have flatmates who are the source of the noise and you're on the tenancy agreement, you're responsible. An agreement about noise standards with flatmates before moving in, while it sounds overly formal, prevents a lot of awkward conversations at midnight.
