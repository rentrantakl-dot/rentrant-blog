Insulation isn't exciting. It's hidden inside walls and ceilings, you never really think about it — until you're lying in bed in July wearing two jerseys wondering why your flat is still freezing despite the heat pump being on full blast.

Poor insulation is one of the biggest quality-of-life problems in New Zealand rentals. And since **1 July 2025**, every rental in New Zealand must meet the Healthy Homes insulation standard. Not eventually. Now.

Here's what your landlord is legally required to provide, how to check whether your flat meets the standard, and what to do if it doesn't.

---

## What the Healthy Homes insulation standard requires

New Zealand is divided into three climate zones for the purposes of the Healthy Homes Standards. Auckland sits in **Climate Zone 1** — the warmest zone, covering Northland and Auckland.

The standard has two components: ceiling insulation and underfloor insulation.

### Ceiling insulation

| Situation | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| New installation (no existing insulation) | Minimum R2.9 in Climate Zone 1 and 2 |
| Existing insulation already in place | Must be in reasonable condition (no minimum R-value if pre-existing) |
| Inaccessible ceiling (e.g. flat roof) | Exempt if installation is not reasonably practicable |

**What is R-value?**

R-value measures a material's resistance to heat flow. The higher the R-value, the better the insulation. For comparison, a 100mm layer of polyester batts typically achieves around R2.8–R3.2 depending on the product.

The "reasonable condition" threshold for existing insulation means it must not be:
- Damaged, wet, or compressed significantly
- Missing sections (gaps are as bad as no insulation)
- Contaminated or harbouring pests

If your landlord claims the ceiling is insulated because there's some aging fibreglass from 1985 that's partly fallen down — that's probably not "reasonable condition."

### Underfloor insulation

| Situation | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Suspended timber floor | Minimum R1.3 in Climate Zone 1 and 2 |
| Concrete slab | Exempt — no underfloor insulation required |
| Inaccessible subfloor | Exempt if installation is not reasonably practicable |

The underfloor standard applies where there's a subfloor cavity — traditional suspended timber floors built on piles. If your flat is on a concrete slab, the underfloor exemption applies.

> **Key point:** The exemption for inaccessible subfloors applies when installation genuinely can't be done — not when it would be inconvenient or expensive. The landlord must provide documentation if they're claiming an exemption.

## Why insulation matters for renters

This isn't just about comfort. Poor insulation has a direct effect on:

**Heating costs.** An uninsulated ceiling can account for up to 35% of a home's heat loss. Paying for heating a poorly insulated flat is essentially paying to heat the sky above your roof.

**Mould risk.** Cold surfaces cause condensation. Poor insulation keeps surfaces cold. Cold surfaces grow mould. The insulation standard directly addresses one of the main causes of mould in NZ rentals.

**Health.** The World Health Organisation recommends indoor temperatures above 18°C for health. A poorly insulated home is harder to heat to that threshold — which has real consequences for people with respiratory conditions, elderly tenants, and children.

**Your power bill.** It's no coincidence that New Zealand has some of the highest residential power prices in the OECD and a large share of poorly insulated housing stock. You're paying for it twice: in rent and in your electricity bill.

## How to check if your rental is insulated

### Step 1: Ask for the compliance statement

Under the Healthy Homes Standards, your landlord must provide a written Healthy Homes compliance statement within **21 days of your tenancy starting**. This must include the insulation statement — specifying what insulation is present, where, and what R-values it meets.

Ask for this in writing if you haven't received it. Failure to provide it is itself a breach of the RTA.

### Step 2: Check the ceiling yourself (carefully)

Most NZ houses have a ceiling access hatch — usually in a hallway or wardrobe. You can look (not walk) into the ceiling cavity and see what's there.

What you're looking for:
- **Batts** — rectangular blocks of material (polyester, glasswool, or rockwool) laid between ceiling joists. Check that they cover the whole ceiling, not just part of it, and that they're not compressed or damaged.
- **Blown-in loose fill** — a layer of loose material spread across the ceiling floor. Should be at least 100mm deep for R2.9.
- **Nothing.** Which means no ceiling insulation.

> **Tip:** If you can feel cold air coming through the ceiling in winter, or if the ceiling surface is cold to touch, that's a practical sign of poor insulation regardless of what the statement says.

### Step 3: Check underfloor (if applicable)

For properties with a subfloor cavity (suspended timber floors), you can sometimes see whether underfloor insulation is present through the subfloor vents — the small vents around the base of the house. A torch and a peek through the vent will show whether there are batts stapled between the floor joists or not.

If the property is on a concrete slab (no visible gap between house and ground, no underfloor vents), the exemption applies.

### Step 4: Cross-check the compliance statement

Compare what you observe with what the compliance statement says. If the statement claims R3.2 ceiling insulation but the ceiling cavity looks like a thin scattering of material from 1992, you have a discrepancy worth raising.

## Common landlord responses — and what they actually mean

**"The property has insulation."**

Not good enough. Ask for the type, the R-value, and the coverage. "Has insulation" doesn't mean it meets the standard.

**"It's exempt — the ceiling is inaccessible."**

Ask to see documentation of the exemption. A genuinely inaccessible ceiling (e.g. a flat-roofed house where the ceiling butts directly against the roof) is a valid exemption. But this is often claimed for ceilings that are merely inconvenient to access.

**"The previous landlord installed it."**

Fine — but the current landlord is still responsible for meeting the standard. A pre-existing install in poor condition doesn't get a pass.

**"We'll install it at the next convenient time."**

The standard has been fully in effect since 1 July 2025. There's no "convenient time" grace period remaining. If the property isn't compliant, the landlord is in breach now.

## What to do if your rental isn't meeting the standard

### Put it in writing

Document the issue and contact your landlord in writing.

> **Template:**
>
> Hi [Landlord/Property Manager],
>
> I'm writing regarding the insulation at [address]. I've reviewed the Healthy Homes insulation standard and I don't believe the property currently complies — specifically: [describe the issue, e.g. "the ceiling insulation is damaged and incomplete" or "the compliance statement hasn't been provided"].
>
> The Healthy Homes insulation standard has been fully in effect since 1 July 2025. Could you please advise how and when you intend to bring the property into compliance?
>
> I've attached [photos / notes from my inspection].
>
> Cheers,
> [Your name]

Keep a copy of everything.

### If they don't act

If your landlord doesn't respond or refuses to fix the issue:

1. **Tenancy Services mediation** — free, and often effective. Call 0800 836 262.
2. **Tenancy Tribunal application** — you can apply for a compliance order, rent reduction for the non-compliant period, and exemplary damages.

### What the Tribunal can award

For insulation non-compliance, the Tribunal can order:
- A compliance order requiring installation by a specified date
- A **rent reduction** for the period the property wasn't compliant — typically 15-25% of weekly rent
- **Exemplary damages** of up to $7,200 (landlords with fewer than 6 properties) or up to $50,000 (larger landlords with 6 or more properties)

## A note on older Auckland housing stock

Auckland's rental market is dominated by older villas and bungalows — typically built between 1910 and 1960, with a second wave of state housing from the 1940s–60s. This housing stock was built with no insulation requirements and minimal consideration for thermal performance.

Many of these properties have had insulation retrofitted over the years, but quality varies enormously. Some have been done properly. Others have partial installs, damaged batts, or decade-old "improvements" that no longer meet the standard.

If you're renting a character villa or pre-1970s house in suburbs like Grey Lynn, Mt Albert, Onehunga, or Henderson, insulation compliance is worth checking specifically — it's more likely to be marginal or missing than in newer builds.

The EECA's [Warmer Kiwi Homes programme](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/our-work/programmes-and-funding/warmer-kiwi-homes/) subsidises insulation upgrades for eligible owner-occupiers — but landlords don't qualify unless the property has been home to Community Services Card holders. If you have a CSC, it's worth raising this with your landlord as a potential pathway to getting the property insulated at reduced cost to them.
