Rental applications feel like a black box. You fill out the form, you wait, and then someone you've never met decides whether you get the keys.

It's not actually that opaque. Landlords are checking a short list of things. And most of the difference between a strong application and a weak one comes down to preparation, not luck.

---

## What Landlords Are Actually Assessing

Three questions, in order:

1. **Can this person afford the rent?**
2. **Will they pay reliably?**
3. **Will they look after the property?**

Everything else — the cover letter, the references, how the application is presented — is evidence for those three questions.

The income benchmark most NZ landlords use: rent should not exceed roughly 30% of gross income. For a $550/week flat, that's approximately $1,650/week gross. It's not a hard rule — stable employment and strong references can compensate for a tighter ratio — but it's where the income check starts.

---

## Build Your Application Packet Before You Start Viewing

The single biggest advantage is speed. In a competitive situation, a complete application submitted within an hour of a viewing beats a better application submitted tomorrow.

**What goes in the packet:**

- **Government-issued photo ID** — NZ driver licence, passport, or other acceptable ID
- **Proof of income** — most recent 2 payslips, or an employment letter on letterhead with salary, or student loan / scholarship confirmation, or last 3 months' bank statements if self-employed or irregular income
- **Rental reference contact details** — name, phone, email, and relationship (previous landlord, property manager). Get their permission before listing them.
- **Employer reference contact details** — especially useful if you don't have a NZ rental history
- **Household details** — names, ages, and relationship of everyone who will live there

> **Practical tip:** Save this as a PDF and keep it somewhere accessible — Google Drive, emailed to yourself, phone storage. When you find a place you want, you copy the packet, add the cover letter, and send within an hour. No scrambling for payslips at 9pm.

## References: The Part Most Applicants Get Wrong

References do more work in NZ rental applications than most first-timers realise. A previous landlord who picks up the phone and says "yes, they were excellent tenants, I'd have them back immediately" is more valuable than any cover letter.

**Call your reference before you apply.**

Tell them you're applying for a rental, give them the property manager's name and rough address, and let them know someone may call in the next day or two. A reference who answers immediately and gives a specific positive account ("paid on time, reported maintenance promptly, left the place spotless") beats one who calls back four days later and vaguely confirms you lived there.

**If you don't have a NZ rental reference:**
- An overseas rental reference is better than nothing — property managers can email even if they can't call
- An employer reference who can speak to reliability and stability is a reasonable substitute
- For detailed tactics on the no-NZ-history situation: [Renting in Auckland without NZ references](/posts/renting-without-nz-references-auckland/)

**Don't pad references with friends.** Landlords ask targeted questions — "did they pay on time?", "would you rent to them again?", "how did they handle maintenance issues?" A friend who vaguely confirms they know you doesn't answer any of those.

## The Cover Letter: What It's Actually For

Cover letters are useful for competitive properties and private landlords. Large property management companies run income and reference checks first, and the cover letter is secondary. For a private landlord choosing between two comparable applications, a good one can make the difference.

What it's for: demonstrating briefly that you're a stable, thoughtful person who actually read the listing. What it's not for: convincing someone who's already decided against you because your income doesn't stack up.

**Template:**

> Hi [Property Manager's name if known],
>
> I'm [name], [job title / situation], based in [area] and looking to move to [suburb]. I'm looking for a long-term home and [this address] caught my attention because [one specific thing — the layout, the garden, proximity to work or study].
>
> [1–2 sentences on income stability: length of current employment, stable funding source, nature of income.] Our household income sits comfortably within the standard benchmark for the listed rent.
>
> Our previous property manager was [name] at [company/number] and is happy to take a call. Happy to provide any additional information.
>
> [Name, phone]

Keep it to 6–10 sentences. An honest, specific letter beats a long one that reads like it was generated from a template.

## The Tribunal Database: What Landlords Check About You

The [Tenancy Tribunal orders database](https://forms.justice.govt.nz/search/TT/) is publicly searchable. Landlords can — and often do — search applicant names.

If you have a past Tribunal case, don't assume it won't surface. It's better to mention it proactively in your cover letter with brief, factual context ("I had a dispute with a previous landlord in 2023 that went to Tribunal — happy to discuss") than to have it appear after an otherwise strong application with no explanation.

If your name returns nothing, that's neutral. Not a positive signal, but not a negative one.

And while you're there — you can also search the property manager or landlord's name. Patterns of Tribunal orders against a landlord are useful context before you commit.

## What Not to Do

**Don't offer more rent than listed.**
This isn't a US-style auction market. Offering $50/week above the listed rent raises questions rather than improving your position — it can signal inexperience or desperation, and neither is what a landlord is looking for in a long-term tenant.

**Don't omit pets and plan to bring them in.**
If you have a pet, say so upfront. "One desexed female cat, 4 years old, no history of damage" is a better position than a blank field. Being discovered with an undisclosed pet is a lease breach, a bond issue, and a trust-ending moment.

**Don't pad references with friends.**
Already covered above, but worth repeating — a reference who can't speak to your tenancy is weaker than it looks. Landlords ask targeted questions. Friends give general character vouches. They don't land the same way.

**Don't apply for places you can't afford.**
The income check is one of the first things assessed. A strong cover letter doesn't overcome a rent-to-income ratio of 60%. Apply for places where the numbers work.

## What Landlords Cannot Do

NZ landlords cannot discriminate based on the 13 protected grounds under the [Human Rights Act 1993](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0082/latest/whole.html) — including race, colour, ethnic or national origin, family status (having children), and employment status.

They cannot require photos of applicants (Privacy Act concern). They cannot charge application fees or letting fees (illegal under the RTA). If something in the process feels discriminatory, it may be.

For full detail: [What can landlords legally ask on a NZ rental application?](/briefs/rental-application-discrimination-nz-brief/)

## Before You Apply: Check Them Too

You're about to let someone check your rental history. It's worth checking theirs.

Search the address on [RentRant](https://rentrant.nz/locations/search) — previous tenants' ratings on maintenance responsiveness, heating, and dampness may tell you things the listing won't. Search the landlord or property manager name in the Tribunal database. Ask anyone you know in the area.

The information asymmetry in rental applications cuts both ways. Use it.

---

Getting approved for a rental in NZ isn't about performing perfectly or saying the right things. It's about being prepared enough to make a clear, complete case quickly.

Most of the work happens before you walk into the viewing: documents ready, references briefed, cover letter drafted. When you find a place you actually want, you can move immediately.
