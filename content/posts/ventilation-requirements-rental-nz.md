It's June. The bathroom mirror fogs up every morning, the kitchen ceiling has a faint stain you noticed too late, and the window above the stove does nothing except let in cold air. That recirculating range hood hasn't been clearing the cooking steam — it's been filtering it and putting it back into your kitchen.

Under New Zealand's Healthy Homes ventilation standard — fully in force since **1 July 2025** — this is not a situation your landlord gets to leave in place. Here's what the law requires, what counts as compliant, and what to do if your flat falls short.

---

## The three requirements

The Healthy Homes ventilation standard has three separate components. All three must be met.

### Openable windows in every habitable room

Every bedroom and living area must have openable windows, doors, or skylights totalling at least **5% of the floor area** of that room. At least one opening must actually open — it's a functional requirement, not just presence.

A painted-shut window, a broken latch, a sash that's swollen shut — these fail the standard even if the window is nominally there. The standard requires openable; if it won't open, it doesn't count.

For most rooms with standard windows, 5% is easily met. The failure mode is usually broken hardware or windows that have been sealed by a previous renovation.

### Kitchen: extractor fan or range hood to outside

The kitchen must have an extractor fan or range hood that vents extracted air **to the outdoors**.

This is where a lot of rentals fail — and where a lot of landlords are confident they don't. Because many kitchens have a range hood. But range hoods come in two types, and only one meets the standard.

| Fan type | Compliant? |
|---|---|
| Range hood with duct to outside | **Yes** |
| Recirculating range hood (filters and returns air) | **No** |
| HRV or DVS whole-house ventilation system | **No** |
| Extractor fan ducted to outside | **Yes** |
| Extractor fan ducted to roof cavity | **No** |
| Nothing | No |

Tenancy Services states this explicitly: recirculating systems — including HRV and DVS systems and hoods that filter and return air — do not meet the standard. A range hood that sounds like it's working but blows air back into the kitchen through a carbon filter is not compliant, regardless of how expensive it looks.

For fans installed after **1 July 2019**, the standard also specifies minimum capacity: at least 150mm duct diameter, or at least 50 litres per second exhaust capacity.

### Bathroom: extractor fan to outside — not a window

Every bathroom must have an extractor fan vented **to the outdoors**.

A window is not a substitute. This is stated clearly in the standard and is one of the most common compliance misunderstandings — landlords who've had a bathroom window for decades assume it satisfies the requirement. It doesn't.

For fans installed after **1 July 2019**: at least 120mm duct diameter, or at least 25 litres per second exhaust capacity.

Fans installed before 1 July 2019 are grandfathered — but only if they still vent directly to outside and are in good working order. A pre-2019 fan that ducts into the roof cavity or no longer functions doesn't get grandfathered.

---

## The roof cavity problem

This catches a lot of older NZ homes out. Many bathroom fans installed in the 1980s and 1990s duct into the roof space rather than through to the outside. At the time, this was considered adequate. Under the Healthy Homes standard, it is not — the standard requires extraction to **outdoor air**, not to the building's interior roof cavity.

How to check: look at the fan housing in your bathroom, then look outside the house. Is there an extractor vent visible on the roof, or on a wall near the bathroom? If you can't identify one, the fan may be ducting into the roof cavity.

This matters not just for compliance but for what happens next: a fan venting into the roof cavity doesn't remove moisture from your bathroom — it just moves it into the roof, where it condenses on the framing and eventually becomes someone else's problem (often yours, in the form of ceiling staining and mould).

---

## Why ventilation matters: the mould connection

Poor ventilation is the primary cause of condensation and mould in NZ rental housing. The mechanism is direct: cooking steam with nowhere to go settles on cool surfaces. Shower moisture with nowhere to go saturates grout, accumulates on ceiling paint, seeps behind tiles.

A kitchen without a working outdoor extractor produces 2–3 litres of moisture per hour of cooking. A bathroom with no functioning fan produces a sustained moisture load every time anyone showers. In a NZ winter, with cold exterior walls, that moisture condenses fast.

If you have mould and the ventilation doesn't meet the standard, they're connected. Fixing the ventilation doesn't remove existing mould — but it addresses the source. Your landlord is responsible for both.

## How to check your rental's ventilation

### Step 1: Walk through every habitable room

Do all windows open properly? Try them. A window that opens 10cm due to a safety catch might count — a window that's painted shut doesn't. Note any that are broken, stuck, or sealed.

### Step 2: Check the kitchen fan or range hood

Is there a fan or range hood? Turn it on. Now look outside the house — is there an extractor vent somewhere on the wall or roof near the kitchen? If you can't find one, the hood is likely recirculating.

You can also check the hood itself. Recirculating hoods typically have a washable or replaceable carbon filter on the underside. Externally-vented hoods have a duct connection at the back or top, leading into the wall or ceiling.

### Step 3: Check the bathroom fan

Is there a fan? Does it work? Look outside for a vent. If the fan runs but you can't find where it exits the building, it may be ducting into the roof cavity — a common older-build problem.

### Step 4: Request the compliance statement

Your landlord must provide a Healthy Homes compliance statement within **21 days of your tenancy starting**. This should detail the ventilation provisions — what's installed, where, and whether it meets the standard. If you haven't received one, request it in writing.

## What to do if your ventilation doesn't comply

### Put it in writing first

Contact your landlord or property manager by email.

> **Template:**
>
> Hi [Landlord/Property Manager],
>
> I'm writing about the ventilation at [address]. I've reviewed the Healthy Homes ventilation standard and I have concerns about compliance — specifically: [describe the issue, e.g. "the kitchen range hood appears to recirculate air rather than vent outside" / "the bathroom has no extractor fan" / "the bathroom extractor fan appears to duct into the roof cavity rather than outside"].
>
> The Healthy Homes ventilation standard has been fully in effect since 1 July 2025. Could you let me know how and when you intend to address this?
>
> Cheers,
> [Your name]

### If they don't act

1. **Tenancy Services mediation** — free, call 0800 836 262
2. **Tenancy Tribunal** — apply at justice.govt.nz ($28 to file)

### What the Tribunal can award

- A compliance order requiring installation by a specified date
- A **rent reduction** for the period of non-compliance
- **Exemplary damages** of up to $7,200 (landlords with fewer than 6 properties) or up to $50,000 (landlords with 6 or more properties)

## Common landlord responses — and why they don't hold

**"The HRV/DVS system handles the ventilation."**

Whole-house ventilation systems like HRV and DVS are explicitly named by Tenancy Services as non-compliant with the ventilation standard. They do not substitute for extractor fans in kitchens or bathrooms.

**"The range hood works fine."**

A recirculating range hood that filters and returns air does not meet the standard. The requirement is extraction to outside — not filtration.

**"The bathroom has a window."**

The standard requires a bathroom extractor fan. A window, regardless of how often it's opened, does not substitute.

**"The fan vents into the roof space — that's the same thing."**

It isn't. The standard requires ventilation to outdoor air. A duct terminating in the roof cavity doesn't meet this requirement.

**"It's always been like this."**

Irrelevant. The compliance deadline was 1 July 2025. All rental properties must meet the standard now.

---

## The bigger picture

Ventilation is the least expensive of the five Healthy Homes standards to fix. An extractor fan is a couple of hundred dollars and a few hours of a tradie's time. A recirculating range hood can be swapped for an externally-vented model for a few hundred more.

The fact that non-compliance is still common isn't because these fixes are hard. It's because most tenants don't push back. The compliance statement your landlord gave you says what's installed — if it doesn't meet the standard, you have the mechanism to require it to.
