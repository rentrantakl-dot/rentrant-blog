You applied for the flat. Decent income, clean rental history, good references. Declined — no reason given. It happens all the time, and most of the time you'll never know why. But sometimes you will. And sometimes the reason isn't legal.

In New Zealand, rental discrimination is unlawful under the [Human Rights Act 1993](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0082/latest/whole.html). A 2025 peer-reviewed study in the *International Journal of Housing Policy* surveyed renters and found nearly half had either experienced or suspected discrimination when trying to rent. This guide sets out three things separately: what the law prohibits, what the evidence shows about how the law plays out in practice, and what you can do if you think you've been discriminated against.

---

## What the law prohibits

Under [section 53 of the **Human Rights Act 1993**](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0082/latest/DLM304476.html) (accommodation), it is unlawful for a landlord or property manager to discriminate in providing rental accommodation based on any of 13 protected grounds:

| Protected ground | Notes |
|---|---|
| Sex | Includes pregnancy and childbirth |
| Marital status | Single, married, civil union, de facto, separated |
| Religious belief | |
| Ethical belief | Lack of religious belief |
| Colour | |
| Race | |
| Ethnic or national origins | Includes nationality and citizenship |
| Disability | Physical, psychiatric, or intellectual impairment; physical illness |
| Age | Must be 16 or older; some exceptions apply |
| Political opinion | Includes lack of any political opinion |
| Employment status | Unemployed, on a benefit, or receiving an ACC entitlement |
| Family status | Having children or dependants; being a relative of a particular person |
| Sexual orientation | Heterosexual, homosexual, lesbian, or bisexual |

**Note on gender identity:** Gender identity is not listed as a standalone ground in the Human Rights Act 1993. Transgender and non-binary people seeking protection have had to argue under "sex" by analogy, based on case law rather than the Act's text. This is a known gap in the legislation.

The law is broad. A landlord who refuses a tenancy because the applicant is Māori, has children, is on a benefit, or is from a particular country is acting unlawfully. Full stop.

**What landlords can do:** Decline applicants for reasons not on this list — poor rental history, inability to afford the rent, insufficient references. These are legitimate selection criteria. The unlawful part is when the real reason is one of the 13 grounds above.

> **Key point:** Landlords can choose tenants. They cannot choose based on the 13 protected grounds. The practical question in any single case is whether a stated reason — references, rental history, affordability — is the real reason or a proxy for one of the protected grounds.

**Exceptions** where some differential treatment is permitted:
- Shared accommodation where the landlord also lives on the premises and shares facilities (e.g. a room in the landlord's home)
- Genuine single-sex accommodation facilities
- Some safety-based disability exceptions

## What the evidence shows

A 2025 peer-reviewed study in the *International Journal of Housing Policy* — "Plausible deniability? Discretion and systemic discrimination in tenant selection in Aotearoa New Zealand's private rental sector" — surveyed renters and interviewed property managers about how tenant selection actually works.

Two findings from the study:

- **32% of renters surveyed reported experiencing discrimination when trying to rent.** A further 16% were unsure — nearly half of respondents had either experienced or suspected it.
- The selection process relies heavily on discretionary judgement. Property managers shortlist from large pools (often 20–50 applications), use reference checks as filters, and describe selection in terms like "gut feel" or applicants who "feel right".

The study's argument — captured in its title — is that current human rights law is well-suited to identifying *individual* acts of discrimination but less effective at addressing patterns produced by aggregated discretionary decisions, where any single decision can be justified on neutral grounds. The authors call this "plausible deniability" and argue it leaves a gap in legal protection. Whether you agree with the diagnosis or not, two things from the study are useful for any individual renter to know: discrimination is reported by a substantial minority of renters, and proving any single instance is harder than the law's clarity might suggest.

That has practical consequences for what you do next. Documentation matters. Patterns matter. A single declined application without explanation is hard to act on; a documented sequence of declines after photo requests, or a written communication that reveals the reason, is materially stronger.

## Occupancy limits: how they function as indirect discrimination

A 2025 Public Health Communication Centre briefing on occupancy limits in NZ rentals found a consistent pattern: landlords routinely set occupancy limits at one more person than the number of bedrooms — a three-bedroom property capped at four people, for example.

This appears neutral. But in practice, it disproportionately excludes:
- Multi-generational South and East Asian households where extended family living is normal
- Māori and Pacific families with children
- Larger families where sharing rent is a financial necessity

Using an occupancy limit to screen out households based on cultural living arrangements can constitute **indirect discrimination** under the Human Rights Act — where the criterion appears neutral but the effect is to disadvantage a protected group.

A landlord who advertises "maximum 3 people" in a three-bedroom property and declines all applications from larger families may not have said anything explicitly discriminatory. But if the effect is to systematically exclude Pacific or South Asian household configurations, the Human Rights Act still applies.

## What landlords cannot ask

Discrimination doesn't only happen at the selection stage. It can happen in the application process itself.

| Request | Status |
|---|---|
| Photo of you | Privacy Act 2020 concern — do not provide |
| Application fee | **Illegal** — Residential Tenancies Act 1986 |
| Credit check fee | **Illegal** — Residential Tenancies Act 1986 |
| Bank statements per person as standard | Grey area — can be Privacy Act concern |
| Employer guarantor as standard requirement for international applicants | Grey area — should not be applied discriminatorily |

Under the **Privacy Act 2020**, landlords can collect information reasonably necessary to assess whether you can pay rent and will maintain the property. A photo of the applicant is not reasonably necessary for that assessment. It should not be requested, and you are not obliged to provide it.

Application fees — any charge for applying or for a credit check — are unlawful under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986. If a landlord or property manager asks for money to process your application, this is a legal breach, not just bad practice.

## What you can do

### Option 1: Document and move on

The pragmatic reality for many renters is that proving discrimination is difficult, the complaints process takes months, and the rental market won't pause while you file. In many situations, the best immediate step is to document what happened — save all written communication, note names and dates — and move on to the next application.

That documentation isn't wasted. If you want to file a complaint later, or if you see the same landlord or property manager discriminate again, you have a record.

### Option 2: File a Privacy Act complaint

If the issue was a photo request or excessive data collection — and you have evidence of this — a Privacy Act complaint is narrower and may resolve faster than a Human Rights complaint.

File with the **Privacy Commissioner** at [privacy.org.nz](https://www.privacy.org.nz/).

### Option 3: File a Human Rights complaint

File with **Te Kāhui Tika Tangata** (Human Rights Commission):
- Website: [tikatangata.org.nz](https://tikatangata.org.nz/)
- Phone: 0800 496 877
- Email: infoline@hrc.co.nz

The process:
1. Lodge a complaint (online, phone, or email)
2. The Commission reviews whether it falls within their jurisdiction
3. A mediator contacts both parties and offers free, confidential mediation
4. If unresolved, the case can proceed to the **Human Rights Review Tribunal**
5. The **Office of Human Rights Proceedings** can provide free legal representation at Tribunal stage — you don't pay for a lawyer

Timeline: months. This is not a fast remedy. Set your expectations accordingly.

> **Warning:** Discrimination complaints are most viable when you have documentary evidence — a written communication revealing the reason, a pattern of rejections documented over time, a statement from someone present. A declined application with no stated reason is difficult to pursue without additional evidence. Get advice from Community Law NZ before filing to assess what you have.

### Getting help before you decide

- **Community Law NZ** — free advice, will review your situation and explain your options before you commit to filing: [communitylaw.org.nz](https://communitylaw.org.nz/)
- **Citizens Advice Bureau** — free guidance on process: [cab.org.nz](https://www.cab.org.nz/)
- **Te Kāhui Tika Tangata infoline** — will explain the process without committing you to a complaint: 0800 496 877

## If you're an international student or recent migrant

Discrimination on the basis of national origin or ethnicity is unlawful. A blanket "no international students" policy, application requirements that function as filters for ethnicity, or differential treatment of applications from people with non-Western names are all potentially unlawful under the Human Rights Act.

The absence of NZ rental history, however, is not a protected ground. Landlords can decline applications that lack local references — this is a legitimate (if frustrating) selection criterion. The key is whether the stated reason is the real reason.

If you're navigating the rental market without NZ rental history, the guides on [renting without NZ references](/posts/renting-without-nz-references-auckland/) and the [international student renting guide](/posts/international-students-renting-auckland/) cover what helps and what landlords can and cannot demand.

If you think you've been discriminated against, three things to take from this guide:

- The thirteen grounds in the [Human Rights Act 1993](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0082/latest/whole.html) are clear and the complaints process is free.
- Documentation is what makes a complaint actionable — written communication, dates, names, a record of what was asked of you.
- Get advice before you file. [Community Law NZ](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) and the [Human Rights Commission infoline](https://tikatangata.org.nz/) (0800 496 877) will both help you assess what you have, at no cost.
