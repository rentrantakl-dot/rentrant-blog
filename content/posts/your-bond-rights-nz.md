You're moving out. You've cleaned the place, patched the nail holes, and scrubbed the oven until your arm went numb. Now you want your bond back.

This should be simple. Often, it's not.

Bond disputes are the single most common issue at the Tenancy Tribunal. And most of them come down to the same handful of disagreements: what counts as damage versus normal wear, who's responsible for what, and whether the place was documented properly at the start.

The frustrating thing is that most bond problems are avoidable. Not by being a perfect tenant — by understanding the rules, documenting everything, and knowing what your landlord can and can't claim for.

---

## The basics: how bond works in New Zealand

### How much can they charge?

Your bond can be a maximum of **4 weeks' rent**. If you're paying $600/week, your bond is capped at $2,400. Any amount above that is illegal — full stop.

Since 1 December 2025, landlords can also charge a **pet bond** if you're keeping a pet. This is capped at an additional **2 weeks' rent** — so for a $600/week property, the maximum pet bond is $1,200.

**Maximum upfront costs for a $600/week rental:**

| Item | Amount | Legal max |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Bond | $2,400 | 4 weeks' rent |
| Pet bond (if applicable) | $1,200 | 2 weeks' rent |
| Rent in advance | $1,200 | 2 weeks' rent |
| **Total (with pet)** | **$4,800** | |
| **Total (without pet)** | **$3,600** | |

That's a lot of money. And it's supposed to be protected.

### Where does the bond go?

Your landlord **does not keep your bond**. They are legally required to lodge it with Tenancy Services within **23 working days** of receiving it. Tenancy Services holds the money in a trust account until the tenancy ends.

When the bond is lodged, you'll receive a confirmation from Tenancy Services. If you don't receive this within about a month of paying your bond, something is wrong.

> If your landlord hasn't lodged the bond, that's a breach of the Residential Tenancies Act. They can face exemplary damages of up to $1,000 through the Tenancy Tribunal. Check your bond status at [tenancy.govt.nz](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/bond/).

### What is the bond for?

The bond covers:
- **Unpaid rent** at the end of the tenancy
- **Damage to the property** beyond fair wear and tear
- **Cleaning costs** if the property isn't left in a reasonably clean state

It does **not** cover:
- Normal wear and tear (more on this below)
- Pre-existing damage that was there when you moved in
- Maintenance the landlord should have done during the tenancy
- Upgrades or improvements the landlord wants to make

## Fair wear and tear vs damage — the line that causes all the fights

This is where most bond disputes happen. The landlord says you damaged the carpet. You say it was already worn when you moved in. Neither of you has great evidence. It goes to the Tribunal.

### What is fair wear and tear?

Fair wear and tear is the natural deterioration that happens from normal use of a property over time. It's expected and it's the landlord's cost, not yours.

**Examples of fair wear and tear:**
- Carpet wearing thin in high-traffic areas (hallway, lounge)
- Small scuff marks on walls from normal furniture placement
- Faded paint or curtains from sun exposure
- Minor marks on benchtops from normal kitchen use
- Wear on door handles, light switches, taps
- Small chips in tiles from normal use

**Examples of actual damage (your responsibility):**
- Large holes in walls (beyond normal picture hooks)
- Burns on carpet or benchtops
- Broken windows, doors, or fixtures
- Stains on carpet from spills that weren't cleaned
- Pet damage — scratched doors, chewed skirting boards, urine stains
- Mould caused by tenant not ventilating properly (though this is often disputed)

### The grey zone

Plenty of situations fall somewhere in between, and this is where documentation matters.

- **Picture hooks:** Small nail holes from normal picture hanging are generally fair wear and tear. Large holes or excessive numbers of them might not be.
- **Marks behind furniture:** Scuff marks from a couch against a wall? Fair wear and tear. A large gouge? Probably damage.
- **Carpet stains:** A faint mark that's been there for three years of normal living? Fair wear. A red wine stain from last week? Damage.

The Tribunal applies a "reasonable person" test: would a reasonable person expect this level of wear from normal use over the length of the tenancy? If yes, it's fair wear and tear.

## How to protect yourself: documentation is everything

The best thing you can do — at the start and end of every tenancy — is document the property's condition thoroughly.

### When you move in

Do a property inspection **before or on the day you move in**, and record everything.

**How to document:**

1. **Take photos of every room** — walls, floors, ceilings, fixtures
2. **Close-up shots of any existing damage** — marks, stains, chips, cracks
3. **Check and photograph:**
   - All window and door locks (do they work?)
   - Oven, stovetop, rangehood
   - Hot water — how long to heat up?
   - Taps, toilets, showers (any leaks?)
   - Carpet condition in each room
   - Walls and paintwork
   - Curtains and blinds
   - Garden and exterior
4. **Make sure photos are timestamped** — your phone does this automatically
5. **Complete the property inspection report** — your landlord should provide one. If they don't, create your own and send them a copy by email

> The property inspection report is your insurance policy. If you skip it, you're relying on the landlord's memory of what the place looked like when you moved in. That rarely works in your favour.

### During the tenancy

- Report maintenance issues in writing (email) as they arise
- Keep copies of all correspondence about property condition
- If damage occurs, photograph it and notify the landlord promptly — honesty here protects you later

### When you move out

1. **Clean the property thoroughly** — it should be in a reasonably clean state, comparable to when you moved in (allowing for fair wear and tear)
2. **Take photos of every room again** — same angles as your move-in photos if possible
3. **Do a final inspection with the landlord** if they're willing — walk through together and agree on the condition
4. **Compare your move-in and move-out photos** — this is your evidence if there's a dispute

## Getting your bond back: the process

### If everyone agrees

When the tenancy ends, the landlord files a bond refund form with Tenancy Services. If both parties agree on how the bond should be split (full refund, partial refund, or full claim by landlord), Tenancy Services processes it.

**Timeline:** Tenancy Services aims to process agreed refunds within **5-10 working days** of receiving the completed form.

You can initiate the refund yourself through [Tenancy Services' online bond service](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/bond/). You don't need to wait for your landlord to start the process.

### If you disagree

If the landlord wants to claim part or all of your bond and you disagree, **do not sign the bond refund form agreeing to their claim**. Once you sign, it's done.

Instead:

1. **Tell the landlord in writing** that you dispute their claim and why
2. **Provide your evidence** — move-in photos, move-out photos, correspondence about maintenance
3. **Try to negotiate** — sometimes you can agree on a smaller amount (e.g., they wanted $800, you agree to $200 for a specific item that was genuinely your fault)
4. **If you can't agree, apply to the Tenancy Tribunal** — either party can do this

### What happens at the Tribunal

The Tribunal will look at:
- The property inspection report (if one exists)
- Photos from move-in and move-out
- The length of the tenancy (longer tenancies = more fair wear and tear expected)
- Any correspondence about maintenance or damage during the tenancy
- Whether the landlord's claims are reasonable

The adjudicator will determine what's fair wear and tear and what's genuine damage, and order the bond divided accordingly.

**Tribunal application fee:** Around $20. If you win, this can be added to your claim.

## The new pet bond rules (from 1 December 2025)

The Residential Tenancies Amendment Act introduced pet bonds, which is a big change for renters with animals.

### What you need to know:

- Landlords **can** charge a pet bond of up to **2 weeks' additional rent**
- This is **separate** from the regular bond — so the total bonded amount can be up to 6 weeks' rent
- The pet bond covers **pet-specific damage** only — not general wear and tear
- It must be lodged with Tenancy Services, same as the regular bond
- At the end of the tenancy, the pet bond is refunded unless there's pet-related damage

### What counts as pet damage?

- Scratched wooden floors or doors
- Chewed skirting boards or fixtures
- Urine stains on carpet
- Damage to garden or fencing from digging
- Flea infestation requiring treatment

### What doesn't count:

- Normal wear from pet hair on carpet (this is more like fair wear)
- Odour that can be addressed with normal cleaning
- Garden wear from a dog running on grass

The pet bond is still new, and the Tribunal is still developing case law around what's reasonable. Document the property's condition before the pet moves in — separate from your general move-in photos — so there's a clear baseline.

## Common landlord claims that don't hold up

Some landlords try to claim bond for things that aren't your responsibility. Here are claims the Tribunal frequently rejects:

- **"The carpet needs replacing"** — if you've been there for 5+ years, carpet replacement is a maintenance cost, not damage. Carpets have a limited life span (typically 8-10 years).
- **"The walls need repainting"** — paint fades and marks over time. Small scuffs and marks from normal living are fair wear and tear. If you've been there several years, repainting is the landlord's maintenance cost.
- **"Professional cleaning is needed"** — you need to leave the place reasonably clean, but you're not required to pay for professional cleaning unless your tenancy agreement specifically requires it AND the place was professionally cleaned before you moved in.
- **"The garden is overgrown"** — if garden maintenance was your responsibility and you neglected it, that's legitimate. But a garden that's simply grown during a normal tenancy isn't damage.

## Quick reference: bond rights checklist

**At the start of the tenancy:**
- [ ] Bond is no more than 4 weeks' rent (+ 2 weeks' pet bond if applicable)
- [ ] Rent in advance is no more than 2 weeks' rent
- [ ] You've received confirmation that the bond was lodged with Tenancy Services
- [ ] You've completed a thorough property inspection with timestamped photos
- [ ] You've kept a copy of the property inspection report

**During the tenancy:**
- [ ] Report maintenance issues in writing
- [ ] Photograph any damage that occurs and notify the landlord
- [ ] Keep copies of all correspondence

**At the end:**
- [ ] Clean the property thoroughly
- [ ] Take move-out photos (same rooms, same angles as move-in)
- [ ] Do a walk-through with the landlord if possible
- [ ] Don't sign a bond refund form you disagree with
- [ ] Apply to the Tribunal if you can't reach agreement
