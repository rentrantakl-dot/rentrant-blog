You've found a flat that ticks most of the boxes. Decent location, reasonable rent, not obviously falling apart. Before you hand over four weeks' bond and sign anything, here's the question worth asking: *what do you actually know about who you're renting from?*

In New Zealand, checking a landlord before you commit is easier than most renters realise. You don't need a lawyer or inside knowledge. You need about 30 minutes and the right places to look.

---

## Why bother checking?

Because the landlord you deal with matters as much as the property itself.

A great property managed by a slow-responding, non-compliant landlord is a genuinely miserable tenancy. The Tenancy Tribunal receives thousands of applications per year — and the same landlords appear repeatedly. A bit of research can tell you whether you're walking into an easy tenancy or setting yourself up for 12 months of email threads going nowhere.

Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, landlords have significant legal obligations — but some ignore them unless someone pushes back. Knowing who you're dealing with puts you in a position to push back, or to walk away before you've signed anything.

---

## Step 1: Search the Tenancy Tribunal records

The Tribunal publishes its decisions publicly. This is your most powerful research tool.

Go to **[justice.govt.nz/tribunals/tenancy-tribunal/decisions](https://www.justice.govt.nz/tribunals/tenancy-tribunal/decisions/)** and search by:
- **Landlord name** — use the surname for individual landlords, or the company name for corporate owners
- **Property address** — if you know the address

What you're looking for:
- Cases where the landlord was the respondent (meaning a tenant took them to Tribunal)
- The issue types — bond disputes, mould, unlawful termination, Healthy Homes non-compliance
- The outcome — was the landlord ordered to pay? Were compliance orders made?

A single case isn't necessarily a red flag — disputes happen. But a pattern across multiple tenants, or cases involving serious issues (Healthy Homes non-compliance, retaliatory eviction, bond theft), should give you serious pause.

> **Key point:** Tribunal decisions name individual landlords and company landlords. If your landlord is a company, search the company name. If it's an individual, try their full name and also their surname alone — the transcription of names in decisions varies.

---

## Step 2: Look up the company on the Companies Register

If the landlord is a company or trust — common for larger rental portfolios — look them up on the **[Companies Register](https://app.companiesoffice.govt.nz/)**.

What this tells you:
- Who the directors are (and their other directorships — a landlord with a portfolio of companies is a different beast from someone renting out a family home)
- Whether the company is in good standing or has been struck off/liquidated
- Any registered charges against the company (potential mortgage stress)

A company that has been struck off and re-registered, or where director names have changed frequently, is worth interrogating further.

---

## Step 3: Check if they're a property manager (and who it is)

Not all landlords manage their own properties. If a property management company is involved, research them separately.

Property management companies in New Zealand aren't licensed — anyone can operate one. But their Tribunal record is searchable just like an individual landlord's.

Common property managers in Auckland include Barfoot & Thompson, Crockers, Quinovic, and a long tail of independent operators. Search the management company name in the Tribunal database and look for patterns.

Questions worth asking:
- Is this a large company with dedicated maintenance staff, or a one-person operation?
- Do they have a dedicated after-hours line for urgent repairs?
- What's their response time standard in the tenancy agreement?

If the property is managed by a company but you'd like to know who the actual owner is, you can ask. Under the Residential Tenancies Act, tenants have the right to know the name and contact details of their landlord — even if day-to-day management goes through a property manager.

## Step 4: Search RentRant

**[RentRant](https://rentrant.nz/locations/search)** aggregates tenant reviews by address. If a previous tenant has rated the property or the landlord's responsiveness, dampness, maintenance quality, or heating — it's searchable.

The platform is newer, so not every address has reviews. But if yours does, it's the most direct signal you'll find: actual tenants, actual experience, at that actual address.

If there are no reviews yet: that's also useful. It means there's no public record — you're going in with less information, which means the other checks matter more.

---

## Step 5: Ask for references from previous tenants

This one most people skip. It's worth doing.

Ask the landlord or property manager: *"Can you provide contact details for a previous tenant at this property?"*

A landlord who manages well and treats tenants fairly will generally have no problem with this. A landlord who refuses, or who can't think of a single previous tenant willing to take a call, is telling you something.

If they do provide a reference, the questions worth asking:
- Was maintenance dealt with promptly?
- Were there any issues getting the bond back?
- Were there Healthy Homes problems — heating, insulation, mould, draughts?
- Would they rent from this landlord again?

---

## Step 6: Ask directly about the Healthy Homes compliance

Since 1 July 2025, every rental in New Zealand must comply with the Healthy Homes Standards. Landlords must provide a written compliance statement within 21 days of a tenancy starting — but you can ask before you sign.

Questions to put in writing (email, so you have a record):

> *"Can you confirm that the property is currently compliant with all five Healthy Homes Standards — heating, insulation, ventilation, moisture ingress and drainage, and draught stopping? Can you provide a copy of the compliance statement?"*

If they hedge, provide vague assurances, or say it's "mostly compliant" — that's a red flag. The standard is binary: compliant or not. "Mostly" doesn't pass the Tribunal's test.

---

## Step 7: Do a basic online search

Simple but effective. Search the landlord or property manager's name along with "NZ" or "Auckland" plus a few keywords:
- "[Name] Tenancy Tribunal"
- "[Name] landlord complaints"
- "[Management company] reviews"

Facebook groups — particularly Auckland flatting and renter groups — often surface experiences that don't appear in formal records. People post about bad landlords. A quick search can surface threads that save you from a difficult tenancy.

## Red flags to take seriously

Some things should give you genuine pause. A landlord who:

- **Won't put things in writing.** Verbal agreements are enforceable but far harder to prove. If they resist email and want to handle everything by phone, ask yourself why.
- **Has multiple Tribunal losses** — particularly for the same types of issues.
- **Pushes you to sign immediately.** Good landlords don't need to pressure you. High-demand properties in competitive suburbs go fast, but anyone creating manufactured urgency around a contract is worth being wary of.
- **Can't produce a Healthy Homes compliance statement** — especially for issues like heating and insulation. Either they don't have one (non-compliant), or they're disorganised enough that you'll spend the tenancy chasing them.
- **Takes your bond and doesn't lodge it.** Under the RTA, the landlord must lodge your bond with Tenancy Services within 23 working days. You can verify this by logging into [My Tenancy](https://mytenancy.tenancy.govt.nz/) — you'll receive a bond receipt number when it's lodged.

## A note on discrimination

New Zealand's Human Rights Act 1993 prohibits landlords from discriminating in rental applications on the basis of sex, marital status, religious belief, colour, race, ethnic or national origins, disability, age, political opinion, employment status, family status, or sexual orientation.

In practice, discrimination in rental applications does happen — and it's hard to prove. If you suspect you've been declined on a protected ground, the Human Rights Commission provides a free complaints process at **[hrc.co.nz](https://www.hrc.co.nz/)**.

## What to do if you find something concerning

Finding a Tribunal case or a bad review doesn't automatically mean you should walk away. Context matters.

**Ask about it directly.** If there's a Tribunal case from four years ago involving a bond dispute, put it to the landlord: *"I noticed a Tribunal case involving this address — can you tell me what happened?"* How they respond tells you a lot. A reasonable landlord will explain. A defensive or dismissive response is information.

**Negotiate written protections into the agreement.** If you proceed, make sure any concerns about maintenance, heating, or compliance are documented — either in a property condition report or in email exchanges before signing.

**Know your exit options.** If things go sideways early in the tenancy, tenants have options — including applying to the Tribunal for remedies or, in serious cases, termination of the tenancy.
