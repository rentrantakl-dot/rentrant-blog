You've done the viewing. You've checked for mould, asked about the heating, maybe Googled the landlord. But have you asked about the smoke alarms? Or whether the front door actually locks properly?

Safety obligations in NZ rental law are more specific than most renters realise — and more limited. Your landlord has clear legal duties around fire detection and building security. A few areas that intuitively feel like they should be regulated (window falls, for instance) aren't, at least not in a way that applies specifically to existing rental stock. Knowing the difference matters, because it tells you when you have a legitimate complaint and when you're better off negotiating a fix before you sign.

---

## Smoke Alarms

This is the clearest part of the safety picture. The [Residential Tenancies (Smoke Alarms and Insulation) Regulations 2016](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2016/0128/latest/DLM6856201.html) set out specific requirements that apply to every rental property in New Zealand.

### The rules

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| Alarm type | Photoelectric only — not ionisation |
| Battery life | At least 8 years, or hard-wired |
| Location — beside bedrooms | Within 3 metres of each bedroom door |
| Location — sleeping rooms | Inside each room used for sleeping |
| Multi-storey properties | At least one alarm per level |
| Caravans and sleep-outs | Required if used for accommodation |

*Source: Residential Tenancies (Smoke Alarms and Insulation) Regulations 2016, in force 1 July 2016.*

### Why the type matters

Ionisation alarms — the older style, common before 2016 — are slow to detect slow-smouldering fires. Consumer NZ has specifically flagged these as inadequate. Only photoelectric alarms meet the current standard. If the alarms in your rental look like they've been there since the 90s, check the type. If they're ionisation, the landlord needs to replace them.

### Who is responsible for what

The **landlord** must:
- Install working alarms before each new tenancy starts
- Ensure they're operational and not past their manufacturer's expiry date
- Maintain them throughout the tenancy

The **tenant** must:
- Not tamper with or remove alarms
- Replace batteries when needed (for older-type alarms still meeting the standard)
- Notify the landlord promptly if an alarm is defective

### What non-compliance costs a landlord

A landlord who doesn't meet the smoke alarm requirements faces exemplary damages of up to **$7,200**. [Tenancy Services has published cases](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/about-tenancy-services/news/landlords-fined-for-tenancy-breaches-across-34-properties/) where landlords with non-compliant smoke alarms across multiple properties faced five-figure total penalties. It's enforced.

## Locks and Security

[Section 46 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95501.html) requires your landlord to "provide and maintain such locks and other similar devices as are necessary to ensure that the premises are reasonably secure."

That's it. The law doesn't specify a type of lock, a deadbolt rating, or any technical standard. "Reasonably secure" is the test — and what that looks like depends on the age and character of the property.

### What this means in practice

A latch lock on an exterior door probably doesn't meet "reasonably secure" in most contexts. A deadbolt on the front door and window locks on accessible ground-floor windows is a reasonable baseline for most suburban rentals. Shared buildings — apartments, boarding houses — may require intercoms or secured access to common areas, depending on the setup.

If you move in and find a lock is broken, doesn't latch properly, or was clearly modified by a previous tenant, that's a maintenance issue under [RTA section 45](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95099.html). Put the request to fix it in writing.

### Limits on what either party can do

Neither landlord nor tenant can unilaterally add, remove, or alter locks without the other's written consent. If you want to fit a chain lock, or the property manager wants to install a security alarm — both parties have to agree.

If a key is lost or a lock fails through no fault of yours, the landlord bears the cost of replacement or repair.

## Fire Egress

Under the [Fire and Emergency New Zealand (Fire Safety, Evacuation Procedures, and Evacuation Schemes) Regulations 2018](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2018/0096/latest/LMS46366.html), the means of escape from fire must be kept clear and functional at all times.

For a rental house, the practical obligations split between landlord and tenant:

**Landlord:** ensure structural exits work — doors and windows that open from the inside, no sealed or boarded exits, fire-rated doors (where required by the building) that close properly.

**Tenant:** don't block hallways, corridors, or exit doors with furniture or stored items. Don't prop fire doors open.

Formal evacuation schemes — signage, drills, documented procedures — apply to larger occupied buildings like boarding houses and apartment blocks. For a standard rental house, the obligations are simpler: exits must work, and neither party should be blocking them.

## Window Safety: the Gap Worth Knowing About

This one gets asked a lot, especially from renters with young children. Here's the honest answer: **there is no specific NZ regulation requiring window stays or restricters in existing rental properties.**

The Building Code clause F4 sets safety-from-falling requirements for new construction and consented alterations — but that doesn't automatically apply to existing rental stock. The [Healthy Homes ventilation standard](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/ventilation/) requires windows to be openable (and fixable open for ventilation), but that's a ventilation requirement, not a child-safety one.

What does exist is the landlord's general duty under RTA s.45(1)(b) to maintain the premises "in a reasonable state of repair having regard to the age and character of the premises." Whether an openable first-floor window without a stay breaches that duty is ultimately a Tribunal question — there's no clear statutory answer.

If you're moving in with children and the property has high-level openable windows, ask the landlord to fit window stays. A reasonable landlord will. If they won't, you can request Tenancy Services assist with mediation — or the Tribunal can determine whether the existing condition breaches the maintenance duty.

## RTA s.45: The Broader Safety Duty

Behind the specific rules is a general obligation. [Section 45(1)(c) of the RTA](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95099.html) requires landlords to "comply with all requirements in respect of buildings, health, and safety under any enactment so far as they apply to the premises."

In plain English: if there's a building code, a health regulation, or a safety standard that applies to the property, the landlord has to meet it. This doesn't extend to requirements that only apply to new buildings — but for things like proper weathertightness, safe electrical wiring, and compliant gas installations, the obligation exists.

This section is the legal anchor for a lot of disputes that don't fit neatly into the Healthy Homes Standards or smoke alarm regulations. If there's a safety problem that isn't explicitly addressed elsewhere, s.45(1)(c) is often the lever.

## What to Do If Safety Standards Aren't Met

The path is the same as most tenancy issues:

1. **Put it in writing.** Email the landlord identifying the specific problem — what it is, where it is, and why you're concerned. Keep the email.
2. **Give a reasonable deadline.** For a missing or broken smoke alarm, 14 days is reasonable. For a broken lock on an exterior door, 48–72 hours — this affects your security immediately.
3. **Contact Tenancy Services.** If the landlord doesn't respond, [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) can provide guidance and formal mediation.
4. **Apply to the Tenancy Tribunal.** If the issue isn't fixed and you're suffering ongoing harm or non-compliance, the Tribunal can order the landlord to comply and award damages.

## The Bottom Line

Smoke alarms: well-regulated, with real enforcement. Locks: covered by law but the standard is deliberately vague. Window stays: a genuine gap in the regulatory framework that the law hasn't caught up with yet. Fire egress: split responsibility, and the rules are straightforward if you know them.

Going into a viewing with these specifics in mind means you can ask the right questions, negotiate fixes before you sign, and know exactly what to put in writing if something goes wrong.
