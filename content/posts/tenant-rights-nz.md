Your rights as a renter in New Zealand are set out in the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, updated regularly since. The law is on your side more than many renters realise — and the Tenancy Tribunal actively enforces it.

This is a guide to the key rights every tenant in New Zealand should know: before they sign, while they're in a tenancy, and if things go wrong.

---

## Before you sign: what landlords can and can't ask for

### Bond limits

Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, the maximum bond a landlord can charge is **4 weeks' rent**. No exceptions.

From **1 December 2025**, landlords can charge an additional **pet bond of up to 2 weeks' rent** — but only if the tenancy agreement allows pets. If it doesn't allow pets, there's no pet bond.

| Payment | Maximum | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Bond | 4 weeks' rent | Longstanding rule |
| Rent in advance | 2 weeks' rent | Longstanding rule |
| Pet bond | 2 weeks' rent | From 1 December 2025 |

Anything above these amounts is unlawful. A landlord asking for more than 4 weeks' bond is in breach — and you can apply to the Tribunal to recover the overpaid amount.

### Bond lodgement

Your bond must be lodged with **Tenancy Services** within **23 working days** of the landlord receiving it. This protects your money — it sits in a government account until the end of the tenancy.

You should receive a bond lodgement receipt. If you haven't received one within about a month of paying, log in to [My Tenancy](https://mytenancy.tenancy.govt.nz/) to check. A landlord who pockets bond and doesn't lodge it is committing an offence.

> **Warning:** If your bond hasn't been lodged after 23 working days, put it in writing to your landlord immediately. This is a Tribunal issue if they don't rectify it.

### Rental application requirements

Landlords can ask for standard identity and income information. They can't:
- Demand a credit check fee (you can be asked to consent to a check, but it must be at no cost to you)
- Require you to waive any rights under the RTA
- Discriminate based on protected grounds under the Human Rights Act 1993 (race, ethnicity, disability, family status, religion, and others)

## During the tenancy: your ongoing rights

### Rent increases

Landlords can only increase rent **once every 12 months**. They must give **60 days' written notice** before the increase takes effect.

The clock on the 12-month period resets from the date the last increase took effect — not from when you signed the lease. If your landlord tries to raise rent within 12 months of the last increase, or with less than 60 days' notice, the notice is invalid and you don't have to pay the higher amount.

> **Key point:** A rent increase notice that doesn't meet the legal requirements is void. You are not obliged to pay the increased rent until a valid notice has been given and the 60 days have elapsed.

### The right to a property that meets Healthy Homes Standards

Since **1 July 2025**, every rental property in New Zealand must comply with all five Healthy Homes Standards. This is a legal requirement — not a guideline, not optional.

The five standards cover:

| Standard | What's required |
|----------|----------------|
| Heating | Fixed heater in main living area capable of heating to 18°C |
| Insulation | Ceiling and underfloor insulation meeting minimum R-values |
| Ventilation | Openable windows in all habitable rooms; extractors in kitchen and bathroom |
| Moisture ingress and drainage | No water entering from roof, walls, or ground; adequate drainage |
| Draught stopping | No significant gaps in walls, ceilings, floors, or joinery |

Landlords must provide a written Healthy Homes compliance statement within **21 days** of a new tenancy starting. If you haven't received one, request it in writing.

For a full breakdown of each standard, see our guides on [heating requirements](/posts/heating-requirements-rental-nz/), [insulation requirements](/posts/insulation-requirements-rental-nz/), and [mould and ventilation](/posts/mould-in-your-auckland-rental/).

### The right to a property in reasonable condition

Under Section 45 of the Residential Tenancies Act, landlords must keep the property in a reasonable state of repair, comply with building, health, and safety laws, and provide and maintain in reasonable condition all the fittings and appliances the tenancy agreement specifies.

This means that if the oven breaks, the hot water cylinder fails, or the roof leaks — fixing it is the landlord's responsibility, not yours. Report maintenance issues in writing and keep copies.

### Privacy and quiet enjoyment

Your landlord cannot enter the property without proper notice except in an emergency. The standard rule is:

- **General inspection or non-urgent work:** At least 24 hours' notice, but access is limited to between 8am and 7pm
- **Urgent repairs:** No notice required, but only to the extent necessary to address the emergency

A landlord who turns up unannounced, harasses you, or interferes with your ability to live in the property quietly is in breach of your right to quiet enjoyment. Document the dates and times of any unwanted entry.

## Rent: what's fair and what you can do about it

If you think your rent is above market rate, you can apply to the Tenancy Tribunal for a market rent assessment. The Tribunal can order rent reduced to market level if there's a clear gap.

The [Tenancy Services Market Rent Tool](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/market-rent/) lets you see what bonds are actually being lodged for comparable properties in your area — a more reliable indicator than listing prices.

## Ending a tenancy: the rules

### Periodic tenancies (week-to-week, month-to-month)

Since **30 January 2025**, the rules are:

| Who gives notice | Notice required | Reason required? |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Tenant leaving | 21 days | No |
| Landlord (no stated reason) | 90 days | No |
| Landlord (owner wants to move in) | 42 days | Yes — must be genuine |
| Landlord (sale with vacant possession) | 42 days | Yes — must be genuine |

The 90-day no-cause notice reintroduced in January 2025 was a significant change. Landlords can now end a periodic tenancy without giving a reason — but the 90-day period gives you time to find somewhere new.

> **Warning:** Retaliatory termination is illegal. If a landlord gives you a termination notice shortly after you've asserted your rights — complained about Healthy Homes, applied to the Tribunal, or contacted Tenancy Services — that notice may be challengeable as retaliatory. Put your complaint in writing before the notice was issued; that timeline is your evidence.

### Fixed-term tenancies

If you're in a fixed-term tenancy, the landlord generally cannot end it before the end date except in very limited circumstances (sale with vacant possession, owner moving in — and even then the notice periods apply).

At the end of a fixed term, the tenancy rolls to periodic unless either party gives notice of termination (or notice of a new fixed term) between 90 and 21 days before the end date. If nobody acts, the tenancy just continues — at the same rent — on a week-to-week basis.

## Your bond: getting it back

At the end of your tenancy, the bond is refunded — usually in full, unless there are legitimate deductions.

A landlord can only deduct from bond for:
- Unpaid rent
- Damage beyond fair wear and tear
- Unpaid utilities the tenant was responsible for

**Fair wear and tear is not a legitimate deduction.** Faded paint, worn carpet from normal use, small scuff marks — these are ordinary wear and are the landlord's cost. Only damage caused by negligence, carelessness, or deliberate acts is deductible.

Your best protection: a thorough property condition report at the start of the tenancy, with photos. This sets the baseline. Anything that was already damaged before you moved in can't be deducted from your bond.

If the landlord tries to claim more than you think is fair, don't agree under pressure. You can let the bond refund go to the Tribunal to be assessed. The Tribunal makes these decisions regularly and applies the "fair wear and tear" standard properly.

## What the Tenancy Tribunal can do for you

The Tenancy Tribunal is a specialist tribunal that handles residential tenancy disputes in New Zealand. It's relatively accessible — the fee to apply is currently $20.44, and the process is designed to be used without a lawyer.

The Tribunal can:
- Order landlords to fix compliance issues (including Healthy Homes)
- Award rent reductions for periods the property wasn't up to standard
- Award compensation for losses caused by the landlord's breach
- Award exemplary damages for serious or deliberate breaches — up to **$7,200 for smaller landlords** (fewer than 6 properties) and up to **$50,000 for larger landlords** (6 or more properties)
- Determine bond refund disputes
- Terminate a tenancy in serious cases

**Before applying:** Tenancy Services offers free mediation. Most disputes can be resolved at mediation without a hearing. If you're not sure whether to apply, call the Tenancy Services line first on **0800 836 262**.

> **Tip:** Keep all communications in writing. Landlords often behave differently when things are on email. The Tribunal relies heavily on documentary evidence — written requests, written responses, photos, dates.

## If you're facing eviction

### Challenging a termination notice

If you receive a termination notice and think it's invalid or retaliatory, you have options:
1. Apply to the Tribunal to have the notice declared invalid
2. Contact Community Law NZ for free legal help
3. If you suspect retaliation, gather evidence of the timeline — when did you complain, when did you receive the notice

### 90-day no-cause notices

These are now legal in New Zealand. But the notice must be in the correct form, give the correct notice period, and not be issued within a period of retaliatory intent. If any of those conditions aren't met, the notice may be challengeable.

### Emergency housing

If you're facing displacement with no housing option, contact **MSD (Work and Income)** — emergency housing assistance is available. The Citizens Advice Bureau can also help navigate your options.

## Your responsibilities as a tenant

Rights come with corresponding responsibilities. The Residential Tenancies Act requires tenants to:
- Pay rent on time
- Keep the property reasonably clean and tidy
- Notify the landlord promptly of any damage or required repairs
- Ensure adequate ventilation (opening windows, using extractor fans)
- Not cause damage beyond fair wear and tear
- Give proper notice when leaving

A tenant who meets these obligations is in the strongest possible position if a dispute arises.
