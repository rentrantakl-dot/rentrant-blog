For the first time, New Zealand tenancy law has a real pet regime. Since **1 December 2025**, the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS1561978.html) sets out when you can keep a pet in a rental, how landlords must handle requests, what a pet bond can be, and who carries the cost when a pet damages the place. Before that date, pets sat in a legal grey zone — landlords could say no for any reason or none. Now the default has shifted: refusal needs **reasonable grounds**.

The regime is part of the wider Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024 changes — see our overview of [the 2025 tenancy law changes](/posts/tenancy-law-changes-2025/) for the rest. This guide is the pet rules in full, including the parts most summaries get wrong.

---

## The New Baseline

Under [s42C](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS1561978.html), a tenant may keep a pet only if the **tenancy agreement allows it** or the **landlord has given written consent**. Keeping a pet without either is an unlawful act, with exemplary damages of up to **$750** under [Schedule 1A](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM3285790.html).

One carve-out matters enormously: a **disability assist dog is not a "pet"** under the Act's definition. No consent is needed, no pet bond can be charged, and none of the rules below apply to one.

For new agreements, landlords can't quietly opt out either. Under [s42D](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS1561979.html), a tenancy agreement made on or after 1 December 2025 may only prohibit pets if it **states reasonable grounds** for the prohibition in the agreement itself — an unreasoned "no pets" clause is of no effect, and including one is an unlawful act (up to $1,500). Worth checking before you sign anything: our guide to [signing your first tenancy agreement](/posts/signing-your-first-tenancy-agreement-nz/) covers what else to look for.

## Making the Request

The process under [s42E](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS1561980.html) is deliberately simple:

1. **Ask in writing, before the pet moves in.** Describe the animal — type, breed, size, age. Tenancy Services publishes a [request form](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/assets/forms-templates/request-to-keep-a-pet-at-a-rental-property-12371.pdf) you can use, and its [pet consent page](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/rules-about-pets/requesting-pet-consent/) walks through the process.
2. **The landlord must respond in writing within 21 days**, either consenting (with any reasonable conditions) or refusing **with the grounds stated**.
3. Consent can't be refused without reasonable grounds — doing so is an unlawful act with exemplary damages of up to **$1,500**.

> **Warning:** You'll see it claimed — including, until recently, on this site's own companion brief — that if the landlord doesn't respond within 21 days, the request is *automatically approved*. **The Act says no such thing.** There is no deemed-consent rule. What actually happens: failing to respond within 21 days is an unlawful act under s42E(5), up to $1,500, and your remedy is the Tenancy Tribunal. Moving the pet in on the strength of silence would put *you* in breach of s42C. Chase the response in writing, then apply to the Tribunal if the silence continues.

## What Counts as Reasonable Refusal

[Section 42F](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS1561981.html) gives a non-exhaustive list of reasonable grounds:

| Ground | What it looks like in practice |
|---|---|
| Premises unsuitable | A large dog in a small unfenced flat; no outdoor space for the animal's needs |
| Rules prohibit the pet | Body corporate bylaws or other applicable rules ban the animal |
| Tenant non-compliance with pet bylaws | You haven't met registration or other legal requirements for the animal |
| The specific pet is unsuitable | Number of pets, size/type/breed relative to the property, propensity to cause damage or disruption, a dog classified as dangerous or menacing under the Dog Control Act 1996, or a history of attacking |
| Conditions refused | You won't agree to a reasonable condition the landlord proposes |
| Past breach | You previously breached a reasonable pet-related condition |

What that list conspicuously doesn't include: "we prefer no pets", "pets cause damage" as a general proposition, or "the last tenant's dog was a nightmare". The grounds have to be about **this property, this animal, or this tenant** — not pets in the abstract.

## Conditions and the Pet Bond

Consent can come with **reasonable conditions**. [Section 42G](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS1561982.html) names three examples: paying a **pet bond**, keeping the pet restrained or contained while the landlord lawfully enters, and **professional carpet cleaning** at the end of the tenancy if the pet was allowed inside. (Note that last one — it's the statutory exception to the usual rule that professional-cleaning clauses are unenforceable.)

The pet bond itself is governed by [s18AA](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS1561960.html):

- Maximum **2 weeks' rent**
- **One pet bond per tenancy**, no matter how many pets
- Only chargeable when a pet is actually kept (or about to be) with consent
- Lodged with Tenancy Services like any bond, and charging more than 2 weeks is an unlawful act (up to $1,500)

Stacked on the [general bond](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95041.html) cap of 4 weeks' rent, the combined maximum is 6 weeks — real money worth understanding before you commit. Tenancy Services' [pet bond page](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/bond/charging-a-bond/charging-a-pet-bond/) covers the mechanics; how bonds are held and released is in [your bond rights](/posts/your-bond-rights-nz/).

## Pet Damage: The Liability Trap

This is the trade the 2025 reforms made, and pet owners should know it. Normally, a tenant's liability for **careless** damage is capped at four weeks' rent per incident. Under [s49B(1)(c)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS245569.html), damage caused by a pet is **carved out of that cap** — you're liable for the full cost of pet damage beyond fair wear and tear.

Two boundaries still hold. **Fair wear and tear is still excluded** — general carpet flattening in a home that happens to contain a cat isn't "pet damage"; scratched door frames and stained carpet are. Where the line falls is exactly the analysis in our [fair wear and tear guide](/posts/fair-wear-and-tear-nz/). And the landlord still has to **prove** the damage and its cost, with condition reports and photos, same as any bond claim.

If you have contents insurance, check whether your liability cover extends to pet damage — many policies treat it separately.

## Old Tenancies and Existing Pets

The transition rules in [Schedule 1AA](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM6866582.html) draw two lines that are easy to mix up:

**Clause 46 — existing pets are grandfathered.** A pet you lawfully kept before 1 December 2025 — because the agreement allowed it, the landlord had consented, or nothing prohibited it — stays lawful. No s42C consent and **no pet bond** can be required for that pet, and a renewal of the tenancy counts as continuation. A landlord can't use the new regime to re-litigate a cat who's been in residence since 2023.

**Clause 47 — old no-pet clauses survive.** The requirement that pet prohibitions state reasonable grounds (s42D) applies only to agreements **made on or after 1 December 2025**. A "no pets" clause in a 2024 agreement isn't automatically void. But — and this is the useful part — the s42E request path is still open to you: ask in writing, and the landlord still has 21 days to respond and still needs reasonable grounds to refuse.

## If You're Refused

A refusal must come **in writing with the grounds stated**. Read them against the s42F list: are they about this property and this animal, or are they pets-in-general? If the grounds look unreasonable — or you got silence instead of a response — the [Tenancy Tribunal](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/) is the enforcement path. Applying costs **$29** and you don't need a lawyer.

One honesty note: this regime is barely seven months old, and there's not yet a meaningful body of Tribunal decisions marking exactly where "reasonable" sits in edge cases. The Act's wording and the s42F examples are the guide for now — which is why this article sticks to what the Act says rather than predicting how the Tribunal will rule. For a steer on your specific facts, [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) gives free advice, and Tenancy Services' [pet rules overview](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/rules-about-pets/) is the official summary.
