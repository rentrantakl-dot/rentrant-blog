Most tenants know they have rights, but the list of what landlords are actually required to do under NZ law is longer than most people realise — and covers a lot more than just keeping the place in reasonable shape.

This guide covers the full picture: what the Residential Tenancies Act 1986 and the Healthy Homes Standards require of landlords, what the rules are around bond, rent increases, and entry, and what to do when the obligations aren't being met.

---

## The tenancy agreement

A landlord must provide a written tenancy agreement before the tenancy starts. Verbal tenancy agreements are legally valid in NZ, but they create significant practical problems for both parties — and a landlord who refuses to provide anything in writing is a warning sign.

The written agreement must include:

- The full names and contact details of all landlords and tenants
- The address of the property
- The weekly rent amount
- The bond amount (if any)
- Whether the tenancy is periodic or fixed-term, and the start date

Under the **Residential Tenancies Act 1986 (s13A)**, landlords must also give tenants a copy of the Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment's *Renting and You* guide, which outlines the rights and obligations of both parties.

If you were never given a written agreement, that's a breach. It doesn't void the tenancy, but it matters if a dispute arises.

## Bond obligations

If a landlord takes a bond, there are strict legal rules around it.

**Maximum bond:** 4 weeks' rent (or up to 6 weeks if a pet bond is included, where applicable).

**Lodgement:** The landlord must lodge the bond with Tenancy Services — not hold it in their own account — within **23 working days** of receiving it. Tenancy Services holds the bond in a government trust account for the duration of the tenancy. A landlord who holds bond money privately is in breach of the Act.

**Return:** Once the tenancy ends, the bond must be returned within **10 working days**, or the landlord must apply to the Tenancy Tribunal to make a claim against it for damage or unpaid rent. A landlord who simply holds the bond past 10 working days without making a Tribunal application is in breach.

**Receipt:** If you pay bond (or rent) in cash, the landlord must provide a receipt.

You can check that your bond has been lodged at [tenancy.govt.nz](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) using your bond lodgement number, which should appear on a receipt or confirmation from Tenancy Services.

## Property condition

Under **section 45 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986**, a landlord must:

- Provide the property in a **reasonable state of repair** at the start of the tenancy
- Maintain the property in a reasonable state of repair throughout the tenancy
- Provide the property in a **reasonable state of cleanliness** at the start of the tenancy
- Comply with all building, health, and safety legislation that applies to the property

"Reasonable state of repair" covers structural elements (roof, walls, foundations), building services (plumbing, electrical), and fixtures and fittings (windows that open, doors that close, appliances that were part of the tenancy).

When something breaks — through normal wear and use, not tenant damage — the landlord is responsible for fixing it. A landlord cannot require a tenant to pay for repairs to things the tenant hasn't damaged.

## The Healthy Homes Standards

Since **1 July 2025**, all NZ rental properties must meet five minimum housing standards established under the Residential Tenancies (Healthy Homes Standards) Regulations 2019. These are not aspirational targets — they are legal requirements, and non-compliance can result in compliance orders, rent reductions, and exemplary damages through the Tenancy Tribunal.

### Heating standard

A **fixed heater** in the main living room capable of heating that room to at least **18°C** when the outdoor temperature is 1°C. The heater must be fixed to a wall, floor, or ceiling — a portable electric heater does not qualify regardless of its wattage. Unflued gas heaters are prohibited in bedrooms and living rooms.

→ [Heating standard — full detail](../heating-requirements-rental-nz/)

### Insulation standard

Ceiling insulation to a minimum of **R2.9** in Auckland (Climate Zone 1). Underfloor insulation to **R1.3** where the sub-floor is accessible. The landlord must disclose insulation details in the Healthy Homes compliance statement.

→ [Insulation standard — full detail](../insulation-requirements-rental-nz/)

### Ventilation standard

Every habitable room must have openable windows or skylights totalling at least **5% of floor area**. The kitchen must have an extractor fan or range hood vented to the outside — recirculating range hoods, HRV systems, and DVS systems explicitly do not satisfy this requirement. Every bathroom must have an extractor fan vented to the outside; a window does not substitute.

→ [Ventilation standard — full detail](../ventilation-requirements-rental-nz/)

### Draught stopping standard

Landlords must block unreasonable gaps and holes in walls, floors, ceilings, and around pipes. Unused fireplaces and chimneys must be blocked. All windows, doors, and skylights must be capable of closing properly.

### Moisture ingress and drainage standard

No water should enter the property through the roof, walls, windows, or ground. Gutters, downpipes, and drains must be maintained. Where there's an enclosed sub-floor space, a ground moisture barrier is required where it's practicable to install one.

### The compliance statement

Landlords must provide a written **Healthy Homes compliance statement** to tenants. This document states whether each of the five standards is met and provides specific details (heater model and capacity, insulation type and R-value, extractor fan details). It must be attached to the tenancy agreement or provided within **21 days of tenancy start**.

If you haven't received a compliance statement, request one in writing. Not providing one is itself a breach of the Act.

## Entry and privacy

A landlord does not have an unrestricted right to access the property. It is your home for the duration of the tenancy.

Under the Residential Tenancies Act:

| Purpose | Notice required |
|---|---|
| General inspection | At least **24 hours** written notice |
| Carrying out maintenance or repairs | At least **48 hours** written notice |
| Showing property to prospective tenants/buyers | At least **48 hours** written notice |
| Emergency (burst pipe, fire risk, etc.) | No notice required |

Entry must be at a **reasonable time** — typically between 8am and 7pm on a weekday. A landlord who enters without notice (outside a genuine emergency), who enters at unreasonable hours, or who persistently inspects far more frequently than necessary may be committing unlawful entry or harassment, both of which are grounds for Tribunal action.

Landlords cannot install surveillance cameras inside the property that monitor tenants' private spaces. External security cameras on the property are a more nuanced question — if in doubt, contact Community Law.

## Rent increases

Landlords can raise the rent, but only within strict rules:

- **Frequency:** No more than **once every 12 months**. The 12-month clock starts from the date the last increase took effect (or from the start of the tenancy if there has been no prior increase).
- **Notice:** At least **60 days** written notice before the new rent takes effect.
- **Form:** Written notice, specifying the new rent amount and the date it takes effect.

A rent increase notice that gives less than 60 days notice, or arrives less than 12 months after the last increase, is **not valid**. You are not obliged to pay the increased amount until valid notice has been given and the 60 days has passed.

## Smoke alarms

Under the **Residential Tenancies (Smoke Alarms and Insulation) Regulations 2016**, landlords must install and maintain working smoke alarms in all NZ rentals. Specific requirements:

- At least one working smoke alarm within **3 metres of each bedroom door**
- At least one on each level of a multi-storey home
- Long-life photoelectric alarms (for new installations and replacements)

The landlord is responsible for installing and maintaining smoke alarms. If an alarm is missing or not working and you've told your landlord, they must replace or repair it.

## Quiet enjoyment

Under **section 38 of the Residential Tenancies Act**, the landlord must ensure the tenant has **quiet enjoyment** of the property. This means the landlord cannot:

- Enter without proper notice
- Interfere with the tenant's use of the property
- Engage in conduct that amounts to harassment — including repeated unannounced visits, intimidating communications, or interference with utilities

Harassment by a landlord is a specific breach of the Act and can attract exemplary damages at the Tenancy Tribunal.

## When obligations aren't met

If your landlord is not meeting their legal obligations, the process is:

**Step 1:** Raise it in writing. An email is best — it's dated and provides a paper trail. Be specific: name the obligation, describe the problem, and ask for a timeline to fix it.

**Step 2:** Give reasonable time. "Reasonable" depends on the urgency — a broken heater in July is more urgent than a loose door handle. The law doesn't specify fixed timeframes for repairs, but 14 days is a common benchmark for non-emergency issues.

**Step 3:** If it's not addressed, apply to the Tenancy Tribunal. The $28 application triggers a free mediation process. If unresolved at mediation, the case goes to a hearing. The Tribunal can issue compliance orders, award rent reductions, and impose exemplary damages.

You don't need a lawyer. Community Law NZ and the Citizens Advice Bureau can help you prepare.
