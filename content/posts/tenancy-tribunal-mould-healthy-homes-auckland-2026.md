A young family, including a newborn, was living in an Auckland, New Zealand rental with mould across the property, rat droppings falling from the ceiling onto their bedding, exposed and damp insulation, and windows that were effectively sealed. Their doctors stated it was not medically appropriate for them to remain there.

Their landlord, Scarlett Hong — a pharmacist — had already been the subject of a Tenancy Tribunal order in November 2024 over water ingress at the same property. She had taken cosmetic action and the problems had persisted. A Healthy Homes Assessment conducted on 25 February 2026 found non-compliance with three standards. The tenants applied to the Tribunal.

In April 2026, Adjudicator Nicole Walker ordered Hong to pay $13,028, according to [RNZ's reporting on the case](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/593451/auckland-landlord-and-pharmacist-scarlett-hong-ordered-to-pay-13k-over-mouldy-rental).

---

## The situation

The property had an active mould problem that predated the tenancy. The tenants documented what they found: mould throughout the flat, damp walls, exposed and deteriorating insulation, and rat droppings contaminating bedding from the ceiling.

The tenants had a newborn. Medical advice was clear that the property was unsafe for their family. They requested the tenancy be terminated and sought compensation.

The key aggravating factor: this was not Hong's first encounter with the Tribunal about this address. A November 2024 order had found water ingress problems. The landlord's response had been cosmetic rather than structural.

---

## What the Tribunal found

The Healthy Homes Assessment conducted on 25 February 2026 documented specific non-compliance:

- Insulation standard — **non-compliant**
- Moisture ingress and drainage standard — **non-compliant**
- Draught stopping standard — **non-compliant**

These are legal obligations under the [Residential Tenancies (Healthy Homes Standards) Regulations 2019](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/). Alongside that, Adjudicator Walker found Hong had failed to maintain the property in reasonable repair — the obligation under [section 45 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html).

The fact that Hong had been placed on notice by the November 2024 order, and had responded only with cosmetic action, mattered. The Tribunal treats a second failure on the same issue as more serious than a first — it is harder for a landlord to argue they didn't know.

Exemplary damages are not awarded for every non-compliance case — they are reserved for conduct the Tribunal considers flagrant. Being warned about a problem in 2024 and taking only cosmetic action before a family with a newborn was living in a mould-contaminated property was, in this case, flagrant enough.

## What renters should take from this

**Prior Tribunal orders are evidence.** If your rental address has an existing order — even from a previous landlord — that's worth knowing. Search the Tribunal database by address at [forms.justice.govt.nz/search/TT](https://forms.justice.govt.nz/search/TT/).

**A Healthy Homes Assessment gives you a formal compliance record.** The February 2026 assessment in this case wasn't just useful — it was the documented foundation of the Tribunal claim. If your landlord has given you a compliance statement you believe is inaccurate, an independent assessment provides evidence that is hard to dismiss.

**Document the timeline.** What made this case stronger wasn't just the mould — it was the sequence: a prior order, cosmetic fixes, and then a formal assessment showing continued non-compliance. Your emails, texts, and photos create the same kind of timeline. Send everything in writing.

**Medical advice counts.** If a GP or specialist states in writing that a property is medically unsafe — because of mould, damp, or inadequate heating — that documentation strengthens a Tribunal application significantly.
