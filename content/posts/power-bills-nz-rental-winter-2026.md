If your power bill made you wince this winter, that's not your imagination. The Electricity Authority says most New Zealand households are paying about **8% more** for electricity this winter, [as reported by RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/640243/most-households-see-an-average-8-percent-increase-in-power-prices-this-winter) in June 2026 — with network charges (the fixed cost of getting power to your door) driving **40–45%** of the increase.

For renters, this stings twice. You pay the bill, but you don't control the building that determines how big it is — the insulation, the heater, the draughts under the door. The good news: NZ tenancy law now puts more of that building on your landlord than most renters realise, and there's real support available on the bill itself. Here's the full picture.

---

## Why Bills Jumped

The short version: lines charges are up (they're 40–45% of this winter's increase, per the [Electricity Authority](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/640243/most-households-see-an-average-8-percent-increase-in-power-prices-this-winter)), and wholesale conditions are tight — less gas, low hydro lakes. [Consumer NZ expects](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/power-prices-predicted-to-surge) lines charges to keep climbing by around $5 a month on average through 2029, so this isn't a one-winter blip.

It's already changing how people live. Consumer NZ's research found **one in five** New Zealanders went to bed early to stay warm last winter, **about one in four** went without heating when it was cold, and **nearly one in five** cut back on food or essentials to pay a power bill.

That last group is who this guide is for. Going cold in a rental is often paying for someone else's compliance failure — so let's sort out whose job is whose.

---

## Who Pays What in a Rental

[Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/utilities-and-other-payments/) draws the lines clearly:

| Cost | Who pays |
|---|---|
| Electricity, gas, internet for your tenancy | You |
| Utilities **shared between different tenancies** (shared meter, shared hot water) | Landlord |
| A working way to heat water and cook | Landlord provides it |
| Gas cylinder + piping where bottled gas is the main supply | Landlord provides; you keep it filled |
| Water | Depends — see our brief on [when landlords can charge for water](/briefs/can-landlord-charge-for-water-nz/) |

The shared-meter row matters more than it looks. If you're in a subdivided house or a backyard unit where your power runs through the main house's meter, that shared supply is the **landlord's responsibility** — an informal "just flick me $50 a week for power" arrangement isn't how it's supposed to work. Anything like that should be in the tenancy agreement in writing, and if you're being billed for a shared supply you never agreed to, put your objection in writing and get advice from [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/).

## Your Flat's Compliance Is a Money Question

The [healthy homes standards](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/) — mandatory for **every** private rental since 1 July 2025 — read like a compliance checklist, but this winter they're better understood as a bill-reduction programme someone else has to pay for:

- **Heating:** your landlord must provide a *fixed* heater capable of holding the main living room at **18°C**. In most cases that's a heat pump — which [EECA](https://www.genless.govt.nz/for-everyone/at-home/improve-energy-efficiency/heat-and-cool-efficiently/) rates as the most energy-efficient, cheapest-to-run space heating available. Running the landlord's heat pump is dramatically cheaper than plugging in your own fan heater to compensate for not having one.
- **Insulation:** ceiling and underfloor insulation to the required standard. Heat that stays in is heat you don't pay for twice.
- **Draught stopping:** no unreasonable gaps or holes letting the warm air out.
- **Ventilation and moisture:** opening windows, extractor fans in kitchen and bathroom, ground moisture barriers. Damp air costs more to heat than dry air — which is why a damp flat is expensive *before* it's mouldy. Our [mould guide](/posts/mould-in-your-auckland-rental/) covers that spiral.

If your rental is missing any of this, you are personally paying the difference, every month, in electricity. That reframes the awkward conversation: asking your landlord for the fixed heater the law requires isn't being difficult — it's declining to subsidise non-compliance out of your own pocket.

Start with our guides on [checking compliance](/posts/is-your-auckland-rental-healthy-homes-compliant/), [heating requirements](/posts/heating-requirements-rental-nz/), and [getting your landlord to fix things](/posts/getting-landlord-to-fix-things-nz/) — the escalation path runs from a written request through to the Tenancy Tribunal ($29 to apply).

## Cutting the Bill Without Freezing

The stuff that's actually in your control, in rough order of payoff:

1. **Check you're on the right plan.** [Powerswitch](https://www.powerswitch.org.nz/) — the free comparison service run by Consumer NZ — takes ten minutes, and checking you're on the cheapest plan for how you actually use power is Consumer NZ's first recommendation. They suggest re-checking after 1 April each year, when retailers reset prices.
2. **Ask about time-of-use plans.** If your retailer offers cheaper off-peak power, shifting the washing, drying, and dishwasher to off-peak hours is free money. (A flat-wide agreement on this is easier than enforcement — see [flatting and bills](/posts/renting-with-flatmates-nz/).)
3. **Use the most efficient heater you have.** If there's a heat pump, use it rather than portable heaters — that's what it's there for.
4. **Dry clothes outside or vented, not on racks in the lounge.** Indoor drying dumps litres of water into your air, and damp air is expensive air.
5. **Do the pre-winter basics** — curtains closed at dusk, furniture off the heat pump's airflow, bathroom fan running. Our [pre-winter rental checklist](/posts/pre-winter-rental-checklist-nz/) has the full list.

## If You're Struggling to Pay

Two things exist specifically for this, and both are underused:

**The Winter Energy Payment.** Paid **automatically** from 1 May to 1 October — no application — to people receiving NZ Super, Veteran's Pension, Jobseeker Support, Sole Parent Support, Supported Living Payment, and other main benefits: **$20.46/week** for singles with no dependent children, **$31.82/week** for couples and people with dependent children, per [Work and Income](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html). It doesn't reduce your other payments. If you're eligible and getting it, budget it against the bill it exists for.

**The Consumer Care Obligations.** Power retailers are bound by the Electricity Authority's [Consumer Care Obligations](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-care-obligations/): if you're having trouble paying, they must work with you on a payment plan, keep fees reasonable, issue late notices and try to contact you before things escalate, and point you to support agencies. The single most useful move is to **call your retailer before the bill is overdue** — the obligations work much better for people who ask early than for people being chased.

If it's beyond a payment plan, [Work and Income](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/) can help with essential costs even if you're not on a benefit, and [Citizens Advice Bureau](https://www.cab.org.nz/) can walk you through what you're eligible for.

## The Renter's Winter Position, Summarised

| Situation | Your position |
|---|---|
| Bill up ~8% like everyone's | Check your plan on Powerswitch; shift use off-peak |
| No fixed heater in the living room | Healthy homes breach — written request, then Tribunal |
| Cold, draughty, damp flat | Compliance question, not a personal failing — pursue it |
| Paying for a shared meter informally | Landlord's cost — object in writing, get advice |
| Can't pay the bill | Call the retailer early; Consumer Care Obligations + WEP + Work and Income |

Cold is a housing-quality problem before it's a personal-budgeting problem. The law finally agrees — make it work for you.
