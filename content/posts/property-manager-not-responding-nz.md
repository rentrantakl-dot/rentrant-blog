You've sent the message. And the one after that. And the call that went to voicemail. And then you sent another message, just to be sure. Weeks later, the same damp patch is on the ceiling, the oven still doesn't work on two burners, or you've been without a functioning heater going into winter.

Unresponsive property managers are one of the most common complaints at the Tenancy Tribunal. And yet most tenants don't escalate — they wait, send another message, and hope something changes.

The problem with that approach is that it doesn't generate the paper trail you'll need if this eventually goes further. Here's what actually works.

---

## What Your Landlord Is Required to Do

Under the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM94278.html), landlords must keep their properties in a reasonable state of repair throughout the tenancy. This isn't just at the start — it's ongoing. They're also required to comply with all building codes applicable at the time the property was built, and since 1 July 2025, to maintain compliance with the [Healthy Homes Standards](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/) covering heating, insulation, ventilation, moisture and drainage, and draught stopping.

These aren't optional. A landlord who lets a property fall below these standards — even gradually, through neglect rather than active choice — is in breach.

The [full breakdown of landlord obligations is here](/posts/landlord-obligations-nz/). For this article, the relevant point is simpler: if something in your rental needs fixing and your landlord is aware of it, they're obligated to act within a reasonable time. The question is what "reasonable time" means.

---

## Timeframes: Urgent vs Non-Urgent

The Act doesn't specify exact timeframes, but Tenancy Tribunal decisions and [Tenancy Services guidance](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/maintenance-and-inspections/repair-and-maintenance/) give a workable picture:

| Repair type | What counts | Reasonable timeframe |
|---|---|---|
| Urgent — health and safety | No hot water, significant heating failure in winter, sewage backup, serious roof leak, electrical hazard, break-in leaving property insecure | 24–48 hours to commence; days at most to resolve |
| Significant — affecting habitability | Broken oven or stovetop, non-functioning bathroom extractor, significant mould requiring treatment, failed locks (non-urgent security) | 7–14 days |
| Non-urgent — maintenance | Minor cosmetic issues, dripping tap, slow-draining sink, minor wear items | 3–4 weeks |

These are guidelines, not hard rules. A Tribunal will look at the specific circumstances: how long the problem has existed, whether the landlord knew about it, and what effect it has had on the tenant's ability to live in the property. A broken oven in summer is less urgent than a broken heater in June — but both have to be fixed.

> **Key point:** For heating failures in winter, the Tenancy Tribunal treats these seriously. New Zealand's Healthy Homes Standards require a fixed heater capable of maintaining at least 18°C in the main living area. If that heater stops working and your landlord doesn't respond, you have grounds to escalate quickly. [More on the heating standard here.](/posts/heating-requirements-rental-nz/)

## Stop Calling, Start Writing

Phone calls are invisible. Conversations in the building car park leave no record. The moment a maintenance dispute becomes serious, the tenant who sent a single email is in a much stronger position than the one who called eight times.

**Switch to written communication immediately.** Email is best — timestamped, retained, and difficult to dispute. If your property manager only communicates via a maintenance portal or app, use that and take screenshots of your submissions and any responses (or lack of them).

A useful maintenance request email:

> **Subject: Maintenance request — [address] — [issue]**
>
> Hi [Property Manager name],
>
> I'm following up on the [describe issue] at [address]. I first reported this on [date] via [call/email/portal]. As at today ([date]), the issue has not been resolved.
>
> The effect on my tenancy is: [describe — no heating in main living area, unable to use kitchen ventilation, etc.].
>
> I'm requesting that this be resolved by [date — give a reasonable deadline based on urgency]. If I don't hear back from you by then, I'll be contacting Tenancy Services for assistance.
>
> Please confirm receipt of this message.
>
> [Your name]
> [Your phone]

Send it. Keep a copy. Note the date you sent it and whether you received any acknowledgement.

## If the Written Request Gets No Response

After your written request and the deadline you gave have both passed without action, you have clear options:

**1. Contact Tenancy Services**

[Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) (part of MBIE) provides free information and assistance to both tenants and landlords. Their advisors can help you understand your rights, what the landlord is required to do, and what remedies are available. They also offer a free mediation service — if your landlord is willing to engage, mediation is faster and less adversarial than a Tribunal hearing, and resolves a significant number of disputes.

Contact Tenancy Services at [tenancy.govt.nz/contact](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/about-tenancy-services/contact-us/) or on 0800 836 262.

**2. Apply to the Tenancy Tribunal**

If mediation isn't suitable or the property manager is simply not engaging, the Tenancy Tribunal is the formal route. It costs $20.44 to apply and you don't need a lawyer. [The full application process is here.](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/)

When applying about a maintenance failure, bring:
- A written timeline of when the issue started, when you reported it, and what happened after
- Copies of all written communications (emails, texts, portal screenshots)
- Photos — dated — showing the problem and its condition over time
- If applicable, evidence of the cost to you: receipts for a portable heater you had to buy, or a record of what you weren't able to use

## What the Tribunal Can Order

The Tenancy Tribunal has real teeth for maintenance disputes. It can:

**Order specific repairs** — the landlord is legally required to carry out the work by a specified date. If they don't, further enforcement action (including fines) follows.

**Order a rent reduction** — covering the period when the property was below the standard it was let at. The amount reflects the diminished value. A property without functional heating in July isn't worth the same weekly rent as one with heating. The Tribunal can award a reduction to reflect that.

**Award exemplary damages** — for serious or repeated breaches. These are punitive, not compensatory, and are reserved for conduct that shows disregard for the tenant's rights. The cap is $7,200 for landlords with fewer than 6 rental properties, or $50,000 for larger landlords — as set under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986 (as amended).

**Issue work orders** — requiring specific work within a timeframe under threat of further action.

## What Not to Do

**Don't withhold rent.** It's not a recognised remedy under the Residential Tenancies Act, and it puts your tenancy in jeopardy. Landlords can issue 14-day notices to remedy for unpaid rent, and a tenant who's withheld rent — even for legitimate reasons — is on weaker ground at a Tribunal hearing than one who paid rent and applied for a reduction.

**Don't arrange repairs yourself and deduct from rent** (unless your tenancy agreement specifically allows this). Some tenancy agreements include a clause permitting this for minor urgent repairs within a cost cap — check yours. Without that clause, self-help repairs and rent deductions are not permitted and could breach your tenancy.

**Don't delay.** Maintenance problems get worse, and the gap between "I told them about this" and "I have a written record showing I told them about this" matters enormously at the Tribunal.

## Working With (or Around) Property Managers

A property manager is not the landlord — they're an agent acting on the landlord's behalf. If a property manager is consistently unresponsive, it's worth asking directly for the landlord's contact details. In some cases, the landlord doesn't know what's happening — the property manager is filtering complaints. Under the Residential Tenancies Act, your legal relationship is with the landlord, and you have the right to contact them directly.

If you're trying to assess a property manager's track record before you sign, [check reviews on RentRant](https://rentrant.nz/locations/search) — responsiveness is one of the aspects that previous tenants specifically rate.
