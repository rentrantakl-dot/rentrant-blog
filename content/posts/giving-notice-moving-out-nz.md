Moving out of a New Zealand rental well is mostly a dates exercise. The [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95514.html) is precise about how much notice you owe, when you can give it, and what happens to rent and bond along the way — and if you get the dates right, everything downstream (the final inspection, the bond refund, the clean exit) tends to follow.

The notice rules changed on **30 January 2025** under the Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024, so plenty of advice floating around — including from people who moved out in 2024 — is now wrong. Here's the current law, tenant's side.

---

## Periodic Tenancy: 21 Days, in Writing

If your tenancy has no fixed end date, you end it by giving **at least 21 days' written notice** — [s51(2A)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95514.html). No reason needed, no fee, no landlord approval. (Before 30 January 2025 this was 28 days — the number you'll still see on older websites.)

Under s51(3), a valid notice must:

- be **in writing** (email counts)
- **identify the premises**
- **specify the date** the tenancy ends
- be **signed** by you (a typed name on an email you sent is fine)

Two rules that save arguments later. First, the **any-day rule** — s51(5): notice can be given on any day of the week and can end on any day. It doesn't need to line up with your rent payment day; you just pay a part-week at the end if the dates don't align. Second, **irrevocability** — s51(6): once given, a notice can only be withdrawn if the landlord agrees. Don't give notice to test the waters.

Count the 21 days from when the landlord **receives** the notice, not when you wrote it. Email with a delivery/read receipt is the cleanest channel.

> **Template:**
>
> Hi [Landlord/Property manager],
>
> This is my notice to end the tenancy at [full address]. My last day of the tenancy will be [date — at least 21 days from today]. I'll return all keys by that date and can be contacted at [phone/email] regarding the final inspection and bond refund.
>
> [Your name], [today's date]

## Fixed-Term: The 90-to-21-Day Window

A fixed-term tenancy longer than 90 days doesn't simply end on its end date. Under [s60A](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM3280851.html), it **automatically becomes a periodic tenancy** on the same terms — unless one of three things happens first:

1. You and the landlord **renew or extend** it,
2. You **agree** it won't continue, or
3. Either party gives **written notice of non-continuation within the "effective period"** — from the **90th day to the 21st day before the expiry date** (s60A(2A)).

The window is the trap. Notice given outside it — too early or too late — doesn't count.

**Worked example.** Your fixed term expires on **1 March 2027**. The effective period runs from **1 December 2026** (90 days before) to **8 February 2027** (21 days before). Any written notice you give in that window ends the tenancy on 1 March 2027. Give it on 20 February 2027 and you've missed the window: the tenancy rolls into a periodic, and you then need a fresh 21-day notice under s51(2A) — which means paying rent into mid-March.

If staying past the end date suits you, doing nothing is a legitimate choice — the rollover to periodic preserves your terms and your bond, and periodic tenancies are more flexible for tenants than fixed terms.

## Leaving a Fixed Term Early

Before the end date, a fixed term can't be ended by notice at all. The honest options, in the order worth trying:

**1. Negotiate a mutual end.** The landlord can agree to release you — get it **in writing, signed by both parties**, with the agreed end date. They may recover their **actual and reasonable costs** of re-letting (advertising, letting fees), but under [s44A](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS451345.html) those costs must be itemised — a round-number "break fee" plucked from the air isn't the standard.

**2. Assign the tenancy.** Under [s43B](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS451316.html) you can transfer the tenancy to a replacement tenant with the landlord's prior written consent — and the landlord **must respond in writing within a reasonable time and must not withhold consent unreasonably**. Finding a good replacement yourself is usually the fastest clean exit.

**3. Apply for a hardship termination.** If an unforeseen change in circumstances means continuing would cause you severe hardship, the Tribunal can reduce or terminate the fixed term under [s66](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95542.html) — weighing your hardship against the landlord's. This is a genuine remedy, not a formality; you'll need evidence.

**4. Family violence withdrawal.** A tenant experiencing family violence can withdraw from the tenancy with at least **2 days' written notice plus qualifying evidence**, in the approved form, without penalty — [s56B](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/LMS533544.html). The tenancy continues for any remaining tenants, with rent reduced by formula for two weeks.

**What just leaving costs.** Abandoning a fixed term exposes you to a Tribunal claim for lost rent until a new tenant is found — though the landlord has a duty to mitigate by genuinely trying to re-let. The full picture is in our brief on [breaking a tenancy agreement early](/briefs/breaking-tenancy-agreement-early-nz/).

## Rent Doesn't Stop When Notice Starts

Rent accrues every day until the tenancy legally ends — whoever gave the notice. Move out on day 10 of your 21-day notice and you still owe the remaining 11 days, unless the landlord re-lets first, in which case your liability ends the day the new tenancy begins. Want the end date brought forward? That needs the landlord's agreement, in writing. The detail is in our brief on [paying rent during the notice period](/briefs/pay-rent-during-notice-period-nz/).

The flip side: once you've given notice, the landlord will likely want to show the property to prospective tenants. Those viewings need your consent — you can't unreasonably refuse, but you can set reasonable conditions. See [viewings during the notice period](/briefs/viewings-during-notice-period-nz/).

If it's the landlord who gave *you* notice, different rules and different rights apply — see our guide to [90-day notices](/posts/90-day-notice-your-rights-and-options/).

## The Moving-Out Fortnight

The last two weeks decide whether your bond comes back whole. Under [s40(1)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95088.html) you must leave the premises **reasonably clean and tidy**, remove your rubbish and belongings, and **return all keys** and security devices. "Reasonably clean" is the legal standard — a blanket professional-cleaning clause generally isn't enforceable, as our [end-of-tenancy cleaning brief](/briefs/end-of-tenancy-cleaning-nz/) explains.

The checklist that protects you:

1. **Book the final inspection** with the landlord for after your things are out, and attend it
2. **Photograph everything on the way out** — every room, wall, carpet, and appliance, dated. Mirror the move-in photos, exactly as our [fair wear and tear guide](/posts/fair-wear-and-tear-nz/) recommends — the pair of photo sets is your evidence if deductions are claimed
3. **Give a forwarding address and bank details** for the bond refund
4. **Complete the bond refund form** — bond is held by Tenancy Services and can only be released by **agreement of both parties or a Tribunal order**. Never sign a blank or partially completed refund form

What deductions are legitimate — and the wear-versus-damage line — is covered in [your bond rights](/posts/your-bond-rights-nz/). And if you're leaving the country, do the bond paperwork **before** you fly: [getting your bond back when leaving New Zealand](/posts/getting-your-bond-back-when-leaving-new-zealand/) is much harder from overseas.

## When It Goes Wrong

If the landlord won't agree to release the bond, claims deductions you dispute, or asserts your notice was invalid, the [Tenancy Tribunal](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/) is the venue. Applying costs **$29**, no lawyer needed, and in bond disputes the burden is on the landlord to prove damage beyond fair wear and tear.

> **Key point:** Most bond disputes are lost at move-in, not move-out. The tenant with dated photos from both ends of the tenancy, a copy of the ingoing condition report, and written notice with a receipt almost always wins the paperwork argument.

|---|---|

| When | What |
