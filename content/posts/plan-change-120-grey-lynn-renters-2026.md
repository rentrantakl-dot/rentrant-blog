There's something slightly surreal about walking past a row of Grey Lynn bungalows and knowing, somewhere in the planning code, that a six-storey building could legally replace them. That's the reality of Plan Change 120 — Auckland's big upzoning push — and it's quietly reshaping what it means to rent in suburbs like Grey Lynn.

This isn't a scare story. Redevelopment happens slowly, and most character streets look much the same as they did five years ago. But for renters in Grey Lynn specifically — particularly long-term tenants in older rental stock — the planning environment creates a real risk worth understanding.

Under New Zealand tenancy law, the same rights apply in an intensification zone as anywhere else. But the economic incentives that redevelopment creates mean those rights get tested differently here.

---

## What Is Plan Change 120?

Plan Change 120 (PC120) is part of Auckland Council's response to national planning legislation requiring cities to allow greater housing density near public transport. In practical terms, it enables significantly taller and denser development — up to **6 storeys** — within "walkable catchments" of key transit stops.

These walkable catchments are roughly circular zones around designated bus and rail stops. The intention is that higher-density housing concentrates where people can walk to frequent transit, reducing car dependence and making better use of infrastructure.

For Grey Lynn, the relevant stops include:
- Key stops along **Ponsonby Road** (Inner Link route)
- Stops along **Great North Road** (serving multiple routes)

This means parts of Grey Lynn — particularly along and near these arterials — fall within zones where six-storey development is now legally permissible.

> **Key point:** Being *in* a PC120 zone doesn't mean your landlord is about to demolish the villa. It means the *potential* for redevelopment exists, which changes the economic calculus for property owners in those areas.

## What "Bungalow Soul" Meets Six-Storey Zoning Looks Like in Practice

Grey Lynn has a strong character identity — 1910s through 1950s villas and bungalows, high ceilings, weatherboards, rimu floors. That character doesn't disappear from a planning map change. But planning maps shape what becomes financially rational for property owners over time.

Here's the tension: a 600m² Grey Lynn site with an older villa generates one tenancy's rent per week. The same site under PC120 zoning could theoretically accommodate a six-storey apartment building with twelve or more units. The math changes the decision-making.

Not every owner acts on that math. Many Grey Lynn landlords are long-term holders who have no intention of selling or redeveloping. But in a market where land values are already elevated and planning permission just got more generous, the incentive structure has shifted.

For renters, this shows up in a few ways:

### The no-cause eviction risk

Since 30 January 2025, the Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024 reintroduced no-cause terminations for periodic tenancies. A landlord can now end a periodic tenancy with **90 days' notice and no stated reason**.

In practice, the reasons landlords do this include selling the property, owner-occupation, and — relevant here — preparing a site for development. PC120 doesn't create new termination rights, but it creates new reasons for landlords to exercise the existing ones.

> **Warning:** If you're renting in a Grey Lynn property that sits on a large site near a bus route, particularly an older villa that hasn't been recently renovated, you're renting in a context where redevelopment is financially plausible. That's not a certainty — but it's worth being aware of when you're deciding whether to put down roots.

### The character home vs. new development divide

The effect of PC120 on Grey Lynn's housing stock is not uniform. Properties that can be cheaply redeveloped (large sites, single older dwellings, no expensive heritage overlays) are more exposed to this pressure. Smaller sites, properties with heritage protection, or those already subdivided may not be economically viable to redevelop under any realistic scenario.

If you're looking at character properties in Grey Lynn, it's worth checking whether the specific site has heritage protections. Auckland Council's [GeoMaps](https://geomaps.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/) tool shows planning zone overlays — search by address.

## The Upside: More Supply Means More Options (Eventually)

Intensification isn't only a story about displacement risk. More housing supply in well-connected suburbs is, over time, a good thing for renters. Grey Lynn's relatively high rents exist partly because supply is constrained — character villas, slow-moving owners, historically restrictive planning rules. PC120 opens the door to more units closer to the city.

That new supply takes time to materialize. A site needs to be assembled, consented, financed, built, and occupied. Most redevelopment that PC120 enables won't complete until 2027 or later at the earliest. But the direction of travel matters.

New-build apartments in Grey Lynn and adjacent suburbs tend to be:
- Better insulated and warmer than character stock
- More likely to be double glazed
- Likely to carry higher rent per square metre
- Smaller (smaller footprint in exchange for higher quality)

They're not the same product as a Grey Lynn villa — but they're a genuine alternative for renters who prioritise warmth and compliance over character and space.

## Retaliatory Termination: The Protection Most Renters Don't Know Exists

One thing worth knowing — especially for renters in older rental stock — is that retaliatory termination protection exists in New Zealand tenancy law.

If a landlord issues a termination notice within a defined period after a tenant has raised a legitimate complaint (requesting repairs, contacting Tenancy Services, etc.), a tenant can challenge that notice at the Tenancy Tribunal on the grounds that it was retaliatory. The burden of proof is on the tenant, but the protection is real.

This matters in the PC120 context because landlords who want to clear a site for redevelopment sometimes do so by issuing termination notices shortly after a maintenance request — technically using the no-cause provision, but in circumstances that may be challengeable.

> **Key point:** If you receive a 90-day termination notice shortly after raising a maintenance complaint, contact [Community Law NZ](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) or [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) before accepting it as final. Retaliatory termination is a specific protection in NZ law.

## What Should Grey Lynn Renters Actually Do?

A few practical steps given this environment:

**Check your lease type.** Periodic tenancies can be ended with 90 days' notice (no-cause). Fixed-term tenancies run to the end of the agreed period — and if a landlord wants to end one early, they need grounds.

**Know the site you're renting on.** A large corner lot near a bus route in the PC120 zone is different from a subdivided villa on a small rear section. Use [Auckland Council GeoMaps](https://geomaps.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/) to check the planning zone for any property you're considering.

**Don't over-index on this risk for most properties.** The majority of Grey Lynn rental properties are rented by owners who have no development ambitions. The risk is real but not universal.

**Document everything from day one.** Regardless of redevelopment risk, thorough documentation of the property's condition at the start of a tenancy protects you on bond. Our [practical renter checklist](/posts/practical-nz-renter-checklist-2026/) covers the specifics.

## The Character Question

There's a broader conversation happening in Grey Lynn about what intensification means for suburb identity. Local groups, heritage advocates, and community organisations have been actively engaged in the PC120 process, with mixed results.

What seems clear: the bungalows and villas that define Grey Lynn's visual identity won't disappear quickly. New development concentrates on sites with strong economics. Many character streets will look similar in 2030 to how they look in 2026. But the margins are moving.

For renters — who can't control planning outcomes — the most useful focus is on understanding what type of tenancy you're in, what your rights are, and who you're renting from. A landlord's long-term intentions for a property aren't always visible at a viewing, but they're the most relevant factor.
