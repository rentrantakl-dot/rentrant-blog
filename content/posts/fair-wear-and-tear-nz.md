The bond dispute is one of the most common ways a tenancy ends badly. You've cleaned the flat, patched what you broke, and handed back the keys — and then the landlord claims the carpet needs replacing, the walls need repainting, and the oven cleaning wasn't up to standard. Suddenly $1,200 of your bond is in dispute.

Some of those claims are legitimate. Many aren't. The difference is the legal standard of fair wear and tear — and whether your landlord can actually prove their case.

This guide covers what the standard means, how the Tenancy Tribunal applies it in practice, and what to do if you think a deduction is unfair.

---

## What Fair Wear and Tear Actually Means

Under the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM94278.html), section 40(1)(d), tenants must not damage a rental property or permit it to be damaged — but the obligation specifically excludes "fair wear and tear." Deterioration from normal, everyday use is the landlord's responsibility to maintain, not the tenant's to pay for at the end.

The practical definition is what a reasonable person would expect a property and its fittings to look like after the tenancy period, given reasonable use. A carpet in a bedroom will flatten and fade over three years — that's expected. A carpet with a wine stain down the hallway is something different.

Several factors influence the assessment:

**Length of tenancy** — more wear is expected over a longer tenancy. Two years of normal use produces more visible deterioration than six months.

**Age of the item at the start** — a 10-year-old carpet that's worn further during a two-year tenancy is not the same as a new carpet damaged in the same period. Landlords cannot charge for full replacement of an old item — they must account for the item's age and remaining useful life.

**Quality of the item** — cheap paint wears faster than quality paint. Budget flooring marks more easily than commercial-grade. If a landlord fitted cheap materials, they can't charge as though they'd installed premium ones.

**Number of occupants** — more people in a property produces more wear. A three-person flat occupying the space for two years will naturally show more wear than a single occupant.

## What Counts as Wear vs What Counts as Damage

This is where most bond disputes live. The following reflects how the Tenancy Tribunal typically approaches common items:

| Item | Fair wear and tear | Damage (chargeable) |
|---|---|---|
| Carpet | Flattened pile, general fading, minor traffic marks | Stains, burns, tears, pet damage |
| Walls / paint | Light scuffs from furniture, fading over time, minor marks near switches | Holes, deep gouges, excessive crayon or writing, heavy staining |
| Curtains / blinds | Fading from sun exposure, minor sagging on rails | Torn, missing, broken mechanism |
| Kitchen bench | Minor surface scratches from normal use | Burns from pots, deep cuts from knives |
| Doors / door handles | General wear, slight warping in humidity | Broken handles, holes in doors, missing fittings |
| Bathroom sealant | Gradual darkening with age | Significant mould from failure to ventilate (this is contested — see below) |
| Light switches / plates | Yellowing or slight discolouration over years | Cracked, broken, missing |
| Oven | General grease buildup from normal cooking | Burnt-on carbon, missing elements, broken seals |
| Gardens | Natural growth and seasonal change | Severe neglect, dead established plantings from lack of water |

**The oven and bathroom mould caveat:** These two generate the most arguments. Ovens are expected to be cleaned to the level they were at when you moved in — but landlords sometimes push for "brand new clean" on a 10-year-old oven, which isn't the standard. The standard is "reasonably clean," not factory condition.

Bathroom mould is trickier. If a bathroom lacks adequate ventilation and mould develops despite the tenant's reasonable efforts to clean it, that's a Healthy Homes compliance issue — the landlord's problem. If a tenant never opened the window or used the extractor fan, and mould has penetrated the sealant and grout as a result, the argument shifts. Tribunal cases go both ways depending on the facts.

## Depreciation: Why Landlords Can't Charge Full Replacement

A landlord cannot charge you the full cost of replacing an item that was already partially into its useful life when you moved in. This is sometimes called the "depreciation principle" and it applies at the Tenancy Tribunal.

**An example:** A carpet has an expected useful life of 10 years. At the start of your tenancy, it was already 7 years old. During your two-year tenancy, it gets a stain from a broken glass. At the end of your tenancy, the landlord wants to charge $1,500 to replace it. But the carpet had 3 years of useful life remaining when you moved in — roughly 30% of its lifespan — so at most they can claim 30% of the replacement cost, which would be $450. They cannot claim the full $1,500.

The Tribunal applies this reasoning to carpet, paint, curtains, and any other item with a finite useful lifespan. The older the item, the less you can be charged even if it was genuinely damaged.

There's no fixed depreciation schedule in NZ law — Tribunal members use judgment — but [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/ending-a-tenancy/damage-or-loss-at-end-of-tenancy/) provides guidance on how claims are assessed, and commonly cited expected lifespans include approximately 10 years for residential carpet and 5–7 years for interior paint.

## Why the Ingoing Condition Report Is Everything

The landlord's ability to claim for damage at the end of a tenancy rests almost entirely on being able to show what the property looked like at the start. Without a dated, detailed ingoing condition report — ideally with photos — they can't prove any deterioration happened during your tenancy.

Under NZ tenancy law, landlords are required to provide tenants with a property inspection report before or at the start of the tenancy, and to give the tenant the opportunity to agree or disagree with its contents. If they didn't do this — or if the report they provided is vague and unphoto'd — their position at Tribunal is significantly weaker.

**What you should do at the start of every tenancy:**

1. Go through the ingoing condition report room by room and mark anything you disagree with
2. Take your own photos of every room, every wall, every carpet, every appliance — date-stamped, covering everything the landlord might later claim was your fault
3. Send those photos to your property manager via email the day you move in, so there's a dated record they received them
4. Keep the ingoing condition report for the entire tenancy

**At the end of the tenancy, do the same.** Walk through and photograph everything. This gives you an independent record of the property's condition when you left, which you can compare against the ingoing report.

The renter who has dated photos from both move-in and move-out is in a much stronger position at Tribunal than one who doesn't.

## If Your Landlord Claims More Than They Should

Bond cannot be released to either party without written agreement or a Tenancy Tribunal order. A landlord who wants to make deductions has to get your agreement — they cannot simply take the money.

If you receive a claim you think is unfair:

**1. Respond in writing, specifically.**

Don't just say "I disagree." Go through each item, note what you believe the correct standard is (wear vs damage), and point to evidence — the ingoing condition report, your photos, the age of the item. Email works — it's dated and documented.

**2. Ask for evidence.**

A landlord claiming $800 for carpet replacement needs to provide a quote or invoice. Claiming without evidence isn't sufficient. Ask for the actual costs documented.

**3. Apply to the Tenancy Tribunal if you can't agree.**

It costs $20.44 and you don't need a lawyer. The Tribunal hears bond disputes regularly — this is exactly what it's for. Bring your photos, the ingoing condition report, and any written communications with the landlord. The burden of proof is on the landlord to show the damage occurred and the cost is reasonable.

## The Practical Summary

| Situation | Who pays |
|---|---|
| Carpet worn flat from 3 years of normal foot traffic | Landlord |
| Carpet stained from a spill the tenant didn't clean | Tenant |
| Paint fading on a 7-year tenancy with 8-year-old paint | Landlord |
| Large hole in wall from a bracket torn out | Tenant |
| Oven grease from normal cooking use | Landlord (normal wear) |
| Oven with burnt-on carbon left for years without cleaning | Tenant |
| Bathroom mould despite adequate ventilation | Landlord (Healthy Homes) |
| Curtains faded from being in a north-facing room | Landlord |
| Broken blind cord from being yanked | Tenant |
| Garden grown over during 18-month tenancy | Depends on tenancy agreement |

This isn't legal advice — edge cases require judgment and the Tribunal looks at specific facts. But these are the kinds of distinctions that determine how bond disputes typically go.
