The text arrives on Tuesday: "We'll pop in Thursday for a quick look." If you rent in New Zealand, that message is not a notice — it's a breach waiting to happen. Section 48 of the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95504.html) sets out exactly when a landlord may enter your home, how much warning they must give, and how often. Most of the folklore renters hear — "24 hours is all they need", "they can inspect whenever they like, it's their house" — is wrong.

This guide covers every lawful entry type, the notice maths for each, the frequency cap, photos and privacy, and what to do when a landlord or property manager just walks in.

---

## The One Rule Everything Hangs Off

Section [48(1)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95504.html) starts from a blanket prohibition: during a tenancy, the landlord **may not enter the premises** except with your consent — given freely, at or immediately before the entry — or under one of the specific exceptions listed in the section.

Ownership does not equal access. The moment a tenancy starts, the legal right to occupy and control the premises sits with the tenant. Everything else in this guide is an exception to that rule, and each exception has its own conditions. A landlord who doesn't meet the conditions is back at the default position: no entry.

A clause in your tenancy agreement saying the landlord "may enter at any time" or "on 24 hours' notice for any reason" doesn't override the Act — section 48 sets the floor, and agreements can't contract below it.

## Every Entry Type and Its Notice Period

Here is the complete statutory map. Every row is what the Act actually says — not the shorthand version that circulates in flat group chats.

| Reason for entry | Notice required | Conditions | Provision |
|---|---|---|---|
| Routine inspection | Written, ≥48 hours and ≤14 days before entry | 8am–7pm; **not more than once in any 4-week period** | [s48(2)(b)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95504.html) |
| Necessary repairs or maintenance | Written, ≥24 hours | 8am–7pm, any day | s48(2)(d) |
| Smoke alarm work | Written, ≥24 hours | 8am–7pm | s48(2)(ca) |
| Healthy homes compliance work | Written, ≥24 hours | 8am–7pm | s48(2)(cb) |
| Emergency (fire, flood, burst pipe) | None | Must be a genuine emergency | s48(2)(a) |
| Showing the property (prospective tenants, buyers, valuer) | No notice period — **prior consent required** | Consent can't be unreasonably withheld; reasonable conditions allowed | s48(3), (3A) |
| Suspected abandonment check | Written, ≥24 hours | Only if rent is at least 14 days in arrears and the landlord has reasonable grounds to believe you've abandoned | s48(2A)–(2B) |

Two things in that table surprise most renters. First, "24 hours' notice" — the number everyone quotes — is the rule for **repairs**, not inspections. Inspections need at least 48 hours. Second, there's a hard frequency cap: one routine inspection per 4-week period, full stop. A landlord who inspects fortnightly is acting unlawfully even with perfect notice every time.

You'll also hear that viewings run on "48 hours' notice, maximum twice a week". That formula appears nowhere in the Act — viewings work on consent, not notice, which matters enough to get its own section below.

## Inspections: The Detail

For a routine inspection to be lawful, all of these must be true:

- The notice is **in writing** — email and text message both count; a verbal mention at the letterbox does not
- It was given **at least 48 hours and no more than 14 days** before the entry
- The entry happens **between 8am and 7pm**
- No other routine inspection has happened in the **past 4 weeks**

The 14-day maximum is easy to miss: a notice given six weeks out is just as invalid as one given the night before. And a vague "we'll be around next week" fails because it doesn't tell you when entry will actually occur.

> **Key point:** A valid inspection notice is not a request for permission — it's notification, and you don't get to veto it. But the notice has to be valid first. An invalid notice binds nobody: you're not required to be home, provide access, or rearrange your week for it.

If the timing genuinely doesn't work — you're on night shifts, sick, or away — most landlords will reschedule when asked in writing. That's negotiation, not law, but it's how the relationship is supposed to work.

## Viewings Are Consent-Based, Not Notice-Based

This is the most widely misunderstood entry type. When a landlord wants to show the property — to prospective tenants near the end of your tenancy, to buyers if the property is for sale, or to a valuer or real estate agent — [s48(3)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95504.html) says they may enter only **with your prior consent**. No amount of notice substitutes for that consent.

The balancing act is in s48(3A): you **may not unreasonably withhold** consent, but you **may impose reasonable conditions**. In practice that means:

- You can require viewings at set times that suit you — say, Tuesday and Saturday between 4pm and 6pm
- You can limit how many sessions per week you'll accept
- You can require the landlord or agent to be present, or require that you be present
- You can decline open homes and insist on accompanied viewings by appointment

What you can't do is refuse everything indefinitely — a blanket "no viewings until I've moved out" would likely be found unreasonable at the Tribunal. The lever you hold is the conditions, and using it in writing ("Happy to allow viewings — here are the times that work") puts you in the strongest position.

## Photos and Your Privacy

Inspection photos are legitimate — they document the property's condition, and they protect you as much as the landlord in a later [bond dispute](/posts/fair-wear-and-tear-nz/). But the [Office of the Privacy Commissioner's rental guidance](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/rental-guidance-for-tenants/) draws real limits under the Privacy Act 2020:

- Photos should capture the **property**, not your personal possessions, where that's avoidable — and never the inside of drawers, wardrobes, or cupboards holding your things
- The landlord must have a **purpose** for the photos, and you have the **right to know** what they'll be used for
- Photographing documents, mail, medication, or anything revealing personal information is off-limits
- If photos end up somewhere they shouldn't — a listing, social media — you can complain to the [Privacy Commissioner](https://www.privacy.org.nz/)

Beyond privacy, there's [section 38](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95082.html): the landlord must not interfere with your reasonable peace, comfort, and privacy — the "quiet enjoyment" obligation. Conduct that amounts to harassment — turning up constantly, photographing you, using entries to intimidate — is an unlawful act in its own right.

## If Your Landlord Just Walks In

Unlawful entry has real consequences, and the escalation ladder is short. Entry without valid notice, outside permitted hours, or without the required consent is an **unlawful act** under s48(4)(a), carrying exemplary damages of up to **$1,500** under [Schedule 1A](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM3285790.html) of the Act. Conduct rising to interference with your privacy or harassment under s38 carries up to **$3,000**. And entry by force or threat of force while you're on the premises is a **criminal offence** under s48(5)–(6) — up to 3 months' imprisonment or a fine of up to $3,000. That last one is a police matter, not a Tribunal one.

Here's the sequence that works:

**1. Object in writing, immediately.** Email is ideal — it's dated and it's evidence.

> **Template:**
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> On [date] you entered the property at [address] without [valid notice / my consent]. Under section 48 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, entry for an inspection requires written notice of at least 48 hours (and no more than 14 days), between 8am and 7pm, and no more than once every 4 weeks. Please confirm in writing that future entries will comply with the Act.
>
> Regards,
> [Your name]

**2. Document everything.** Dates, times, what was said, who was present. If entries keep happening, a contemporaneous log is the difference between an allegation and a finding.

**3. Apply to the Tenancy Tribunal.** It costs **$29**, you don't need a lawyer, and repeated unlawful entry is exactly the kind of case exemplary damages exist for. Our guide to [applying to the Tribunal](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/) walks through the process. If the entries are coming from an unresponsive property manager, see [what to do when your property manager won't respond](/posts/property-manager-not-responding-nz/).

**4. If you're threatened or entry is forced, call the police.** That's the criminal provision, and it's theirs to enforce.

One more protection worth knowing: if you assert these rights and your landlord responds with a termination notice, that notice may be [retaliatory — and the Tribunal can strike it down](/posts/retaliatory-termination-nz/).

## The Practical Summary

| Situation | Your position |
|---|---|
| "Inspecting tomorrow" text sent tonight | Invalid — inspections need ≥48 hours' written notice |
| Inspection notice for 3 weeks from now | Invalid — notice can't be more than 14 days ahead |
| Second routine inspection within a month | Unlawful — one per 4-week period is the cap |
| "We need to fix the hot water cylinder, coming Thursday" (24h+ written) | Valid — repairs need only 24 hours |
| Agent wants Saturday open homes for the sale | Needs your consent — you can require appointment-only viewings at times you set |
| Burst pipe flooding the kitchen | Landlord can enter immediately — genuine emergency |
| Landlord lets themselves in with their key, no notice | Unlawful act — up to $1,500 exemplary damages; document and escalate |
| Landlord photographs inside your wardrobe | Privacy Act issue — complain to the landlord, then the Privacy Commissioner |
