Most people get into their first flat without much of a system. You find a place, you like the people, someone knows the landlord, and by the time you've moved the couch in you've agreed on approximately nothing. That works fine until it doesn't — and when it stops working, it usually stops working badly.

In New Zealand, the way your flat is legally structured at the start determines your options when things change: when someone wants to leave, when someone stops paying their share, when a dispute about bills or damage needs resolving. These things feel remote when you're signing the agreement. They don't feel remote in month six, in winter, when someone's three weeks behind on their rent share and you're the one whose name is on the lease.

This isn't a guide to your legal rights as a flatmate — that's [covered in detail here](/posts/flatting-in-auckland-your-rights-as-a-flatmate/). This is a guide to *setting it up right* before you move in, so you have fewer of those conversations later.

---

## The First Decision: Which Structure?

Before anyone signs anything, you have a structural choice to make. This matters more than people realise.

### Option A: Joint Tenancy

Everyone who's going to live in the flat is named on the tenancy agreement. You all sign. You all have equal rights and equal responsibilities to the landlord.

What this means in practice:
- All flatmates can deal directly with the landlord
- Everyone is equally protected by the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM94278.html)
- Bond goes to [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) in one lump and is held there until the tenancy ends
- Everyone is jointly and severally liable for the rent — if one person doesn't pay, the others are legally responsible for the full amount
- No one can exit without the agreement of all remaining tenants and the landlord

Joint tenancies work well when the group is stable, everyone's equally committed, and you trust each other with the joint liability. They offer the most legal protection for everyone involved.

**The complication:** Changing the flatmate group is not simple. A tenant can only be added or removed with the consent of all existing tenants and the landlord. There's no mechanism to "swap out" a flatmate unilaterally. The [signing your first tenancy agreement guide](/posts/signing-your-first-tenancy-agreement-nz/) covers what the agreement should say and what to push back on before you sign.

### Option B: Head Tenant Arrangement

One person (the "head tenant") signs the tenancy agreement with the landlord. The other flatmates are subtenants — their arrangement is with the head tenant, not with the landlord. The landlord may not even know who the subtenants are.

What this means in practice:
- The head tenant carries full legal responsibility for rent and property condition
- Subtenants have no direct legal relationship with the landlord
- Adding or removing flatmates is simpler — it's the head tenant's arrangement to manage
- Flatmate "bond" paid to the head tenant sits outside Tenancy Services — it's private money with no formal registration or protection

This arrangement is common because it's administratively easier. But it creates an asymmetry: the head tenant carries the full legal risk, and subtenants have significantly less formal protection. If you're the head tenant, you're liable for everything. If you're a subtenant, your protection depends on your relationship with the head tenant, not the law.

> **Warning:** Under section 45 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, subletting without the landlord's written consent is a breach of tenancy. If a head tenant takes in subtenants without informing the landlord and the landlord finds out, the head tenant can be issued a Notice to Remedy or face Tenancy Tribunal action. Sort the subletting arrangement in writing before the new flatmates move in.

**The practical guidance:** If your group is stable and you trust each other, push for a joint tenancy. If the arrangement is more informal — you're filling a room in an existing house, or people are likely to come and go — a head tenant arrangement may be more practical. Just understand the asymmetry before agreeing to it.

## The Flatmate Agreement

Whether you're in a joint tenancy or a head tenant arrangement, a flatmate agreement governs the day-to-day running of the flat. The tenancy agreement covers your obligations to the landlord. The flatmate agreement covers your obligations to each other.

It doesn't need to be witnessed or notarised. It does need to be written down, signed by everyone, and kept somewhere you can all find it. A flatmate agreement is enforceable as a private contract — the Disputes Tribunal can act on it.

**What to cover:**

### Rent

- Each flatmate's weekly share
- When it's due (typically the day before rent is paid to the landlord — to give the paying flatmate time to transfer)
- Which account it goes to
- What the expectation is if someone's late (a brief grace period? immediate notification?)

### Bond

- How much each flatmate contributed to the total bond
- Where the records of contribution are kept
- How refunds will be calculated if someone leaves mid-tenancy
- Who is responsible for which room's condition at the end of the tenancy

### Bills

Utilities are the most common source of ongoing flat conflict, particularly in winter when heating costs spike. The default "split equally" sounds fair and often isn't. Agree before the first bill arrives:

- Which bills are in whose name
- How they're split — equally by flatmate, or proportionally by usage?
- Who tracks, divides, and sends payment requests each month
- Whether heating is treated as a shared cost or each person pays their own area

That last point matters in an Auckland villa in June. A heat pump running in the main living area all evening costs real money. If one flatmate works late and is rarely home, or goes to bed early and turns it off, "split equally" can feel unfair fast. Agree on a heating arrangement now, not after the first $400 power bill lands.

### Cleaning

- Who cleans common areas, and how often
- Whether there's a roster
- What the standard is — not spotless, but the [reasonable clean and tidy standard](/briefs/end-of-tenancy-cleaning-nz/) that will apply at the end of the tenancy applies throughout

### Guests

- Short-term guests are fine and don't require discussion (a night or two)
- A guest staying more than a week: let the flatmates know
- A guest effectively moving in: this creates a subletting situation and needs the landlord's involvement — see above

### Notice to Leave

When someone wants to move out, how much notice do they give the other flatmates? Note that their formal notice obligations depend on the tenancy structure — this is a practical agreement on top of those. Four weeks is common and gives everyone time to find a replacement or make decisions.

## Bond: Getting It Right

This is the area where most flatmate disputes end up. Getting it clear at the start costs nothing. Untangling it afterwards costs time, money, and goodwill.

**In a joint tenancy:** Bond is lodged with Tenancy Services as one total sum. Tenancy Services holds no record of which flatmate contributed what amount. If a flatmate wants to leave mid-tenancy, Tenancy Services cannot refund their individual share — the bond stays until the tenancy ends.

The practical mechanism: a [change of tenant form](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/change-of-tenant/) on tenancy.govt.nz formally substitutes a new flatmate for the departing one, with consent from all parties. The departing flatmate sorts their bond share directly with the incoming flatmate — Tenancy Services doesn't split or transfer it. [This brief covers the mechanics in detail](/briefs/flatmate-moves-out-bond-nz/).

**In a head tenant arrangement:** Any "bond" a flatmate pays to the head tenant is unregistered private money. If a dispute arises about its return, it goes to the Disputes Tribunal, not Tenancy Services. Keep written records of any money exchanged. A message saying "I transferred $800 for bond on [date]" is better than nothing. A signed acknowledgement is better still.

Keep a written record of each flatmate's bond contribution. If the tenancy ends with everyone having looked after their area well, [the bond should come back in full](/posts/your-bond-rights-nz/). The record of who paid what determines how it's divided.

## When Someone Wants to Leave

Someone always wants to leave before the lease ends. Planning for it makes it manageable.

**In a joint tenancy:** All remaining tenants and the landlord must consent to a change of tenant. No single flatmate can exit without everyone's agreement. The landlord cannot unreasonably withhold consent to a suitable replacement, but they can assess the incoming person.

**In a head tenant arrangement:** A subtenant leaving is simpler — they give the agreed notice to the head tenant, sort their bond share, and go. The tenancy with the landlord continues unchanged.

In either case: document the departing flatmate's room condition with photos on their move-out day. Agree what, if anything, is being deducted from their bond contribution — and put it in writing. The [fair wear and tear standard](/briefs/fair-wear-and-tear-nz-rentals/) applies: normal use of a property over time is not damage, and cannot be charged for.

## Disputes Between Flatmates

The Tenancy Tribunal handles disputes between tenants and landlords. It does not handle disputes between flatmates.

If a flatmate takes your bond contribution and refuses to return it, won't pay their share of a bill, or causes damage and won't contribute to repairs — the venue is the [Disputes Tribunal](https://www.disputestribunal.govt.nz/). Filing costs $45 for claims up to $2,000, $90 for claims up to $30,000. The hearings are typically faster than Tenancy Tribunal proceedings.

> **Key point:** The flatmate agreement doesn't need to be formally witnessed to be enforceable as a contract. A signed written document outlining what each party agreed to is enough for the Disputes Tribunal to work with. "We agreed verbally" is much harder to act on.

For serious situations — a flatmate who is threatening, abusive, or genuinely making the flat unsafe — this escalates beyond civil dispute resolution. Contact police if there's a safety issue. For advice on your specific situation, [Community Law NZ](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) or the [Citizens Advice Bureau](https://www.cab.org.nz/) are both free.

## At the End of the Tenancy

Everyone's jointly responsible for the property's end-of-tenancy condition in a joint tenancy. The landlord can claim against any or all named tenants for any shortfall.

Agree in the flatmate agreement who is responsible for which areas. The [end-of-tenancy cleaning standard](/briefs/end-of-tenancy-cleaning-nz/) is "reasonably clean and tidy" — not deep-cleaned, not hotel-standard. What you must return to is the condition the property was in when you moved in, accounting for fair wear and tear.

Take photos when you move in. Take photos when you move out. The [bond rights guide](/posts/your-bond-rights-nz/) covers what can and can't be deducted. The [fair wear and tear brief](/briefs/fair-wear-and-tear-nz-rentals/) covers the distinction between damage and normal use.

The goal is a clean exit where everyone gets their money back and nobody carries resentment into the next flat. It's achievable — it just requires the conversations to happen at the start, not at the end.

Before you sign, [check what previous tenants have said about the property on RentRant](https://rentrant.nz/locations/search) — or leave a rating when you go, so the next group knows what they're getting into.
