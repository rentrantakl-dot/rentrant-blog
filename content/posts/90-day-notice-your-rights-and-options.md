The letter arrives. Or the email. Or the text message.

*We are giving you 90 days' notice to vacate the property.*

No reason. No warning. No context. Just 90 days to find somewhere else to live.

Since 30 January 2025, this is legal in New Zealand. The Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024 reintroduced no-cause terminations for periodic tenancies — a power that was removed in 2021 and has now come back. Tenant advocates and legal commentators have documented a chilling effect: renters who know the threat exists are less willing to raise maintenance requests or Healthy Homes complaints in case it triggers a notice.

This guide explains what the notice actually means, when it can be challenged, and what your realistic options are.

---

## What the 90-day notice actually is

A 90-day termination notice is a landlord's way of ending a periodic tenancy without giving a reason.

Under the Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024 (in force 30 January 2025), a landlord can give 90 days' written notice to terminate a periodic tenancy. They don't have to tell you why. They don't have to show a cause. They just have to give you the notice in writing and count out 90 days from the date it's received.

**Periodic tenancy** means a tenancy that continues week to week or fortnight to fortnight indefinitely — no fixed end date. This is the most common tenancy type in NZ. If your tenancy agreement doesn't specify an end date, you're almost certainly on a periodic tenancy.

**Fixed-term tenancies are different.** If you have a tenancy agreement that specifies an end date (e.g. 1 October 2026), a landlord cannot issue a 90-day no-cause notice to cut it short. They can let it expire at the agreed end date, but they generally can't terminate early without your agreement or a specific ground under the Residential Tenancies Act.

## When landlords can — and can't — issue termination notices

| Notice type | Length | When it applies |
|---|---|---|
| No-cause termination | **90 days** | Any periodic tenancy, no reason required |
| Owner/family moving in | **42 days** | Landlord or their family member genuinely moving in |
| Sale requiring vacant possession | **42 days** | Property sold, buyer requires it vacant |
| Employment housing ends | **42 days** | Tenancy tied to employment, employment has ended |
| Tenant notice to leave | **21 days** | Tenant ending any periodic tenancy |

The 42-day notices require the landlord to state the reason. If they give you 42 days and claim the property is being sold, and it's not actually sold, that is potentially fraudulent. You can check on the title.

The 90-day notice requires no reason at all. That's the crux of it.

> **Key point:** A no-cause termination is legal, but it doesn't mean you have to leave immediately or quietly. There are specific validity requirements the notice must meet, and there are protections against retaliatory use. Read on.

## Check the notice validity first

Before you do anything else, make sure the notice is actually valid. An invalid notice does not end your tenancy.

**The notice must:**
- Be in **writing** (email counts, but verbal notice does not)
- State the **date of termination** clearly
- Give at least **90 days** from the date the notice is received (not sent — received)
- Be for a **periodic tenancy** (not a fixed-term that's still running)

**What to check:**
1. Count the days from when you received it (not when it was written or sent). 90 days minimum.
2. Does it specify a termination date? If it's vague — "approximately three months" — it's defective.
3. Are you on a periodic tenancy? If you're in a fixed-term, the 90-day no-cause power generally doesn't apply.

If the notice doesn't meet these requirements, you can write back to the landlord pointing out the defect. Until a valid notice is served, the tenancy continues as normal.

> **Template:** If you believe the notice is invalid:
>
> *Dear [Landlord/Property Manager],*
>
> *Thank you for your notice dated [date]. After reviewing it, I believe it may not meet the legal requirements under the Residential Tenancies Act.*
>
> *[State the specific issue — e.g., "The notice gives [X] days, which is less than the required 90 days" or "I am on a fixed-term tenancy until [date] and I understand that periodic no-cause terminations do not apply to fixed-term tenancies."]*
>
> *I'd appreciate your clarification on this before taking further steps.*
>
> *Regards, [Your name]*

## Retaliatory termination: the protection you probably don't know exists

The law includes a specific protection against landlords using the 90-day notice as retaliation for tenants who complain about property conditions or assert their rights.

Under the Residential Tenancies Act, if a landlord issues a termination notice and the tenant can show that the notice was issued in response to a legitimate complaint or the exercising of a legal right, the Tenancy Tribunal may treat it as a retaliatory termination — and cancel it.

**What counts as a legitimate complaint or action:**
- Requesting maintenance or repairs
- Raising a Healthy Homes compliance issue (heating, insulation, ventilation, moisture/draught, drainage)
- Contacting the Tenancy Tribunal or Tenancy Services
- Exercising any other right under the tenancy agreement or the Residential Tenancies Act

**The timing matters.** If you made a complaint and a termination notice arrived shortly afterward, that sequence is relevant evidence. Document everything: dates, what was said, method of communication (email, text, verbal).

The challenge is that proving a landlord's motivation is difficult. A landlord will rarely say "I'm giving you notice because you complained about the mould." They'll just give the 90-day notice and say nothing. Establishing that the notice was retaliatory requires you to show the connection between your complaint and the notice — and that's not always easy.

> **Warning:** The burden of demonstrating retaliation lies primarily with the tenant. This is hard. But it is possible, especially where the timing is close (notice issued within weeks of a documented complaint), the property has documented compliance issues, and there's written evidence of the complaint. If this describes your situation, contact [Community Law NZ](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) for free advice before your situation becomes urgent.

If the Tribunal finds the notice was retaliatory, it can:
- Cancel the termination notice
- Award compensation to the tenant
- Issue exemplary damages: up to $7,200 for landlords with fewer than 6 rental properties, and up to $50,000 for larger landlords

## Your options after receiving a valid notice

Assuming the notice is valid and there's no retaliatory termination angle, you have these realistic options.

### Option 1: Accept it and plan your move

Sometimes the right answer is to start looking. Ninety days is enough time to find another property if you begin immediately — don't wait until week eight.

What to do:
- Start searching on Trade Me now
- Give yourself a target of finding something by day 60, so you have a buffer
- If you plan to leave before the 90 days are up, you can give 21 days' notice yourself and leave early — you're not required to stay until day 90
- Ensure your bond is in order and your ingoing inspection report is documented for the outgoing inspection

### Option 2: Negotiate a voluntary extension

There's no legal obligation on the landlord to extend, but many will agree to a short extension if asked professionally. Reasons they might agree:
- Finding a new tenant takes time (advertising, vetting) and costs money
- They may not actually need you out on day 90 and just wanted the option
- A cooperative existing tenant may be preferable to uncertainty about who comes next

Write to the landlord, explain your situation briefly, and ask for a specific extension — say, an extra 30 or 60 days. Don't make it an ultimatum. Frame it as a practical request.

### Option 3: Apply to the Tribunal for retaliatory termination

If you have evidence that the notice was issued in response to a complaint or exercise of your rights, you can apply to the Tenancy Tribunal.

Apply as early as possible — before the termination date. The Tribunal will consider the application and may cancel the notice.

**Application process:**
- Apply via [Tenancy Services — disputes](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/disputes/)
- Filing fee: approximately $20 for most applications
- You'll need to submit your evidence of the retaliatory connection — documented complaints, dates, correspondence
- The hearing may be in-person or by phone

Even if the Tribunal doesn't cancel the notice, it may award compensation if the notice was found to be in bad faith.

## Practical checklist after receiving a 90-day notice

**Immediately:**
- [ ] Check whether your tenancy is periodic or fixed-term
- [ ] Count the days — was the notice received with at least 90 days to go?
- [ ] Is the notice in writing with a clear termination date?
- [ ] Did you recently make a complaint, maintenance request, or Healthy Homes report?

**In the first week:**
- [ ] Document everything — when you received the notice, any recent complaints you made, dates
- [ ] If you believe it's retaliatory, contact Community Law NZ for free advice
- [ ] If the notice is invalid, write back to the landlord identifying the defect

**If accepting:**
- [ ] Begin searching for a new property immediately
- [ ] Confirm your bond is correctly lodged with Tenancy Services
- [ ] Locate your ingoing property inspection report — you'll need it for the outgoing
- [ ] Give 21 days' notice yourself if you find a place before day 90

**At all times:**
- [ ] Continue paying rent — moving out is not your legal obligation until the termination date, but rent continues until you vacate
- [ ] Keep communications in writing (email, not just text where possible)

## One last thing

The 90-day no-cause notice was controversial when it came back in. Tenant advocates argued — and documented — that it silences renters who fear eviction if they complain. That chilling effect is real.

If you've been sitting on a legitimate complaint about your property because you were worried about a notice, this is worth knowing: the law has a retaliatory termination protection precisely because Parliament recognised that landlords might misuse the power. It's imperfect, and proving retaliation is hard. But it's there.
