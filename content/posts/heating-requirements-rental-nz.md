It's a Monday morning in July. Auckland outside is 10°C and damp. Your living room is 13°C because the small wall unit your landlord installed is losing a battle with a draughty 1930s bungalow.

This is not a situation you have to accept. Under New Zealand's Healthy Homes Standards — fully in force since **1 July 2025** — your landlord is legally required to provide a heater capable of keeping your main living area at a liveable temperature. Not just any heater. A properly sized, fixed heater.

Here's what the law requires, what counts as compliant, and what to do if your rental falls short.

---

## The core rule: 18°C in the main living room

The Healthy Homes heating standard requires landlords to install a fixed heater in the main living room that is capable of heating that room to at least **18°C** when the outdoor temperature is 1°C.

The **18°C** threshold comes from the World Health Organisation's recommendation for minimum indoor temperatures for general health. Below 16°C, respiratory health risks increase. The standard applies to the main living area — typically the lounge or open-plan living/kitchen space.

> **Key point:** The standard is about *capability*, not just presence. A heater that's too small to warm the room to 18°C fails the standard, even if it's fixed to the wall.

---

## What counts as a compliant heater

Not all heating systems qualify. The standard is specific.

### Compliant heater types

| Heater type | Compliant? | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Heat pump (reverse cycle) | Yes | Most common compliant option in Auckland |
| Pellet burner | Yes | Must meet NZ Clean Air standards |
| Wood burner | Yes | Must meet NZ Clean Air standards; some Auckland council restrictions apply |
| Flued gas heater | Yes | Flue must vent to outside |
| Fixed electric heater (panel, convector, etc.) | Yes | Only if sized correctly for the room |
| Portable electric heater | **No** | Portable heaters do not meet the standard |
| Unflued gas heater | **No** | Prohibited in living rooms and bedrooms |
| Heat pump in hallway | **No** | Must be in the main living room |

The portable heater rule catches a lot of landlords out. Providing a 2.4kW oil column heater and calling it heating compliance is not good enough — regardless of how powerful it is, a portable heater doesn't satisfy the standard.

Unflued gas heaters — the kind that burn gas and release combustion gases directly into the room — are prohibited under the standard. They produce nitrogen dioxide, which is harmful to breathe. If your rental has one of these in a living room or bedroom, it shouldn't be there.

---

## The sizing requirement: this is where it gets technical

Having the right *type* of heater is necessary but not sufficient. The heater must also be **capable of heating the room to 18°C**. This is calculated based on:

- The floor area of the main living room
- The room's heat loss — factored from ceiling height, insulation level, window type (single vs double glazing), and orientation
- The climate zone of the property

Auckland is in **Climate Zone 1** under the Healthy Homes Standards. MBIE provides a [free heating assessment tool](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/heating-standard/heating-tool/) that calculates the minimum heating capacity (in kilowatts) required for a given room.

**Why this matters:** A property manager who installed the smallest compliant heat pump (say, 2.5kW) in a large open-plan living area with high ceilings and single-glazed windows may have technically installed a heat pump — but if the tool shows the room requires 3.8kW minimum, the property doesn't meet the standard.

> **Tip:** Run the MBIE tool yourself using your living room dimensions and a rough assessment of your insulation level (you'll find ceiling and underfloor insulation details in your Healthy Homes compliance statement, if your landlord has provided one). If the minimum required capacity is higher than the installed heater's output, you have a compliance issue to raise.

## What about heat pumps specifically?

Heat pumps are the dominant heating solution in Auckland rentals — they're efficient, they heat and cool, and they've become the standard retrofit across NZ housing stock. But a few things to know:

**Rated output vs. effective output:** The kW rating on a heat pump is its rated heating output under standard test conditions (usually 7°C outdoor temperature). As outdoor temperatures drop, most heat pumps produce less heat. This is a consideration in Otago or the South Island, but less critical in Auckland where winters rarely see temperatures near 0°C.

**Placement matters:** The heat pump must be in the main living area. A unit installed in a bedroom, hallway, or separate room does not count toward compliance for the main living room.

**Condition matters:** A heat pump that hasn't been serviced in years, has dirty filters, or isn't functioning properly isn't meeting the standard in practice. If your heat pump is running constantly without warming the room, there may be a maintenance issue worth raising.

## How to check your rental's heating compliance

### Step 1: Identify the heater in your main living room

Is there a fixed heater? What type is it? Is it in the living room specifically, or elsewhere?

If there's no fixed heater in your main living area — only portable heaters — the property almost certainly doesn't meet the standard.

### Step 2: Check the compliance statement

Your landlord must provide a Healthy Homes compliance statement within **21 days of your tenancy starting**. This should include details of the heating system — what it is, where it is, and its rated output.

If you haven't received one, request it in writing.

### Step 3: Run the MBIE heating tool

Go to [tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/heating-standard/heating-tool](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/heating-standard/heating-tool/) and enter:
- The floor area of your main living room (measure it if needed — length × width)
- The ceiling height
- The level of ceiling and underfloor insulation (from your compliance statement or your own inspection)
- The number and type of windows (single or double glazed)

The tool will output the minimum heating capacity required. Compare it against the rated output of your installed heater.

### Step 4: Test it practically

On a cold winter night (outdoor temperature below 10°C), set the heater to maximum and leave it running for 30–60 minutes. Can you get the room to 18°C? If not — despite the heater running properly — the installed capacity may genuinely be insufficient.

Document this: note the outdoor temperature, the indoor temperature before and after, and how long you ran the heater.

## What to do if your heating isn't compliant

### Put it in writing

Contact your landlord or property manager in writing.

> **Template:**
>
> Hi [Landlord/Property Manager],
>
> I'm writing about the heating at [address]. I've reviewed the Healthy Homes heating standard and I have concerns about compliance — specifically: [describe the issue, e.g. "there is no fixed heater in the main living room" / "the installed heat pump does not appear to be capable of heating the room to 18°C"].
>
> I've used the MBIE heating assessment tool and the minimum capacity for this room appears to be [X] kW. The installed unit is rated at [Y] kW.
>
> The Healthy Homes heating standard has been fully in effect since 1 July 2025. Could you please let me know how and when you intend to address this?
>
> Cheers,
> [Your name]

### If they don't act

If the landlord won't fix it or disputes your assessment:

1. **Tenancy Services mediation** — free, call 0800 836 262
2. **Tenancy Tribunal** — apply for a compliance order, rent reduction, and exemplary damages

### What the Tribunal can award

- A compliance order requiring installation or upgrade by a specified date
- A **rent reduction** for the period of non-compliance — tenants have received 15-30% reductions in heating-related cases
- **Exemplary damages** of up to $7,200 (landlords with fewer than 6 properties) or up to $50,000 (landlords with 6 or more properties)

## Common landlord excuses — and why they don't fly

**"You can use a portable heater."**

Portable heaters don't meet the standard. The regulation requires a *fixed* heater. This excuse doesn't hold.

**"It's an old house, you can't expect it to heat perfectly."**

The standard applies to all rental properties regardless of age. "It's old" isn't an exemption.

**"The heat pump in the hall heats the whole flat."**

The compliance requirement specifies the *main living room* specifically. A heat pump in a hallway or adjacent room doesn't satisfy the standard for the main living area.

**"It worked fine for the last tenants."**

Irrelevant to whether the property meets the standard. The law applies to your tenancy.

**"Heating is expensive — you should use it efficiently."**

This conflates the standard (capable of reaching 18°C) with the tenant's choice of how much to heat the flat. The landlord's obligation is to provide a compliant heater. What you do with it is up to you.

## A broader picture: heating, insulation, and mould

Heating doesn't exist in isolation. A flat with good heating but poor insulation will burn power trying to maintain comfortable temperatures, and will still have cold surfaces that cause condensation and mould. A flat with great insulation but no adequate fixed heater is warm-ish but still non-compliant.

The Healthy Homes Standards address these together because they work together. If your landlord is focused on only one of the five standards, push for the full picture.
