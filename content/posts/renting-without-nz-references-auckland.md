There's a particular catch-22 facing everyone who moves to New Zealand to study, work, or settle. To get a rental, you need a rental history. To build a rental history, you need to rent. The first tenancy is always the hardest.

This problem isn't unique to NZ — but the way it plays out here has some specific features. NZ landlords, particularly private ones and smaller property managers, rely heavily on references from previous NZ landlords. That's understandable from their perspective. It's also a systematic disadvantage for anyone who's just arrived, and some landlords use it to filter applicants in ways that shade from preference into discrimination.

This guide covers three things: what landlords can and cannot legally ask you during an application, what to actually prepare to give yourself the best chance, and what to do when the answer is no despite a fair application.

---

## What landlords can legally ask for

Rental applications aren't unregulated. Two pieces of law set clear limits on what landlords and property managers can request: the Privacy Act 2020 and the Human Rights Act 1993.

### What is legal to request

**Identity.** Confirming who you are is a legitimate need. A copy of your passport, a NZ driver's licence, or another form of photo ID is standard and reasonable.

**Income and ability to pay.** A landlord needs to know you can meet the rent. Payslips, an employment confirmation letter, bank statements covering your recent income, a student funding letter, or a parental support letter are all legitimate. What's reasonable here depends on context — a bank statement showing three months of income is sensible; asking for your complete financial history going back five years is not.

**Rental references.** This is the one that causes the most friction for newcomers. Landlords can ask for references from previous landlords, and they often weight them heavily. But they cannot require those references to be from NZ. References from landlords in another country are legitimate — a good reference from a private landlord in India, a university in the Philippines, or a property manager in the UK is evidence of your tenancy history, even if it can't be verified by a local phone call.

**Credit check consent.** A landlord may ask you to consent to a credit check. The cost of the check is the landlord's to bear — charging you for it is illegal under the Residential Tenancies Act. If a credit check is run, the results should relate to your financial reliability, not your national origin or ethnicity.

### What is not legal to request

**A photo.** Asking for a photograph as part of a rental application is not permitted. Under the Privacy Act 2020, information collected must be reasonably necessary for the lawful purpose — and your appearance has no bearing on whether you can pay rent or maintain a property. If an application form has a photo field, or if an agent asks you to include a photo with your application, that is a red flag.

**Your nationality or ethnicity.** Under the Human Rights Act 1993, landlords cannot discriminate on the basis of national origin, ethnicity, race, or religion. Directly asking for this information as part of an application is not appropriate, and using it as a selection criterion is unlawful.

**An application fee or credit check fee.** This bears repeating because it still happens. Charging any fee before a tenancy is signed — whether labelled as an application fee, credit check fee, administration charge, or anything else — is explicitly illegal under the Residential Tenancies Act. If you're asked to pay anything before you have a signed tenancy agreement and keys, refuse.

### The grey areas

Some requests are genuinely ambiguous.

**Visa type.** A landlord can legitimately want to know whether you have the right to reside in New Zealand for the duration of the tenancy. Whether this extends to asking the specific category of your visa is contested. Confirming "I have a student visa valid until [date]" is reasonable in context. Being asked to list your full immigration history or disclose visa conditions is not.

**Bank statement detail.** Income verification is legitimate; requiring you to disclose the country of origin of your bank account is not. If a property manager asks for bank statements and then asks follow-up questions about your financial institution's country of origin, that's unusual and potentially improper.

**"Rental CV" formats.** Some Auckland property managers have circulated "rental CV" templates that request photo, visa status, and extensive personal information beyond what's needed. These formats sit in legal grey territory. You are not obligated to complete sections that ask for information you're not required to provide — and a PM who insists on a photo is telling you something about their selection process.

## Building your application without NZ history

The legal limits matter — but the more practical question is how to make your application as strong as possible when you don't have a NZ reference to put at the top of the page.

### Your overseas references

Prepare your overseas rental references before you start applying. What makes a useful overseas reference:

- The landlord's or property manager's name, contact details (phone and email), and the address of the property
- Dates of your tenancy
- A brief statement of your tenancy record — on-time rent payments, property condition on departure, whether they'd rent to you again
- If the reference is not in English, provide a translation

Contact your previous landlords before you leave your home country if possible. A reference that can be followed up by email — even internationally — is far more useful than one with a phone number only a different time zone can reach.

### Financial evidence

Strong financial documentation can partly compensate for the absence of NZ history. What helps:

- Bank statements showing consistent income or adequate savings — aim for three months of statements as a minimum
- An employment confirmation letter from your NZ employer on company letterhead, stating your role, start date, and salary. This is the single most powerful document for people who've secured a job before arriving.
- A student enrolment confirmation from your institution, with your course details and duration
- If your funding comes from a scholarship, sponsor, or family support, a letter from that source confirming the amount and duration

The goal is to demonstrate three things clearly: you can pay the rent, you have reliable ongoing income or funding, and you intend to stay for the duration of the tenancy. Evidence on all three reduces the risk a landlord is taking on an applicant they can't verify through a NZ landlord reference.

### A NZ-based guarantor

If you have a family member, friend, or trusted contact already living in New Zealand who is willing to act as a guarantor, this significantly changes your application. A guarantor means someone with NZ financial standing has agreed to be responsible for unpaid rent or damage if you default. Not every landlord will ask for one, but offering one proactively demonstrates seriousness.

A guarantor arrangement should be documented — a short letter with the guarantor's name, contact details, and confirmation of what they're agreeing to guarantee.

### University accommodation as a stepping stone

For students specifically: a year in university halls gives you a NZ institutional landlord reference for your next application. The university is accountable and easy to verify, and a clean reference from their accommodation services is trusted by private landlords. If you can get into university accommodation for your first year, use it strategically — leave in good standing and get a written reference for use in the private market.

## Writing your application cover letter

Most rental applications ask for a cover letter or "about me" section. Most applicants write something vague. If you're competing without NZ history, a clear, direct letter that addresses the reference gap head-on works better than hoping the landlord doesn't notice.

What to cover:

> **Example cover letter:**
>
> *Hi [Property Manager],*
>
> *I'm applying for the property at [property address]. I'm a [student at the University of Auckland / AUT / Massey Albany / software engineer at X / registered nurse] arriving in Auckland in [month].*
>
> *I don't have a NZ rental reference yet — this will be my first tenancy in New Zealand. I've included references from my previous landlord in [country], who I rented from for [X] years. You're welcome to contact them by email; they're available at [email address].*
>
> *My income is [employment / scholarship / parental support], and I've included [payslips / employment letter / bank statements] to confirm this. I'm looking for [length] of tenancy and am available to inspect at your convenience.*
>
> *I'm happy to answer any questions about my situation.*
>
> *Cheers, [Name]*

The tone is direct and practical. It doesn't over-explain or apologise. It addresses the reference situation honestly and redirects to the evidence that does exist.

## For students: what university enrolment adds to your application

If you're coming to study at the University of Auckland, AUT, Massey, MIT, Unitec, or another institution, your enrolment confirmation is a meaningful piece of supporting evidence — and many landlords underestimate its value.

An enrolment letter demonstrates:
- You have a confirmed reason to be in Auckland for a specific duration
- Your institution has vetted you to some degree
- You intend to stay for the length of the tenancy (unlike short-term visitors)

Pair it with proof of your funding source (student loan, scholarship letter, or a parental support letter) and you've addressed the two questions any landlord is really asking: *Can you pay? Will you stay?*

The platform is still building its database — not every address will have ratings yet — but where data exists, structured ratings on heating, maintenance responsiveness, moisture, and noise tell you things no listing photo can.

## When the answer keeps being no

If you're being consistently declined and you believe it's connected to your national origin rather than your application quality, that's discrimination — which is illegal under the Human Rights Act 1993.

Proving it is hard. A landlord who is discriminating won't say so. They'll say the property was taken, or that another applicant was preferred, or give no reason. Without evidence of explicit discrimination — a message, a statement, a pattern — filing a complaint is difficult.

That said, the complaint pathway exists. The [Human Rights Commission](https://www.hrc.co.nz/) handles complaints about discrimination in housing. Complaints are free to file. Resolution through the Commission is typically through mediation, not punishment, but a documented complaint creates a record.

More practically: some landlords and areas will be less receptive to your application than others. Larger property management companies operating at scale tend to have more standardised selection processes that are less open to individual bias. Private landlords vary enormously. If you're experiencing consistent rejection in one area or with one type of landlord, broadening your search is often more productive than persisting with the same approach.
