New Zealand changed the rules on small dwellings this year, and renters are about to feel it. Since **15 January 2026**, a standalone dwelling of up to **70 square metres** can be built without a building consent — the [granny flat exemption](https://www.building.govt.nz/about-building-performance/all-news-and-updates/granny-flats-building-consent-exemption-set-for-early-2026) under the Building Act 2004. [MBIE expects](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/granny-flats-exemption-in-place-from-early-2026) the change to increase housing supply, and it's explicit that these units can be rented out — no family connection required.

That means a wave of backyard flats is heading for the rental market from this year on. Some will be warm, code-compliant little homes. Some will be the same old damp sleepout with a new coat of paint and a confident listing. This guide is how to tell the difference — and what your rights are once you're in one.

---

## What Actually Changed in January

Under the Building and Construction (Small Stand-alone Dwellings) Amendment Act 2025, a granny flat up to 70m² doesn't need a building consent — **if all the conditions are met**. Per [Building Performance's guidance](https://www.building.govt.nz/projects-and-consents/planning-a-successful-build/scope-and-design/check-if-you-need-consents/building-work-that-doesnt-need-a-building-consent/granny-flats-exemption-guidance-and-resources), that means:

| Condition | What it requires |
|---|---|
| Simple design | Single standalone dwelling meeting the design requirements in the Building Act's new Schedule 1A |
| Building Code | The dwelling must still fully comply — the exemption removes the consent, not the standards |
| Licensed professionals | Work carried out or supervised by licensed building practitioners; registered professionals for plumbing and drainlaying |
| Council notification | The owner must notify the council **before** starting work and **again on completion**, with records of work |

Alongside it, a national environmental standard removed the resource consent requirement for one granny flat per residential or rural site at the end of 2025, per [MBIE](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/granny-flats-exemption-in-place-from-early-2026).

**Key point:** "No consent needed" does not mean "no rules." A lawful 2026 granny flat has a paper trail — council notifications and records of work — and meets the Building Code. That paper trail is exactly what you can ask about as a prospective tenant.

## Your Rights Are the Same as in Any Rental

Here's the part landlords sometimes get wrong, occasionally on purpose: a granny flat rented to you as your own home is a **standard tenancy** under the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/who-is-protected/who-tenancy-law-protects/). The owner living twenty metres away in the front house changes nothing legally. You're entitled to:

- A **written tenancy agreement**
- A bond of no more than **4 weeks' rent**, lodged with Tenancy Services within **23 working days** — see [your bond rights](/posts/your-bond-rights-nz/)
- **Quiet enjoyment** — the owner can't wander in because it's "their backyard"; normal [entry and inspection rules](/posts/rental-inspections-nz/) apply
- Standard notice: you give **21 days** on a periodic tenancy; the landlord needs **90 days** (no cause) or **42 days** (specific grounds) — the [tenant rights overview](/posts/tenant-rights-nz/) covers the full picture

The distinction that actually changes your rights isn't the building — it's the arrangement. If you rent a *room in the owner's own home*, you're a flatmate-style occupant and most of the Act doesn't apply. A self-contained dwelling of your own, even a small one behind the main house, is a tenancy. If you're not sure which side of the line you're on, our [flatmates guide](/posts/flatting-in-auckland-your-rights-as-a-flatmate/) walks through it.

## Healthy Homes: Small Building, Same Standards

Every private rental in New Zealand has had to meet the [healthy homes standards](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/) in full since **1 July 2025** — heating, insulation, ventilation, moisture and drainage, draught stopping. A granny flat is not exempt because it's small, new, or out the back.

In practice, a genuine 2026-exemption build should clear the bar easily — Building Code compliance gets you modern insulation, and a 70m² dwelling needs only a modest fixed heater to hit the 18°C living-room requirement. The units to watch are conversions: the sleepout that grew a kitchenette, the garage with a shower bolted on. Those often fail on insulation, drainage, or ventilation — and they're where the "is this even legal?" question gets serious.

Your tenancy agreement must include a **healthy homes compliance statement**. If it's missing, or you doubt the claims in it, our guide to [checking healthy homes compliance](/posts/is-your-auckland-rental-healthy-homes-compliant/) covers how to verify and what to do next.

## Lawful or Unlawful? The Question That Matters Most

This is the load-bearing check. Tenancy Services [specifically lists](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/who-is-protected/who-tenancy-law-protects/) garages and minor dwellings among premises that can be **unlawful to live in** — because of unconsented building work, because the space was never consented for residential use, or because the council has deemed it insanitary.

For a granny flat, there are now two lawful routes:

1. **Built under the 2026 exemption** — council notified before and after, records of work filed, Building Code met
2. **Built with an ordinary building consent** — the pre-2026 route, with a code compliance certificate

A unit that took neither route is a risk. Before signing, ask the landlord directly: *was this built under the granny flat exemption or with a consent?* A landlord with a lawful unit can answer in one sentence and show the paperwork. You can also ask the council what's on record for the address — a LIM or a simple property-file enquiry will show consents and notifications.

**Key point:** If you're already living in one and it turns out to be unlawful, you don't lose your rights — the law was changed to stop exactly that. The [Tenancy Tribunal has full jurisdiction](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/who-is-protected/who-tenancy-law-protects/) over unlawful residential premises, and if the premises were unlawful at the start of the tenancy and still are, you can end the tenancy with just **2 days' notice**. Renting out an unlawful dwelling is the landlord's problem to answer for at the Tribunal — not yours.

## Before You Sign: The Granny Flat Checklist

- [ ] **The lawful-build question answered** — exemption paperwork or building consent, in one sentence, ideally sighted
- [ ] **Written tenancy agreement** with a healthy homes compliance statement — see [signing your first tenancy agreement](/posts/signing-your-first-tenancy-agreement-nz/)
- [ ] **Power metering clarified** — is there a separate meter, or is it shared with the main house? Utilities shared between different tenancies are the [landlord's cost](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/utilities-and-other-payments/), and any power-sharing arrangement should be in the agreement in writing, not a vibe
- [ ] **Water arrangements in writing** — same logic
- [ ] **Bond lodgement confirmed** — you should get Tenancy Services confirmation within a few weeks of paying
- [ ] **Fixed heater in the living area** — not a promise of one, an actual installed one

Twenty minutes of questions before signing beats six cold months of finding out the hard way.

## What This Means for the Market

More small rentals are coming — that's the policy's whole point, and for renters it's mostly good news: more supply at the cheaper end, and new stock that has to meet modern code. The catch is that the exemption also lowers the barrier for cutting corners, and the enforcement moment has moved from "council inspects before anyone moves in" to "tenant asks questions afterwards."

Which makes the asking part your job now. Do it before you sign.
