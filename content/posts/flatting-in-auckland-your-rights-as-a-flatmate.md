Most students and young people in Auckland start their rental life as a flatmate — moving into an existing household, finding a room through Flatmates.co.nz, or joining a house someone else set up. It's cheaper, it's quicker, and it feels lower-stakes than signing a full lease.

The lower-stakes feeling is real in some ways and misleading in others. You're not responsible for the full rent if something goes wrong. But you also have fewer formal protections than someone named on the tenancy agreement, and the protections you do have work differently.

This matters most in two situations: when you pay "bond" to a head tenant and later want it back, and when the head tenant leaves, defaults, or the living arrangement falls apart. Both are common. Neither is well explained.

---

## The legal distinction: tenants versus flatmates

To understand your situation, you need to understand one piece of NZ law.

The [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM94278.html) gives rights and obligations to **tenants** — the people named on the tenancy agreement. If your name is on the agreement signed with the landlord, you're a tenant. If it's not, you're not — regardless of how long you've lived there, how much you pay, or what anyone calls the arrangement.

As a flatmate not on the tenancy agreement, you are legally a **subtenant** (or in some arrangements, a **licensee**). Your legal relationship is with the **head tenant**, not with the landlord. The Residential Tenancies Act doesn't directly govern your rights in the way it would if you were on the agreement.

This distinction affects three things above all:

1. **Your bond** — if you paid it to the head tenant, it's not in the Tenancy Services system
2. **Dispute resolution** — flatmate disputes go to the Disputes Tribunal, not the Tenancy Tribunal
3. **Security of tenure** — your right to stay is conditional on the head tenant's continued tenancy

None of this means you have no protection. It means your protection works differently, and you need to know where it comes from.

## Subletting requires the landlord's consent

Under section 45 of the Residential Tenancies Act, a tenant cannot sublet the whole or any part of the premises without the **landlord's written consent**, unless the tenancy agreement specifically permits it.

What this means in practice:

- The head tenant should have permission from the landlord before bringing in flatmates
- If they do it without permission, they're in breach of the tenancy agreement
- If the landlord finds out and the head tenant can't remedy the situation, this could lead to the tenancy being terminated — taking all flatmates with it

When you move into a flat, ask the head tenant directly: *Does the landlord know you're subletting rooms?* You can also ask to see the tenancy agreement to check whether there are any restrictions on how many people can live in the property.

If the landlord has given consent, and that consent is in writing (even just an email), that's the proper situation. Your arrangement is above board.

> **Key point:** You're not responsible for getting the landlord's consent — that's the head tenant's job. But you are the one who'd lose their housing if it turns out the sublet was never approved and the landlord terminates the tenancy. Ask before you move in.

## The bond problem

This is the most common source of grief for flatmates, and it's largely invisible until the moment you move out.

When a head tenant collects money from flatmates as "bond," that money is **not lodged with Tenancy Services**. The head tenant has no legal obligation to lodge it — it's a private arrangement between the head tenant and each flatmate. The money typically sits in the head tenant's bank account or under their personal management.

This has several consequences:

**It's not verified or protected.** You can't check your "bond" status on the Tenancy Services website because it's not registered there. You're relying on the head tenant's good faith to keep it separate and return it when you leave.

**Disputes go to the Disputes Tribunal.** If the head tenant refuses to return your bond at the end of your stay, the Tenancy Tribunal has no jurisdiction over this. Your dispute is between you and the head tenant — a private civil matter — and it goes to the [Disputes Tribunal](https://www.disputestribunal.govt.nz/), which handles civil claims between private parties up to $30,000 (or $50,000 with mutual agreement).

**Claims require evidence.** To recover your bond at the Disputes Tribunal, you need to show you paid it, the amount, and that the head tenant has no legitimate basis to withhold it. A written record of payment — a bank transfer with a description, a receipt, or an agreed flatmate document — is essential.

### What to do when you move in

Before you pay any money to a head tenant:

- Get something in writing confirming the amount, what it's for, and the conditions for return. An email exchange is fine.
- Pay by bank transfer, not cash — your bank statement is then your payment receipt.
- Agree in writing what the bond can be used for (unpaid rent, damage you caused) and what it cannot.

A short written flatmate agreement (more on this below) can cover all of this.

## What protections you DO have as a flatmate

Less than a named tenant, but not zero.

**You have a right to quiet enjoyment.** The head tenant cannot harass you, enter your room without reasonable notice, or physically remove your belongings. These are fundamental rights that apply even in informal arrangements.

**You have consumer law protections.** If the head tenant collects money from you under false pretences, or refuses to return your bond without cause, the Disputes Tribunal can order repayment. This isn't fast or easy, but it's real.

**Human rights protections apply.** The Human Rights Act 1993 covers discrimination in housing and accommodation — this applies to who a head tenant chooses to let into the flat, not just to direct landlord-tenant relationships.

**You have protections against illegal conduct.** A head tenant cannot physically force you out of the property without a proper legal process. If this happens, it's potentially assault or unlawful eviction — a matter for the police and potentially a civil claim.

---

## What you DON'T have as a flatmate

**You don't have direct rights against the landlord.** If the landlord enters the property without proper notice, your recourse runs through the head tenant, not directly to the Tenancy Tribunal (unless you're also named on the agreement).

**You can't stop the tenancy from ending.** If the head tenant chooses to leave, is evicted, or voluntarily gives notice, the tenancy ends — and your right to be in the property ends with it. You have no independent right of occupancy once the head tenant's tenancy is over, even if you've paid your share of rent faithfully.

**Your "bond" isn't protected.** Covered above, but worth restating. If the head tenant goes through financial difficulty and your "bond" money is gone, the Disputes Tribunal can order repayment — but ordering repayment from someone with no money doesn't recover it.

**The Tenancy Tribunal isn't your tribunal.** Any dispute that's between you and the head tenant (not you and the landlord) goes to the Disputes Tribunal. Many flatmates go to the Tenancy Tribunal first and find out the hard way that it has no jurisdiction over their claim.

## If the head tenant leaves

This is a common and stressful situation. The head tenant is moving out — they're going travelling, they've broken up with someone, they got a job elsewhere. What happens to you?

**Option 1: Replace the head tenant.** The remaining flatmates can ask the landlord if they can add a new person to the tenancy agreement and have the departing head tenant removed. The landlord has to agree to this change. If they do, you get a new head tenant and the arrangement continues.

**Option 2: All remaining tenants are added to the agreement.** This is the cleanest option if flatmates want to take over the tenancy. Everyone becomes a named tenant with direct rights and obligations. The landlord has to agree.

**Option 3: The landlord finds new tenants.** If the head tenant leaves without replacing themselves and the landlord doesn't want the existing flatmates to continue, the landlord can end the tenancy and begin a new one with new tenants.

**Option 4: You negotiate your own exit.** If the situation is uncertain, it may be simpler to negotiate your departure and move on. Give the head tenant proper notice (whatever you agreed in your flatmate arrangement), recover your bond, and find a new place.

The common thread: everything depends on the landlord's agreement. The landlord has the power here because the tenancy agreement is their contract. Approaching the landlord professionally and early — before things become adversarial — is almost always the right move.

## Protecting yourself: the flatmate agreement

A written flatmate agreement isn't legally required, but it's one of the most practical things you can do to protect yourself in a shared living situation.

It doesn't need to be formal or long. An email agreed by all parties works. What to cover:

- Each person's contribution to rent and bills
- How the "bond" is managed — amount, conditions for return, who decides on deductions
- Notice periods for leaving (typically 3–4 weeks between flatmates, not the 21-day RTA notice that applies to the head tenant)
- How decisions about new flatmates are made
- Rules for common areas, overnight guests, and the things that actually cause conflict

Tenancy Services has [guidance on flatmate agreements](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/flatmates/) and a template you can adapt.

---

## Getting your name on the agreement

The cleanest solution to most of the risks described above is to be named on the tenancy agreement yourself.

If you're moving into a flat as a flatmate, you can ask whether it's possible to be added to the tenancy agreement. Whether this happens depends on:
- The landlord agreeing to add you
- The head tenant being willing to have co-tenants rather than subtenants

A co-tenant on the same agreement has direct rights and obligations with the landlord. You can go to the Tenancy Tribunal directly. Your bond is registered with Tenancy Services. The landlord cannot end the tenancy without giving you notice.

Not every landlord will agree, and some head tenants prefer the arrangement as-is. But if the opportunity is there, it's worth asking.

## Moving into a flat: what to check before you commit

- [ ] Is the landlord aware of and consenting to flatmates in this property?
- [ ] Is the head tenant happy to show you the tenancy agreement (or at least the key terms — rent, term, any restrictions)?
- [ ] Have you agreed in writing on the bond amount, payment method, and return conditions?
- [ ] Have you agreed on a notice period for leaving?
- [ ] Do you know who to contact if something goes wrong with the property (the head tenant manages this with the landlord)?
- [ ] Is there a flatmate agreement covering the key practical arrangements?
