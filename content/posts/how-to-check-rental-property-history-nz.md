The listing looked fine. The viewing went well. The landlord seemed reasonable. And now you're being asked to sign a lease on a property you've spent maybe 30 minutes inside.

In New Zealand, there's more you can check about a rental property before signing than most renters realise. Some of it is free, some costs a bit of money, and some things you simply cannot find out — the law doesn't require disclosure. Knowing which is which lets you spend your time on the checks that actually tell you something.

This guide covers the property, not the landlord. If you want to research who you're renting from, see [How to Check a Landlord Before You Sign](/posts/how-to-check-a-landlord-nz/).

---

## Tenancy Tribunal Records — Search by Address

The Tribunal publishes its decisions publicly. Most renters know you can search by landlord name — but you can also search by **property address**, which gives you a different slice of information: what happened at this specific flat, regardless of who owned it at the time.

Go to **[forms.justice.govt.nz/search/TT](https://forms.justice.govt.nz/search/TT/)** and enter the property address.

What you might find:
- A mould or maintenance dispute a previous tenant brought against an older landlord
- A bond dispute that reveals what condition the property was left in
- Healthy Homes non-compliance cases — useful for knowing which specific standards have been an issue at this address

### The 3-year window

The online database covers approximately the last three years of published decisions. This is a meaningful limitation — a property with a long history of disputes that happened before that window won't show up online.

Older records are available from the District Court where the hearing took place, typically for a search fee plus a per-page copying charge. You'd need to know roughly when the case occurred and which court had jurisdiction. For most renters, the online search is enough — but if you have a specific suspicion about an older issue, it's worth knowing the option exists.

## LIM Reports: What Councils Actually Know

A Land Information Memorandum (LIM) is a summary of all the information a council holds about a property. Issued under section 44A of the Local Government Official Information and Meetings Act 1987, LIMs are ordered directly from the council.

### Who can order one

Anyone. LIMs are not restricted to property owners or buyers. A prospective tenant can order a LIM for any property.

### What a LIM contains

| Category | What's included |
|---|---|
| Building consents | What building work has been done, whether it was consented and signed off |
| Resource consents | Any use consents, zone conditions, notified activity |
| Natural hazards | Flooding, landslides, liquefaction, coastal erosion |
| Leaky building claims | WHRS claims, disclosed under s.124 of the WHRS Act 2006 |
| Rates | Outstanding rates (relevant if ownership is uncertain) |
| Special features | Protected trees, heritage status, drainage easements |

### Natural hazard update: October 2025

In October 2025, Auckland Council [updated how natural hazard information appears on LIMs](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/media-centre/2025/october/changes-to-natural-hazard-information-on-your-property-lim/) — linking directly to the Flood Viewer, landslide susceptibility maps, and GeoMaps rather than embedding the data directly. The intent was to make hazard information clearer and easier to navigate. If you're looking at a property in a low-lying area, a flood-prone catchment, or anywhere near the coast or steep terrain, the LIM is now a more useful starting point than it was before.

### What a LIM does NOT contain

Tenancy Tribunal records are not held by councils — the Tribunal is a justice system record. And a LIM won't tell you about meth contamination unless the council has independently recorded something about it (rare).

### Cost and timing

| Council | Standard LIM | Urgent LIM |
|---|---|---|
| Auckland Council | ~$375 (10 working days) | ~$506 (3 working days) |
| Christchurch City Council | ~$290 (10 working days) | ~$390 (5 working days) |

*Fees as of 2025/2026; verify at your council's website before ordering.*

### Is a LIM worth ordering as a renter?

Honestly — it depends. $375 is real money on a rental budget, and many of the things a LIM covers matter more for buyers than renters. But a LIM is worth it if:
- You're signing a long-term tenancy and want to understand the flood or hazard risk
- The property looks like it could be a leaky building (monolithic cladding, flat or low-pitch roof, 1990s–2000s construction)
- You're considering exercising a right of first refusal to buy
- Something felt off at the viewing and you want a fuller picture before committing

If the LIM is out of budget, the Auckland Council's Flood Viewer (below) gives you the most critical piece for free.

## Methamphetamine Contamination: the Honest Gap

Here's what the law doesn't give you: a public record of meth contamination.

There is no NZ database of which properties have been contaminated, tested, or remediated. And as of the [Residential Tenancies (Managing Methamphetamine Contamination) Regulations 2026](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/properties-affected-by-meth/new-meth-regulations-as-of-april-2026/), which came into force 16 April 2026, landlords are required to:
- Notify existing tenants of any contamination test results within 7 days of receiving them
- Maintain the property below the contamination threshold of **15 µg/100cm²**

What the regulations do **not** require: disclosure to a new prospective tenant before signing. If a property was contaminated, remediated, and re-tested before your tenancy started, there is currently no legal obligation to tell you that history.

### What you can do

- **Ask the landlord directly.** "Has this property ever been tested for meth contamination? Can you share the results?" A landlord who has nothing to hide generally has no problem answering this.
- **Check the meth brief on RentRant** — previous tenants sometimes flag contamination issues in reviews.
- **Self-test during the tenancy.** Once you're living there, you can conduct a screening assessment using consumer testing kits available from hardware stores. If any room tests above 15 µg/100cm², the regulations are triggered and the landlord must act.

See the [meth contamination brief](/briefs/meth-contamination-rules-nz-2026/) for the full picture on what the April 2026 rules require.

## Leaky Building History

Properties built roughly between 1994 and 2004 — often with monolithic (plaster) cladding, low-pitch roofs, and inadequately sealed window and door penetrations — were disproportionately affected by water ingress. The resulting legal claims went through the Weathertight Homes Resolution Service (WHRS), administered under the Weathertight Homes Resolution Services Act 2006.

WHRS claims can no longer be lodged — the deadline was December 2021. But the historical claims are still relevant.

### How to find out

**Check the LIM.** Under section 124 of the WHRS Act 2006, councils must disclose WHRS claims on LIM reports. If a claim was ever made for the property — whether it resolved, settled, or was withdrawn — it will appear there.

You can also contact MBIE directly (Official Information Act request or an inquiry form) to ask whether a specific property has or had a WHRS claim. This is slower but available without ordering a full LIM.

### What a WHRS claim actually means

A claim on a LIM doesn't mean the building is still leaky. It means a claim was made — the property may have been fully remediated since. But it's a flag that tells you: the construction type is one that had water ingress problems. Always combine this with a physical inspection if you're seriously concerned.

If the property has plaster cladding, look carefully at the junction between the cladding and window frames, around penetrations (pipes, meter boxes), and at the base of walls near the ground. Water damage shows up as staining, bubbling paint, or soft areas.

## Flood and Natural Hazard Risk: Free Tools

If you're looking at a property in a low-lying area or anywhere near a stream or coastline, you don't need to order a LIM to assess flood risk.

**Auckland Council Flood Viewer** — searchable free by address at [gis.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz](https://geomaps.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/) — shows:
- 1% AEP (1-in-100 year) flood extents
- 2% AEP, 5% AEP, and 10% AEP flood extents
- Overland flow paths

A property inside the 1% AEP flood extent has experienced or is modelled to experience flooding. That's not automatically a dealbreaker — but it tells you the risk exists, and it's worth asking the landlord directly whether the property has flooded.

For Canterbury properties: liquefaction and land damage risk data from the 2010–11 earthquake sequence is in council LIM reports. For Wellington: the Greater Wellington Regional Council publishes fault line and liquefaction zone maps.

## Property File: More Detail, More Cost

Separate from the LIM, councils hold a **property file** — the underlying building consent documents, inspection records, and council correspondence that the LIM draws from but doesn't reproduce in full.

Property files are available to anyone from the council at a separate fee, and the detail varies by council. They contain architect's drawings, code compliance certificates (or lack of them), and correspondence about defects. Useful if you've found something in the LIM you want to dig into. Not necessary as a first step.

## Builder's Report vs Property Inspection

Two different things worth keeping straight:

| | Builder's report | Property condition inspection (RTA) |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | $500–800 | Free (part of your tenancy process) |
| What it covers | Current structural condition, visible defects, cladding, roof, subfloor, electrical, plumbing | Condition at the start of tenancy — damage, cleanliness, appliances |
| Who uses it | Buyers; renters with serious concerns | Both landlord and tenant, before moving in |
| What it doesn't cover | Council history, hazard designation, leaky building claims | Any building quality questions |

For most renters, a full builder's report ($500–800) is hard to justify. But the **property condition inspection report** — filled in before you move in and signed by both parties — is essential, not optional. It's your primary protection against a landlord claiming at the end of the tenancy that damage was caused by you.

Tenancy Services has a property inspection report template at [tenancy.govt.nz](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/property-condition-reports/).

## What to Do with What You Find

Finding something concerning in a Tribunal search, a LIM, or through meth testing doesn't automatically mean walking away. Context matters.

**Ask directly.** If there's a Tribunal case on the property from three years ago involving a mould dispute, put it to the landlord: *"I found this Tribunal decision at this address — can you tell me what happened and what was fixed?"* How they respond tells you more than the record itself.

**Negotiate fixes in writing.** If you've found evidence of a past issue that might recur — drainage problems, a repaired leaky building, poor weathertightness in one area — you can ask for assurances or remediation before signing, or have specific obligations written into the agreement.

**Document the condition.** Regardless of what you find in pre-signing research, fill in a thorough property condition report on day one. Take photos. Date them. Keep them somewhere you'll find them at the end of the tenancy.
