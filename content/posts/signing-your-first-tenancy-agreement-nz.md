You've been approved. After the viewings and the applications and the waiting, it actually happened — and now someone's put an agreement in front of you and there's a gentle pressure to sign.

This is the moment most first-time renters rush. And it's the one moment you really shouldn't.

Here's what the document actually says, what matters, and what — if it's in there — you don't have to accept.

---

## What Kind of Agreement Is This?

All NZ tenancy agreements use the [Tenancy Services standard form](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/tenancy-agreements/), or must incorporate its prescribed terms. It's the same agreement used across the country — the landlord doesn't write it from scratch.

What they can do is add extra clauses, usually printed at the end under "special conditions." This is where the problems live.

Here's the thing that matters most: **any clause that attempts to override the Residential Tenancies Act 1986 is void.** You cannot sign away your statutory rights — even voluntarily, even if you think you understand what you're agreeing to. If a clause contradicts the Act, the Act wins.

---

## Fixed-Term or Periodic: The Actual Decision

This is the most consequential choice in the document, and it's worth thinking through properly.

**Fixed-term** means you and the landlord are locked in for a set period — usually 6 or 12 months. Neither party can end it early without a valid reason. At the end of the term, if neither party gives notice in the 90–21 day window before the end date, the tenancy automatically rolls to periodic.

**Periodic** has no set end date. You can leave with 21 days' notice. But since 30 January 2025, landlords can end a periodic tenancy with 90 days' notice and no reason required.

That last point matters. Under previous NZ tenancy law, periodic tenancies were arguably more stable — landlords couldn't just end them without cause. Under current law, they can. Which means a fixed-term now provides *more* security against termination than periodic, not less.

For a first-timer who wants stability and isn't planning to leave early — fixed-term is the better call. If you're genuinely unsure whether you'll stay (new job, new city, uncertain plans), periodic gives you flexibility but less protection against being given notice.

**Breaking a fixed-term early:** If you need to leave before the end date, you're liable for rent and reasonable re-letting costs until the landlord finds a replacement. The landlord has a duty to mitigate — they can't just let the property sit vacant and keep charging you. But breaking a fixed-term is still costly, so be honest with yourself about your plans before you sign one.

## Money: What's Legal and What Isn't

Before you pay anything, check these figures:

| Payment | Maximum | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bond | 4 weeks' rent | Held by Tenancy Services, not the landlord; must be lodged within 23 working days |
| Rent in advance | 2 weeks | Cannot be topped up until the first lot is used |
| Pet bond | 2 additional weeks | Only if pets are permitted; applies from 1 December 2025 |
| Application fee | **Illegal** | Any amount — do not pay |
| Letting fee (to tenant) | **Illegal** | Any amount |
| Key deposit / admin fee | **Illegal** | Any amount charged as a condition of tenancy |

> **Example:** For a $600/week flat with a cat, the maximum you should hand over at signing is $2,400 bond + $1,200 advance rent + $1,200 pet bond = **$4,800**. Anything beyond that, ask what it's for and what law authorises it.

Your bond must be lodged with Tenancy Services within 23 working days. You should receive a receipt. If you don't follow up within a month, follow up — an unlodged bond means your money is sitting with the landlord rather than protected.

## Rent Increases: The Rules

Rent can only be increased **once per 12 months**, with **at least 60 days' written notice**. The notice must state the new amount and the date it takes effect.

For fixed-term tenancies, rent can only be increased if the agreement explicitly includes a rent increase clause — and even then, a vague clause like "rent may be reviewed at landlord's discretion" is likely unenforceable because it doesn't specify how the increase is calculated.

If you see "rent may be increased with 14 days' notice" in the special conditions, that clause conflicts with the 60-day statutory requirement. It's void. The Act applies regardless.

---

## Landlord Access: Your Right to Quiet Enjoyment

You have the right to live in the property without unreasonable interference. Your landlord can't just turn up whenever they feel like it.

The rules:
- **Inspections:** Minimum 48 hours' written notice; maximum 4 per year under the standard periodic inspection regime
- **Other permitted entry** (repairs, showing prospective tenants with your consent): Minimum 24 hours' notice
- **Genuine emergencies:** No notice required, but the definition is narrow — actual emergency, not "I was just passing"

Watch for clauses that say "landlord may enter with reasonable notice." Reasonable isn't a legal standard under the RTA — the specific notice periods are. The Act's requirements apply regardless of what a vague clause says.

## The Healthy Homes Compliance Statement

For all tenancy agreements commencing after 1 July 2025, the landlord must provide a signed Healthy Homes compliance statement at tenancy start. It confirms all five standards are met: heating, insulation, ventilation, moisture and drainage, draught stopping.

Read it properly. It will tell you:
- What heating system is installed and whether it meets the heating standard
- What insulation is in place and the R-values
- Whether the kitchen and bathroom ventilation are compliant

If it's not attached when you go to sign, ask for it before you proceed. If the landlord says "we'll get it to you after you move in," that's not acceptable — it's required at the start of the tenancy, not after.

A missing compliance statement doesn't automatically mean the property is non-compliant. But a landlord who hasn't organised it is a signal about how they approach their obligations generally.

## Special Conditions: What to Read Carefully

The extra clauses added by the landlord are where unlawful or overly aggressive terms tend to appear. Scan these carefully.

**Legitimate additions** (these are enforceable and common):
- No smoking on the premises
- Pet conditions and any restrictions
- Lawn and garden maintenance responsibilities
- Parking allocation
- Heat pump filter cleaning (usually every 6 months — reasonable)

**Clauses that are unlawful or unenforceable:**

| Clause | Issue |
|---|---|
| "Tenant must have carpets professionally cleaned on exit" | Unenforceable unless property was professionally cleaned on entry; the legal standard is reasonably clean |
| "Rent may be increased with 14 days' notice" | Contradicts the 60-day statutory notice requirement |
| "No visitors after 10pm" | Unreasonable restriction on quiet enjoyment |
| "Tenant responsible for all repairs and maintenance" | Landlord cannot contract out of the s.45 duty to maintain |
| "Tenant must repaint on exit" | Fair wear and tear includes paint degradation; repainting only required if you caused damage beyond normal wear |

> **What to do:** If you spot a clause that looks wrong, ask the landlord to remove it. If they say "it's standard" — that's probably true, but standard doesn't mean legal. If they won't remove it and it's genuinely unlawful, it likely won't be enforceable anyway. Get advice from [Community Law NZ](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) before signing if you're unsure.

## Pre-Existing Damage: Get It in the Agreement

This is the most important practical step, and the one most first-timers skip.

Before you sign, walk through the property and note every mark, stain, crack, and chip **in the tenancy agreement itself** — not on a separate piece of paper, not in a text message. In the signed document that both you and the landlord sign.

Be specific:
- ✅ "Bedroom 2, north wall — scuff mark approx 30cm diameter at 1.2m height"
- ❌ "Some marks in bedroom"

Template wording for the agreement:

> *"The following pre-existing conditions were noted at inspection and agreed by both parties: [list]. These will not be claimed against the tenant's bond. Landlord will address [specific issue] by [date]."*

Both parties sign off on it. If the landlord won't sign next to your notes, that tells you something.

After signing: photograph everything documented (timestamped photos, emailed to yourself immediately), and save a copy of the tenancy agreement somewhere you can find it in 12 months' time.

## Before You Sign: Checklist

Run through this before you put your name on anything:

- [ ] Tenancy type confirmed (fixed-term or periodic) and you understand the implications
- [ ] Rent amount is correct and payment method is a bank transfer
- [ ] Bond amount is 4 weeks or less; matches what you're paying
- [ ] Rent in advance is 2 weeks or less
- [ ] Healthy Homes compliance statement is attached and signed
- [ ] No unlawful fees charged
- [ ] All pre-existing damage noted in the signed document
- [ ] You've read the special conditions and flagged anything unusual
- [ ] Both parties have signed and dated
- [ ] You have a copy

## When Things Go Wrong

If a landlord tries to enforce an unlawful clause — keeps your bond for carpet cleaning, charges a fee that's illegal, provides inadequate notice for entry — you have options.

Start with a written complaint to the landlord, citing the specific provision of the Act that the clause violates. If that doesn't resolve it, [apply to the Tenancy Tribunal](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/) — the $28 filing fee covers claims on bond, unlawful fees, and breach of statutory obligations. The Tribunal can order repayment and, for deliberate breaches, exemplary damages.

[Community Law NZ](https://communitylaw.org.nz/) will review your situation for free before you file if you want advice on the strength of your case.

---

Signing feels like the finish line. It's actually the starting line for your rights as a tenant.

Most landlord-tenant relationships go fine. But the agreement is what protects you when things go sideways — when someone tries to claim bond for carpet cleaning the previous tenant did, or when a clause appears in an email that was never in the original document. The agreement you both signed is the record.

Read it. Note the damage. Keep a copy.
