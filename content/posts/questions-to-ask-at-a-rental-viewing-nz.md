Open homes are designed for throughput, not due diligence. The agent has a clipboard, a handful of application forms, and somewhere else to be.

You have 15 minutes.

There's a whole separate guide on what your eyes should be checking during those 15 minutes — the mould spots, the extractor fans, the draughts. This one is about what your mouth should be asking and what you should be listening for.

---

## Before You Ask Anything: Two Things First

Check the Trade Me listing date before you walk in. Know how long the property has been vacant. If it's been listed for six weeks, that's context for every answer you receive — and some negotiating room on the rent.

[Search the address on RentRant](https://rentrant.nz/locations/search) before you go. Not every property will have ratings yet, but if it does, you're walking in informed about how previous tenants rated the heating, maintenance, and dampness.

---

## Compliance Questions (Ask These First)

### "Can I see the Healthy Homes compliance statement?"

Ask this early. Landlords are legally required to provide a signed Healthy Homes compliance statement at tenancy start — for all tenancies beginning after 1 July 2025.

Why ask at the viewing? Because how they answer tells you whether it exists and whether they're organised.

**Good:** They produce it, email it on the spot, or confirm it comes with the agreement package. Better still, they mention it before you ask.

**Red flag:** "We're getting it sorted." "I think we're compliant." A blank look, or a pivot to how nice the living room is.

Read the compliance statement if they hand it over. It'll tell you the heating system installed, insulation R-values, and ventilation details. It's the most information-dense single document available on the property.

---

### "Does the kitchen range hood vent outside, or is it recirculating?"

This is the most specific compliance question you can ask — and almost no viewer asks it.

Recirculating range hoods are explicitly non-compliant under the [Healthy Homes ventilation standard](/posts/ventilation-requirements-rental-nz/), which has been fully in force since 1 July 2025. The standard requires kitchen ventilation that vents extracted air to the outdoors. A range hood that filters the air and returns it to the room — no matter how powerful it sounds — doesn't meet it.

**Good:** "It vents outside — there's the external exhaust vent on the wall [points to it]."

**Red flag:** "It has a charcoal filter." "It's a good one, really powerful." Hesitation, not knowing, or a confident "yes it's compliant" without being able to point to the vent.

---

### "When was the insulation installed? Is it up to current standards?"

You're checking whether the insulation is post-2019 (which must meet the current R-value requirements) or pre-2019 (potentially grandfathered at lower standards, depending on when it was installed).

**Good:** Specific date, "fully compliant, here's the insulation spec in the compliance statement."

**Acceptable:** "It's been upgraded — we can confirm the details in the compliance statement."

**Red flag:** Vague, defensive, "it's all been done," or genuine uncertainty. A property manager looking after the property properly will know when the insulation was done.

## Landlord Quality Questions

### "Who handles maintenance — you directly, or a property manager? And what's the typical response time for urgent repairs?"

This is the most predictive question you can ask at a viewing. The maintenance relationship is where most tenancies succeed or fail. A landlord who responds to a leaking ceiling in 24 hours is a fundamentally different experience from one who takes three weeks and two follow-up emails.

**Good:** A named person. A specific phone number. A realistic timeframe ("urgent issues within 24 hours, non-urgent within 5 working days"). A clear escalation path if the primary contact doesn't respond.

**Red flag:** "The owner is really responsive." "We try to get things sorted quickly." Any answer that describes intent without specifics. If they manage multiple properties, ask: "Is there an after-hours number for genuine emergencies?"

The person you're talking to at the viewing is probably the person who'll take your call when the hot water cylinder fails on a Friday afternoon. If they can't describe the maintenance process clearly during a structured viewing, imagine that scenario.

---

### "Why did the previous tenants leave?"

One data point — but a useful one when combined with listing age and general property condition.

**Good:** Specific and unsurprising. End of fixed term. Moved cities. Bought a place. Outgrew the space. These are all normal.

**Red flag:** Vague, evasive, a laugh and a subject change. "Personal reasons" without any elaboration. Not knowing at all (which suggests they haven't been managing the property attentively).

Cross-check: search the address on Trade Me. If the same property has been listed repeatedly over multiple years with the same photos, that's its own answer to this question.

---

### "Is there anything about the property not in the listing I should know?"

Ask this at the end, once you've covered the specific questions. It's an open question that invites disclosures.

Agents have obligations to disclose known material facts. Most of the time you'll get "no, it's all there." Occasionally you'll get a useful honest disclosure — planned building work next door, a known maintenance item in the pipeline, a council notice, a pipe that needs attention.

The non-answer is still data. Someone managing the property attentively will at least mention minor quirks unprompted. Someone who hasn't been paying attention won't.

## Financial and Contract Questions

### How Long Has the Property Been Listed?

You already know — you checked before walking in. Asking anyway tests whether they'll be honest with you about it. A property that's been vacant for six weeks has different negotiating dynamics than one that went live this morning.

In Auckland's 2026 market (elevated supply, slower absorption than previous years), asking "is there any flexibility on the rent?" for properties that have been sitting a while is reasonable. It won't always get a yes, but it costs nothing to ask.

---

### Fixed-Term or Periodic?

"Is this a fixed-term or periodic tenancy? Would you consider the other if I preferred it?"

Private landlords often have flexibility on this. Large property management companies usually don't. Asking at the viewing sets expectations early and opens the conversation if you have a preference.

For the full picture on which option suits you better: [Signing your first tenancy agreement in NZ](/posts/signing-your-first-tenancy-agreement-nz/).

## Practical Questions That'll Matter Daily

### "Is fibre connected? Where does the router go?"

Fibre installation takes 2–4 weeks. If it's not connected, factor that into your move-in timeline. Check independently at the [Chorus broadband checker](https://www.chorus.co.nz/tools-support/broadband-checker) using the property address — don't rely solely on the agent's answer.

### "Do you know what the power bills were like in winter?"

A genuinely well-insulated flat costs materially less to heat than a draughty one — the difference can be $100–200 a month over winter on what looks like comparable rent. This question is partly about the answer and partly a test of how well the agent knows the property.

If they have no idea, that's fine — but file it alongside your assessment of the insulation and heating from the compliance statement.

---

## The Meta-Signal: How They Answer

Pay attention to the pattern, not just the content.

A property manager who knows their building answers immediately and specifically, without reaching for their phone or hedging. One who doesn't know deflects, checks notes, says they'll follow up.

Neither response type is automatically disqualifying — some agents manage large portfolios and genuinely need to check specific specs. But a pattern of not knowing basic compliance details is different from not knowing whether the dryer is included.

The pattern of answers to your questions is more useful than any single answer. You're building a picture of who you'd be dealing with.

## After the Viewing

Before you apply, do a bit more due diligence. Search the property address and the property manager's name in the [Tenancy Tribunal orders database](https://forms.justice.govt.nz/search/TT/). It's publicly searchable. Patterns of Tribunal orders against a landlord or property manager are useful context.

Check [RentRant](https://rentrant.nz/locations/search) again if you haven't already.

Ask anyone you know in the area — neighbourhood intel is underrated and can surface things that don't show up anywhere official.

---

You're not being difficult by asking these questions. You're interviewing a future business partner.

Most of them will answer fine. A few will deflect. One or two will tell you everything you need to know without saying anything useful at all.

Ask the questions. Listen carefully. Trust your gut about the gap between what they say and how they say it.
