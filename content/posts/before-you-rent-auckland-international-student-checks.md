You've found a place. It looks right, the price works, and someone's waiting on your response.

Before you do anything — before you transfer a dollar or sign a thing — run through this.

New Zealand has real tenant protections, but they only work if you've used them correctly from the start. These checks take less time than recovering from a bad tenancy or a scam. Most of them you can do in an afternoon.

---

## 1. Verify the property is real

Scammers copy legitimate listings and repost them at lower prices to attract desperate searchers. Before you get emotionally attached to a place, check it's actually what it claims to be.

- **Reverse image search the listing photos.** Right-click any photo and search by image (Google Images or TinEye). If the same photos appear under a different address or price, that's a fabricated listing.
- **Look up the address on Auckland Council's [GeoMaps](https://geomaps.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/).** Confirm the property exists, that it's residential, and that the description roughly matches.
- **Search the address on NZ rental platforms.** Has it been listed before at a different price? Has it been on the market for an unusually long time? Either can tell you something.

> **Key point:** A listing with professional photos, a vague address ("near the CBD"), and a price well below market is the classic setup. Low price plus high pressure equals high risk.

---

## 2. Confirm the landlord or agent is who they say they are

If you're dealing with a property management company or real estate agency, check they're actually licensed.

Licensed property managers in New Zealand are regulated by the Real Estate Agents Authority. You can search for any individual or company on the [REAA register](https://www.reaa.govt.nz/for-consumers/find-a-licensed-agent/). If the agent you're dealing with doesn't appear there, they're either unlicensed or they're not who they claim to be.

For private landlords, you can cross-check property ownership records through [Land Information New Zealand (LINZ)](https://www.linz.govt.nz/). The process takes a few steps, but if someone's claiming to be the owner of a property they don't appear to own, that's information worth having.

If your contact is offshore — based overseas, asks you to communicate only via WeChat or email, and can't meet or arrange an inspection — proceed with extreme caution.

---

## 3. Check previous tenant reviews

Before you apply, find out what other tenants experienced at the property or with the landlord.

[RentRant](https://rentrant.nz) lets you search properties by address and see ratings and reviews from previous tenants. Issues around maintenance, bond disputes, landlord responsiveness, and property condition are the kinds of things that don't show up in a listing but absolutely affect whether you'll have a decent year.

Tenancy Tribunal records are also public. If a landlord or property manager has been taken to the Tribunal — and lost — that's on record. Our [guide to checking a landlord in NZ](/blog/how-to-check-a-landlord-nz) explains how to find and read those records.

---

## 4. Know exactly what you can be charged before you sign

This is where a lot of international students get caught out, because the rules in New Zealand are stricter than in many countries.

Before you sign a tenancy agreement, the only money a landlord or agent can legally ask for is:

| Charge | Maximum |
|--------|---------|
| Bond | 4 weeks' rent |
| Rent in advance | 2 weeks' rent |
| Pet bond (if you have a pet, from 1 December 2025) | 2 weeks' rent |

That's it. Application fees, credit check fees, background check fees, admin fees — all of these are **illegal** under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986. If anyone asks for money before you've signed, that's a red flag.

> **Warning:** No legitimate landlord or property manager in New Zealand charges a fee to apply. If you're asked to pay anything before signing, something is wrong.

---

## 5. Insist on inspecting before you sign

There's no legal requirement for a pre-signing inspection, but insist on one anyway. If the landlord or agent refuses — or makes it unusually difficult — that tells you something about how they operate.

If you're still overseas, organise someone you trust to view on your behalf. A friend in Auckland, a university contact, or a prospective flatmate who's already in the city can do a walkthrough and report back. A live video call is better than nothing — but a scammer can conduct a video call in a flat they don't own. Physical presence from someone you trust is the safest option.

At the viewing, check: heating (especially for winter), signs of moisture or mould on ceilings and walls, window seals, hot water, and the general condition relative to the photos. [Our viewing checklist](/blog/red-flags-to-watch-for-when-viewing-auckland-rentals) goes through this in detail.

## 6. Complete the property condition report carefully

When you move in, you'll receive a property condition report — a document that records the state of the property at the start of your tenancy. This is one of the most important documents you'll deal with as a renter.

Go through it room by room. Note every mark, stain, scratch, and issue, no matter how minor it seems. Photograph everything with a timestamp. If the report says "good condition" for something that clearly isn't, write your disagreement on the form before signing.

> **Key point:** At the end of your tenancy, your landlord can claim against your bond for damage. If you can prove the damage existed before you moved in, they can't charge you for it. If you can't prove it — because you signed a condition report that said everything was fine — you may be liable.

You don't have to sign a condition report you think is inaccurate. Discuss any disagreements with the landlord or agent before signing.

---

## 7. Confirm your bond goes to Tenancy Services — not into a private account

Once you've paid your bond, your landlord is legally required to lodge it with [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/starting-a-tenancy/bonds/) within 23 working days. Tenancy Services holds the bond on behalf of both parties — not the landlord.

After 23 working days, you can check that your bond has been lodged at tenancy.govt.nz. You'll need your tenancy start date and address. If the bond wasn't lodged, contact Tenancy Services directly — this is a breach of the Act and they can pursue the landlord on your behalf.

This is one of the clearest differences between NZ and many other rental markets. Your bond money is legally protected. But only if it's been lodged correctly.

---

## 8. Read the tenancy agreement before you sign — all of it

You're not obligated to sign immediately, no matter what pressure you're under. Take the time to read the whole document.

Key things to check:

- **Type of tenancy:** Fixed-term (e.g., 12 months, specific end date) or periodic (ongoing, no fixed end). Fixed-term gives you more security but less flexibility to leave early.
- **Rent amount and review date:** Confirm it matches what was advertised and note when it can next be reviewed.
- **Notice periods:** For a periodic tenancy, you need to give 21 days' notice to leave. Your landlord needs to give 90 days.
- **What's included:** Parking, storage, utilities — anything discussed verbally needs to be in the agreement or it may not be enforceable.
- **Subletting rules:** If you plan to travel or have someone stay for extended periods, check what the agreement says.

If anything is unclear or different from what was discussed, ask before signing. If you're being pressured to sign without time to read — that's a red flag, full stop.

## 9. Know who to call before you need to

Save these contacts now, not when something has already gone wrong:

| Organisation | What they do | Contact |
|---|---|---|
| Tenancy Services | Official tenancy info, complaints, bond disputes | 0800 836 262 / tenancy.govt.nz |
| Community Law NZ | Free legal advice | communitylaw.org.nz |
| Citizens Advice Bureau | Free general advice and referrals | cab.org.nz |
| Tenancy Tribunal | Formal dispute resolution (~$20 to file) | justice.govt.nz/tribunals/tenancy-tribunal |

Tenancy Services has information available in multiple languages. If English isn't your first language, it's worth checking what's available in yours before you need to make a call under pressure.
