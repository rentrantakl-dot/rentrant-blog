Most renters never try to negotiate. They see the listed price, assume it's fixed, and either take it or move on.

Which is exactly what landlords are counting on.

Here's the reality: rent is a price, and prices are negotiable — especially in a market where vacancy rates are climbing, listings are sitting longer, and the balance of power has shifted more towards tenants than it has in years. The Housing Minister himself told renters to negotiate. The Spinoff's Stuart Donovan ran the numbers and showed that in many Auckland suburbs, landlords are competing for tenants, not the other way around.

If you've never pushed back on a rental price, you're probably not alone. But that doesn't mean you shouldn't start.

---

## When you actually have leverage

Not every situation is ripe for negotiation. But more are than you'd think.

**You have leverage when:**

- The listing has been up for **three weeks or more** — the Auckland average is about 21 days. Anything beyond that, and the landlord is losing money every week the place sits empty.
- There were **few people at the viewing** — if you were one of two or three, the landlord doesn't have a queue of applicants.
- The property has **visible issues** — deferred maintenance, older fittings, single glazing, no heat pump. These reduce its market value.
- It's **winter or a quieter rental period** — January and February are peak season. June through August, things slow down. Landlords listing in the off-season are often more flexible.
- The property is **overpriced compared to similar listings** — if comparable flats in the same suburb are going for $50-80/week less, the landlord knows they're ambitious.

**You probably don't have leverage when:**

- The listing just went up and the open home was packed
- It's peak season and vacancy rates are low
- The property is genuinely well-maintained and priced fairly
- Multiple applications have already been submitted

Know which situation you're in before you start the conversation.

## Build your case before you open your mouth

Negotiation without evidence is just arguing. You need data, and luckily it's free.

### 1. Check the Market Rent Tool

The [Tenancy Services Market Rent Tool](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/rent-bond-and-bills/market-rent/) uses bond lodgement data — actual rents tenants agreed to pay, not listing prices. Filter by your suburb, property type, and bedroom count.

If the property you're looking at is listed above the upper quartile for the area, that's your opening.

### 2. Screenshot comparable listings

Jump on Trade Me and find 3-5 similar properties in the same area. Screenshot the ones that are cheaper. If there are three comparable two-bedroom flats listed at $560-580/week and this one's at $640, you've got evidence that speaks for itself.

### 3. Note the property's shortcomings

Be honest but specific. Is the insulation subpar? Are the windows single-glazed? Is there no dishwasher, no heat pump, or dated fixtures? These aren't complaints — they're reasons why the property doesn't justify top-of-market pricing.

### 4. Check how long it's been listed

The Trade Me listing date tells you everything. If it's been sitting for 25+ days, the landlord is paying a mortgage (or rates, or a property manager) on an empty property. Every week of vacancy costs them the full week's rent. That maths works in your favour.

## How to actually negotiate

### Before you sign (new tenancy)

This is your strongest position. You haven't committed to anything yet.

**The approach:** Apply for the property at the listed price — don't lead with a lower offer in your application, because you'll likely get filtered out. Once you've been approved or shortlisted, that's when you negotiate.

**Example email after approval:**

> Hi [Property Manager/Landlord],
>
> Thanks for approving my application — I'm keen to move forward.
>
> Before signing, I wanted to discuss the rent. I've had a look at comparable properties in [suburb] using the Tenancy Services market rent data and current Trade Me listings, and similar [X]-bedroom [property type] properties are currently sitting around $[amount]/week.
>
> Given the property's condition and the current market, would you consider $[your proposed amount]/week? I'm a reliable tenant — [brief relevant detail: stable employment, references available, no pets, etc.] — and I'm looking for a long-term tenancy.
>
> Happy to chat about this if it's easier by phone.
>
> Cheers,
> [Your name]

Attach screenshots of comparable listings and the market rent data.

### During an existing tenancy (rent increase)

If your landlord has sent a rent increase notice, you can still negotiate. We've covered this in detail in our [rent increase guide](/posts/what-to-do-when-your-landlord-raises-the-rent/), but the short version:

- The increase must follow the rules (12-month gap, 60 days' notice)
- Check whether the proposed rent matches market data
- Respond calmly and factually with evidence
- Propose a specific alternative number

Landlords want to avoid turnover. Replacing a tenant costs them advertising fees, potential void periods, and the risk of getting someone worse. If you're a good tenant and you negotiate reasonably, most will meet you partway.

## The numbers behind the negotiation

Let's make this concrete.

**What vacancy costs a landlord:**

| Scenario | Weekly rent | 3-week vacancy cost | Property manager re-let fee |
|----------|------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| Budget flat | $520 | $1,560 | ~$600 |
| Mid-range | $650 | $1,950 | ~$750 |
| Premium | $800 | $2,400 | ~$925 |

A landlord who rejects a $30/week reduction to hold out for full price needs the property to re-let within days to come out ahead. If it takes three weeks, they've lost more than they'd have saved in the first year of a slightly lower rent.

**What $30/week actually means:**

- To you: $1,560/year saved
- To the landlord: $1,560/year less — but offset by zero vacancy costs, no re-let fees, and a known good tenant

When you frame it like that, the negotiation starts to make sense for both sides.

## What to say (and what not to say)

### Do:
- **Be specific.** "The market rent tool shows comparable properties at $570/week" beats "I think this is too expensive."
- **Acknowledge the relationship.** "I'd like to stay long-term" signals stability, which landlords value.
- **Propose, don't demand.** "Would you consider $590?" invites conversation. "I'm not paying more than $590" shuts it down.
- **Be prompt.** Respond within a few days. Waiting too long signals you've already accepted.

### Don't:
- **Get emotional.** "I can't afford this" isn't a negotiation strategy — it's a personal finance problem. Focus on market evidence, not your budget.
- **Threaten to leave unless you mean it.** Empty threats damage the relationship. If you're prepared to walk, say so calmly. If you're not, don't bluff.
- **List every minor grievance.** "The tap drips and the paint is peeling and the carpet is old" sounds like you're whingeing. Pick the 1-2 issues that genuinely affect the property's market value.
- **Negotiate by text.** Email gives both sides a record and time to think. Texts feel pushy and informal.

## The 90-day reality check

Since January 2025, landlords can end a periodic tenancy with 90 days' notice, no reason needed. This changes the dynamic — a landlord who doesn't want to negotiate can, in theory, just give you notice and find someone who'll pay full price.

In practice, most won't. The cost and hassle of turnover is real. But it does mean you should negotiate like someone proposing a mutually beneficial deal, not someone making demands.

If you're on a fixed-term tenancy, the 90-day no-cause rule doesn't apply during the term. Your position is actually more secure during a fixed term.

> The 90-day rule is real but it's not the trump card landlords think it is. Most would rather keep a reliable tenant at slightly lower rent than gamble on the market.

## When it doesn't work

Sometimes the answer is no. The landlord won't budge, and the listed price is what it is.

At that point, you have three options:

1. **Accept the price** — if the property is worth it and you want to live there, that's a legitimate choice
2. **Walk away** — there are other listings, especially in the current market
3. **Accept but flag the conversation** — "I understand. I'd appreciate revisiting this when the lease comes up for renewal." This plants a seed

Not every negotiation ends in a win. But you miss every one you don't take.

## Quick negotiation checklist

**Before you approach:**
- [ ] Checked the Market Rent Tool for your suburb and property type
- [ ] Screenshotted 3-5 comparable listings at lower prices
- [ ] Noted how long the property has been listed
- [ ] Identified 1-2 specific property shortcomings that affect value
- [ ] Decided on your target price and your walk-away point

**When you negotiate:**
- [ ] Applied and been approved first (for new tenancies)
- [ ] Communicated in writing (email preferred)
- [ ] Led with market evidence, not personal budget
- [ ] Proposed a specific number
- [ ] Kept the tone collaborative, not confrontational
