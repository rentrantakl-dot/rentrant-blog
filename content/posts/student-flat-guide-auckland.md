You're going to spend a lot of your university life in a flat. Probably more time than you'll spend in lectures. The difference between a good year and a year you spend cold, broke, and arguing about the power bill usually comes down to choices you make before you move in — and a few you make once you're there.

This is the full lifecycle guide for flatting as a student in Auckland. Where to start, how to choose, what to sign, how to survive a winter in a leaky villa, and how to leave without losing your bond. Built specifically for Auckland — but most of it applies wherever you're studying in NZ.

---

## Year one: halls or straight into a flat?

Most Auckland students do their first year in halls. There are good reasons for it.

**What halls give you:** a built-in social environment in a year where you don't know anyone, meals (in catered options) so you don't have to cook between assignments, power, internet, and a single bill that covers everything. You're not negotiating a tenancy agreement, you're not splitting bills with strangers, and the institution handles disputes through its own pastoral processes.

**What halls cost:** more, on the sticker. Catered halls at the University of Auckland or AUT typically run $400–$625/week depending on the residence. Self-catered apartment-style is $360–$520/week. That's substantially more than a $280–$340/week room in a flat — but the flat number doesn't include power, internet, or food.

A rough comparison for first-year:

| Option | Weekly cost | What's included |
|---|---|---|
| Catered hall | $475–$625 | Room, all meals, power, internet, social environment |
| Self-catered hall | $370–$520 | Room, kitchen, power, internet (you cook) |
| Flat (room in shared house) | $280–$340 | Room only — add ~$80 food, $40 power/internet/water = ~$420 |

The gap is smaller than it looks. For a first-year living away from home for the first time, halls are usually worth the premium. By second year, almost everyone moves into a flat.

For specifics on University of Auckland halls and AUT halls — costs, walk times, what's available — see [renting near the University of Auckland](/posts/renting-near-university-of-auckland/) and [renting near AUT](/posts/renting-near-aut-auckland/).

## Choosing a suburb: rent vs commute

Auckland is spread out. The suburb you pick shapes your daily life more than you'd expect — a 40-minute commute looks fine on paper and feels long by week three.

The honest trade-offs:

**Walking distance to campus** ($280–$340/week room): Eden Terrace, Newton, Grafton, Kingsland, Mt Eden, the City. You walk to lectures, you can stay late on campus, you don't think about transport. You pay the most.

**Bus belt** ($240–$330/week room): Sandringham, Mt Eden, Kingsland, Mt Albert, Onehunga. 15–25 minute commute; cheaper rent, more space, often nicer flats.

**Western Line train** ($210–$290/week room): Mt Albert, Avondale, New Lynn, Henderson. The cheapest sustainable option for City Campus students. Train every 10–15 min peak. Becomes faster from late 2026 with the City Rail Link.

**North Shore** (AUT North Campus only) ($220–$330/week room): Northcote, Birkenhead, Sunnynook, Constellation, Albany. The Northern Busway is genuinely good.

**South Auckland** (AUT South only) ($190–$260/week room): Manukau, Papatoetoe, Manurewa. Cheapest; best for students whose degree is South-Campus only.

A reasonable rule of thumb: every $30/week saved on rent costs you about 10 minutes of commute each way. Whether that's worth it depends on your timetable, your tolerance for early mornings, and whether you'd rather have the time or the money.

## Finding flatmates

In second year, most students flat with friends from halls. By third year, that's broken up — people transfer, study abroad, drop out, or pair up — and you're often looking for one or two flatmates you don't know.

The reliable routes:

- **Friends-of-friends.** Whoever's looking for a room in your year-group is your warmest pool.
- **University Facebook groups.** Most year-groups and faculties have private flatting pages. The signal-to-noise is better than open marketplaces.
- **Trade Me Property — flatmates section.** Public, busy, mixed quality. Useful when you've left it late.
- **Facebook Marketplace and groups.** Wide, but verify everything — scammers post here.

Two questions to ask any prospective flatmate before signing:

1. **What's your sleep and study pattern?** A 9–5 sleeper sharing with someone who's up till 3am playing CS is a slow-burn problem.
2. **How do you handle bills and chores?** "Whatever's chill" is not an answer that survives June.

Always meet in person, ideally at the flat itself. Meeting one flatmate while the others are conveniently absent is a flag.

## What you'll pay upfront — and what's illegal

Under the [Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html), the only legal upfront payments are bond (max 4 weeks' rent) and rent in advance (max 2 weeks).

For a $310/week room:

| Item | Amount | Legal limit |
|---|---|---|
| Bond | $1,240 | 4 weeks max |
| Rent in advance | $620 | 2 weeks max |
| Pet bond (if pets agreed) | $620 | 2 weeks (from 1 December 2025) |
| Application fee | **$0** | Illegal |
| Letting / admin fee | **$0** | Illegal |
| Credit-check fee | **$0** | Illegal |
| **Total upfront (no pet)** | **$1,860** | |

The bond goes to [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) — not the landlord. They have to lodge it within 23 working days. After you've paid, check it's been lodged at tenancy.govt.nz under your own name. If it hasn't, that's a serious red flag — and a Tenancy Tribunal matter.

> **Warning:** If you're being asked to pay anything above bond + rent in advance to secure a flat, push back. Most "admin fees" and "key deposits" are illegal. Real property managers know this and don't ask. Anyone who does is either uninformed or chancing it.

For more detail on bonds specifically, see [your bond rights in NZ](/posts/your-bond-rights-nz/).

## Signing the tenancy agreement

You'll be handed a standard Tenancy Services agreement, often with extra clauses bolted on at the end. The full breakdown is in [signing your first tenancy agreement in NZ](/posts/signing-your-first-tenancy-agreement-nz/), but the student-flat highlights:

**Fixed-term vs periodic.** Most student flats are 12-month fixed-term, starting late January or February. Fixed-term locks both you and the landlord in — neither party can end early without serious cause. Periodic is more flexible (21 days' notice from you, 90 days from landlord) but offers less stability.

**Who's on the lease.** Make sure every flatmate is named on the agreement. If only the "head tenant" signs and others sublet, the unnamed flatmates have weaker protections — and the head tenant carries all the legal liability.

**The Healthy Homes compliance statement.** Required by law since 1 July 2025. It must be attached to the agreement and tells you the heating, insulation, and ventilation specs. Read it. If the heater output isn't enough for the room, that's a compliance issue.

**Special conditions.** Things like "tenant must professionally clean carpets on exit" are usually unenforceable unless the property was professionally cleaned before you moved in. Don't sign a clause you know is unlawful — ask for it to be removed.

**Pre-existing damage.** Walk through the property before signing. Note every mark, stain, crack, and chip in the agreement itself — not on a separate piece of paper. Photograph everything with timestamps. If the landlord won't sign next to your notes, that's a flag.

## Surviving winter in an Auckland flat

Auckland winters aren't cold by Wellington or Dunedin standards, but Auckland flats are a different problem — older villas, often poorly insulated, sometimes still drying out from the last leak. June through August in a damp Mt Eden bungalow can be miserable.

What helps:

**Use the heating.** A heat pump that gets switched off because "it's too expensive" is a heat pump growing mould in your wardrobe. Running a heat pump on low in the main living area is cheaper than running an electric heater on high in your bedroom.

**Open windows daily.** Ten minutes in the morning. Yes, even when it's cold. Most winter mould comes from indoor moisture (your breath, your shower, your cooking) with nowhere to go. The Healthy Homes ventilation standard is the legal floor — see [our ventilation requirements guide](/posts/ventilation-requirements-rental-nz/) for what's compliant.

**Tell your landlord about damp and mould early.** If you don't, you're more likely to get blamed for it at the end of the tenancy. Email — not text. Email creates a record. Photograph the issue. If they don't act, [our mould guide](/posts/mould-in-your-auckland-rental/) covers what to do next.

**Check your power plan.** Most Auckland student flats are on standard variable rates. If the flat has a heat pump and you're using it, a "low fixed daily charge" plan can save real money over a winter. Compare on [Powerswitch](https://www.powerswitch.org.nz/) (free, run by Consumer NZ).

> **Key point:** Under the Healthy Homes Standards, your landlord must provide a fixed heater capable of warming the main living area to 18°C. Plug-in panel heaters don't count. If your flat fails this, you can request a compliance order from the Tenancy Tribunal — and you can claim a rent reduction for the period of non-compliance. See [how to apply to the Tenancy Tribunal](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/).

## When things go wrong: your rights, briefly

A few things to know that the agreement won't tell you in plain English:

**Rent increases.** Once every 12 months, with 60 days' written notice. No mid-tenancy hikes on a fixed-term.

**Landlord access.** Inspections need 48 hours' written notice and can only happen between 8am and 7pm, no more than once every four weeks. Other entry (repairs, viewings near end of tenancy) needs 24 hours' notice. Emergencies aside, your landlord cannot let themselves in.

**Maintenance.** The landlord has to keep the property in a reasonable state of repair. If something breaks through normal use — heat pump, hot water, fridge if it came with the flat — they fix it. Putting your maintenance request in writing is what makes it enforceable.

**Ending the tenancy.** On periodic, you give 21 days' written notice. The landlord can give 90 days' no-cause notice (since 30 January 2025). On fixed-term, neither party can end early without specific grounds — but if both parties agree in writing, you can break a fixed-term lease.

**The Tenancy Tribunal.** $28 to apply, no lawyer needed, most cases resolve at free mediation. If the landlord won't fix something, won't return your bond, or breaches the lease, this is the mechanism. See [how to apply to the Tenancy Tribunal](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/) for the step-by-step.

For the full picture of your rights, [our tenant rights guide](/posts/tenant-rights-nz/) covers it.

## Moving out without losing your bond

Bond disputes are the single most common Tenancy Tribunal complaint, and most of them are avoidable. The principle: leave the property in the condition you found it, with allowance for fair wear and tear.

The practical version:

- **Photograph everything before you hand back keys.** Same angles as the move-in photos. Timestamped. Email them to yourself.
- **Reasonably clean and tidy is the legal standard.** Not "as professionally cleaned" — unless the property was professionally cleaned before you moved in. See [fair wear and tear in NZ rentals](/briefs/fair-wear-and-tear-nz-rentals/).
- **Don't agree to professional carpet cleaning unless required.** If the agreement says you must, and the property wasn't professionally cleaned before you moved in, that clause is likely unenforceable. See [can a landlord keep my bond for carpet cleaning?](/briefs/can-landlord-keep-bond-for-carpet-cleaning-nz/).
- **Sign the bond refund form together.** Tenancy Services releases bond when both tenant and landlord agree on the split. If you can't agree, lodge a Tribunal application — don't just leave the bond in dispute.

The single best thing you can do for your bond outcome is the move-in photo set you took on day one. If a dispute arises in 12 months, those photos are your evidence.
