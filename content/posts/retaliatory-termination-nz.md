The notice arrives a week after you emailed about the mould. Or the day after you asked your landlord to explain the rent increase. Or the month after you told them you'd contacted Tenancy Services.

Since 30 January 2025, landlords in New Zealand can issue a [90-day termination notice](/posts/90-day-notice-your-rights-and-options/) to periodic tenants without giving any reason. But there is a limit: if the notice was issued because of something you did — because you complained, because you exercised a legal right — the law calls that retaliation. And retaliation is unlawful.

This guide explains what retaliatory termination is legally, what evidence matters, and how to challenge a notice at the Tenancy Tribunal.

---

## What the law says

[Section 54 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95517.html) gives the Tribunal the power to declare a termination notice retaliatory and of no effect. The Tribunal can do this when it is satisfied the notice was given because the tenant — or an external enforcement body — exercised rights that the law protects.

The two protected triggers under s54 are:

**s54(2)(a):** The tenant exercised, or proposed to exercise, any right under the tenancy agreement or any Act, or made a complaint against the landlord. This includes: requesting a Healthy Homes compliance statement, making a maintenance complaint, applying to the Tribunal, disputing a rent increase, raising a bond dispute, or contacting Tenancy Services.

**s54(2)(b):** An external body — Tenancy Services, a local council, MBIE, or another enforcement authority — exercised powers in respect of the tenancy or premises. This trigger was strengthened under the [Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/law-changes/) (in force 30 January 2025). A proactive Healthy Homes inspection that results in enforcement action now also triggers the protection.

---

## The honest reality: there is no "90-day rule"

A common misconception is that if a landlord issues a termination notice within 90 days of a complaint, it is *presumed* to be retaliatory. That is not how NZ law works.

There is no fixed-day threshold that shifts the burden to the landlord. Instead, under [section 54 of the RTA 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95517.html), the Tribunal must consider what inferences can be drawn from the timing and sequence of events. Proximity in time is an inferential factor — it is relevant evidence — but the **burden remains on you** to show the landlord was motivated wholly or partly by your complaint.

This matters because it affects how you approach the challenge. You can't simply point to a calendar and say "notice arrived 12 days after my email, therefore retaliation." You need to build a case where the timeline, combined with other evidence, makes the inference compelling.

---

## What evidence matters

The Tribunal is assessing motivation. The following helps establish it:

**The timeline.** Note the date of your complaint and the date the notice arrived. A notice issued the day after a Healthy Homes complaint tells a different story than one issued six months later. Write the dates down now; do not rely on memory.

**Written communication.** Every email and text exchange about the complaint. This is your primary evidence. If you made the complaint verbally, follow up in writing immediately: *"Hi [Landlord], just confirming our phone call today where I raised the issue of [X]."*

**The landlord's response to your complaint.** Did they dismiss it? Say they'd "look into it"? Say nothing? Defensiveness or silence in response to a legitimate complaint, followed shortly by a notice, is exactly the pattern the Tribunal is assessing.

**The underlying issue.** Photos of mould, screenshots of the Tenancy Services complaint form, the compliance statement you requested. These establish that your complaint was legitimate, not vexatious.

**Absence of any alternative explanation.** If the landlord hasn't mentioned selling the property, isn't moving into it, and has given no reason for the notice, the timing becomes more significant.

> **Key point:** The Tribunal has an exception under s54(4): it need not declare a notice retaliatory if it is satisfied your exercise of rights was "vexatious or frivolous to such an extent as to justify" the notice. A legitimate maintenance complaint about mould or heating cannot be vexatious. A pattern of repeated trivial complaints designed to harass a landlord might be. For almost every real tenant situation, this exception does not apply.

## How to apply: the 28-day window

You have **28 working days** from the date you receive the termination notice to apply to the Tribunal and request that the notice be declared of no effect (under [RTA 1986 s54](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM95517.html)).

This is not a soft deadline. After 28 working days, you can still raise retaliation as part of a broader Tribunal application — but you lose the ability to have the notice cancelled while your tenancy continues.

**Step 1: Apply online at [justice.govt.nz → Tenancy Tribunal → Apply](https://www.justice.govt.nz/tribunals/tenancy-tribunal/apply/).** For a full walkthrough of the application process — what to include, what to expect at mediation and hearing — see our [Tenancy Tribunal guide](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/).

**Step 2:** In your application, state clearly: (a) the date you received the termination notice, (b) the date and nature of your complaint, (c) that you are applying under section 54 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986 for the notice to be declared retaliatory and of no effect.

**Step 3:** Include your evidence: screenshots of the complaint, the notice itself, photos of the issue you complained about.

The application fee is [$28](https://www.justice.govt.nz/tribunals/tenancy-tribunal/apply/). If your application succeeds, you can ask the Tribunal to order the landlord to reimburse it.

## What the Tribunal can order

If the Tribunal finds the notice was retaliatory:

| Order | What it means |
|---|---|
| Notice declared of no effect | Your tenancy continues — the notice is cancelled as if it was never given |
| Exemplary damages | Issuing a retaliatory notice is an unlawful act; the Tribunal can award damages against the landlord |
| Application fee reimbursement | If your application succeeds, ask the Tribunal to order the $28 fee paid back |

**Exemplary damages** for an unlawful act are capped under [Schedule 1 of the RTA 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/whole.html) at **$7,200** for landlords with fewer than six properties and **$50,000** for larger landlords. Exemplary damages for a retaliatory notice are not automatic — the Tribunal looks at how flagrant the conduct was. A notice issued the day after a legitimate complaint with no other explanation is more likely to attract them than a marginal case where timing is ambiguous.

The notice being declared of no effect is the most significant outcome. Your tenancy does not end, and your landlord cannot simply reissue the same notice — they'd need a fresh reason. They can issue another 90-day notice in the future, but the documented pattern of retaliation will be on record if they do.

## Realistic expectations

This is not an easy path. The burden of proof is on you, the evidence is inferential, and the Tribunal does not always find retaliation even where tenants believe it occurred.

What makes a strong case:
- A clearly documented complaint, in writing
- A notice that arrived promptly after the complaint (days, not months)
- An unresolved underlying issue the landlord had not acted on
- No other plausible reason for the termination

What makes a weak case:
- An oral complaint with no written follow-up
- A long gap between complaint and notice
- A landlord with a stated reason unrelated to your complaint (sale, owner-use)

> **Example:** A tenant emails their landlord on 14 April about a Healthy Homes heating complaint. The landlord replies "we'll look into it" and takes no further action. On 3 May — 19 days later — the tenant receives a 90-day notice with no stated reason. There's a written complaint, a documented non-response, and a tight timeline. Strong candidate for a retaliation application. Compare that with a tenant who mentioned heating verbally at an inspection in February and receives a 90-day notice in June — no written trail, a four-month gap, no documented link between the two events. Much harder to pursue.

If you're uncertain whether your situation meets the threshold, get advice before applying. The costs of an unsuccessful Tribunal claim are low — but the emotional investment is real, and knowing where you stand before you file is worth the Community Law call.
