April in Auckland is the window.

Temperatures have dropped enough to feel the difference in an unheated room, but winter hasn't fully arrived yet. That gap — right now — is the best time to check whether your flat is actually going to be liveable in July. Not because the problems will be easier to fix later, but because they won't be.

If you discover in July that your bathroom extractor doesn't vent outside, or that the heat pump is undersized for the room, or that there are gaps under every external door — you'll be living with those problems for months while your landlord works through the usual cycle of acknowledgement, delays, and half-measures. If you raise the same issues in April, in writing, you've created a documented timeline that gives your landlord reasonable time to fix things before winter proper — and gives you a strong Tribunal case if they don't.

Here's the five-point audit, and how to document what you find.

---

## Before you start: find your compliance statement

Your landlord is legally required to provide a **Healthy Homes compliance statement** — a document stating whether the property meets each of the five standards. It should have been attached to your tenancy agreement or provided within 21 days of tenancy start.

Find it before you do the audit. It tells you what the landlord has declared about insulation type and location, heater model and capacity, extractor fan details, and draught-stopping measures. Comparing the statement to what you actually find is often where discrepancies appear.

If you don't have a compliance statement, that's already a breach of the Residential Tenancies Act. Make a note of it.

---

## 1. Heating

**What the law requires:** A fixed heater in the main living room capable of heating that room to at least **18°C** when the outdoor temperature is 1°C. Portable heaters do not qualify under the Healthy Homes heating standard, regardless of their wattage.

**What to check:**

- Turn the heater on. Does it actually work?
- Is it fixed — heat pump, panel heater on the wall, log burner — or is it a portable unit that could be moved?
- Does it heat the room adequately? If you've got a 40m² open-plan living/kitchen and a small wall panel that struggles to push the temperature above 16°C, that's a sizing problem.
- Check the compliance statement for the heater model and the room it covers.

**Common problems:** A heat pump that works but hasn't been serviced in years (filters clogged, airflow restricted). A wall heater installed in the living room that was measured as "compliant" but the room has since been opened up to a larger space. Unflued gas heaters — these are prohibited in bedrooms and living rooms under the standard.

**If it's not right:** Get it in writing now. → [Full details on the heating standard](../heating-requirements-rental-nz/)

## 2. Insulation

**What the law requires:** Ceiling insulation to at least **R2.9** in Auckland (Climate Zone 1). Underfloor insulation to **R1.3** where the sub-floor is accessible. Wall insulation is only required when walls are being rebuilt or replaced.

**What to check:**

- Your compliance statement should list the insulation type, R-value, and location. If it says R1.8 ceiling insulation, that's below the required standard and should have been flagged as non-compliant.
- You can't easily check insulation yourself without a roof hatch or accessing the sub-floor. But you can feel the effects: a cold ceiling in winter often means inadequate insulation above.
- If the statement lists the insulation as "pre-2016 existing" — this may be grandfathered at R1.5, which is lower than the current minimum. Check whether the property qualifies for this exception.

**If it's not right:** Raise it in writing, citing the compliance statement's listed values. → [Full details on the insulation standard](../insulation-requirements-rental-nz/)

---

## 3. Ventilation

**What the law requires:** Every habitable room must have openable windows totalling at least **5% of floor area**. Kitchens must have an extractor fan or range hood **vented to the outside** — recirculating range hoods don't qualify. Bathrooms must have an extractor fan vented to the outside — an openable window does not substitute.

**What to check:**

- Turn on the kitchen extractor. Can you feel air being drawn? Hold a piece of paper near it — does it get pulled toward the fan? Now check: does the duct actually go outside, or does it just recirculate filtered air back into the kitchen? A recirculating range hood has a charcoal filter and no exterior duct. It fails the standard.
- Check the bathroom extractor. Is there one? Does it switch on? Can you feel suction? A fan that vents into the roof cavity rather than outside is also non-compliant — a common problem in older NZ houses.
- Try to open the bedroom and living room windows fully. Painted shut? Hardware broken? Hinges seized? Windows that nominally open but require significant force to move may not be meeting the "openable" requirement in any practical sense.

**If it's not right:** → [Full details on the ventilation standard](../ventilation-requirements-rental-nz/)

---

## 4. Draught stopping

**What the law requires:** Landlords must block unreasonable gaps and holes that allow draughts. This includes gaps in walls, ceilings, floors, and around pipes. Unused fireplaces and chimneys must be blocked. Windows, doors, and skylights must close properly.

**What to check:**

- On a cold morning, walk slowly past all external doors and windows. Cold air coming through at the edges is a gap problem.
- Check under external doors — a gap wider than roughly 5mm is unreasonable. You shouldn't be able to see daylight under a closed door.
- Check around pipe penetrations through walls and floors — kitchen waste pipes, bathroom pipes. Gaps here are common in older properties and make a real difference.
- Older houses with unused fireplaces: is the flue blocked? An open flue is a direct column of cold air into the room.

**Why it matters:** Draughts directly undermine your heating's effectiveness. A correctly sized heat pump fighting a draught from an unsealed fireplace is working much harder than it should. Draught stopping is one of the cheapest fixes a landlord can make — but they have to do it.

---

## 5. Moisture, drainage, and mould

**What the law requires:** No water should be entering the property through the roof, walls, windows, or ground. Gutters, downpipes, and drains must be maintained and in working order. Where there's an enclosed sub-floor, a ground moisture barrier is required where practicable.

**What to check:**

- Do a pre-winter mould inspection now, before condensation season starts. Check: ceiling corners of bedrooms, the area around bathroom windows and extractor fans, behind furniture pushed against exterior walls, inside wardrobes on exterior walls, and under bathroom sinks.
- Any mould you find now can be photographed with a timestamp before winter arrives. This matters. If mould appears in August and you haven't documented anything, a landlord may claim it's caused by "insufficient ventilation" or "lifestyle condensation." Pre-winter documentation establishes that structural issues existed before the cold season.
- Check gutters from the ground: are they overflowing or visibly blocked with leaves? Water pooling against the foundation is a moisture ingress risk.
- After any rain, check for drips or wet patches in the ceiling, around window frames, and on walls.

**If mould is already present:** → [Mould in your rental: who's responsible and what to do](../mould-in-your-auckland-rental/)

## Document what you find

Walk the flat with your phone. Take timestamped photos of everything you're flagging. Don't just note "the heating doesn't work" — photograph the heater model plate (often a sticker inside the unit), the room it's in, and the general room dimensions if the concern is sizing.

For mould: photograph affected areas close-up with the room context visible. Note which wall or surface it's on (interior wall, exterior wall, ceiling).

Save everything somewhere you control — email it to yourself, add to cloud storage. Not just your phone's camera roll.

---

## Raise issues in writing

Once you've done the audit, write to your landlord or property manager. Keep it factual and specific.

> **Template**
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I've done a check of the property ahead of the change of season and wanted to flag a few things I'd like addressed.
>
> **[Issue 1]:** [Specific description — e.g. "The bathroom extractor fan does not appear to vent to the outside. I can hear the fan but there is no exterior duct visible from outside."]
>
> **[Issue 2]:** [Specific description — e.g. "There is a significant draught under the front door. I can see daylight when the door is closed."]
>
> **[Issue 3 if applicable]:** [Description]
>
> The Healthy Homes compliance statement we received lists [what it lists]. I'd appreciate confirmation of which standard each of these issues relates to and a timeline for addressing them.
>
> Please reply in writing to this email.
>
> [Your name]
> [Property address]
> [Date]

Keep the tone matter-of-fact. This isn't a complaint, it's a record.

## If your landlord doesn't act

A documented written request that goes unaddressed is the basis for a Tenancy Tribunal application. You don't need a lawyer. The application fee is $28. Most cases go to free mediation first, usually within 2–6 weeks.

The Tribunal has awarded tenants compliance orders, rent reductions for the period of non-compliance, and exemplary damages for Healthy Homes breaches.

Your April paper trail — the photos, the email, the landlord's response or non-response — is what makes that application strong.

→ [How to apply to the Tenancy Tribunal](../tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/)
