Landlords don't fix things for a few different reasons. Sometimes it's genuine unawareness — they don't know the heat pump has stopped working because you mentioned it in passing rather than in writing. Sometimes it's cost avoidance — they know and are hoping you'll forget or move out. And sometimes it's something in between: a property manager who logged the request, sent it to a contractor, and it's been sitting in an inbox for three weeks with nobody following up.

The path from "told my landlord about the mould" to "still waiting six weeks later" is one of the most common tenancy disputes in New Zealand. In winter, when heating fails or moisture problems peak, it can feel genuinely urgent — because it is. Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, you have a clear legal mechanism for forcing action on a maintenance issue that isn't being fixed. Most tenants don't use it because they don't know the steps.

This is the escalation path.

## What Landlords Are Legally Required to Fix

The baseline is [section 45(1)(b) of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/0120/latest/DLM94278.html): a landlord must keep the property in a reasonable state of repair having regard to the age and character of the premises and the likely duration of the tenancy.

"Reasonable state of repair" covers:
- Structural integrity (roof, walls, foundations)
- Plumbing and drainage (leaks, hot water, toilets)
- Electrical systems
- Appliances provided with the tenancy (oven, stove, fridge, dishwasher, washing machine — [see the full brief on appliances](/briefs/can-landlord-refuse-to-fix-appliances-nz/))
- Doors and windows that open, close, and lock properly

Since 1 July 2025, the [Healthy Homes Standards](/posts/is-your-auckland-rental-healthy-homes-compliant/) add five specific requirements fully in force across all private rentals:

| Standard | What is required |
|---|---|
| Heating | Fixed heater capable of warming the main living area to at least 18°C |
| Insulation | Ceiling and underfloor insulation to specified R-values by climate zone |
| Ventilation | Extractor fans in kitchen and all bathrooms; openable windows throughout |
| Moisture & drainage | Drainage and vapour barriers in enclosed subfloors where applicable |
| Draught stopping | Gaps and holes in walls, ceilings, windows, and floors sealed |

A landlord who isn't meeting the Healthy Homes Standards is in breach of a specific statutory obligation, not just a general duty of care. This matters at the Tribunal stage.

For more on [mould and dampness](/posts/mould-in-your-auckland-rental/), [heating requirements](/posts/heating-requirements-rental-nz/), or [insulation standards](/posts/insulation-requirements-rental-nz/), those guides cover each standard in detail.

## Urgent vs Non-Urgent Issues

Not all maintenance problems are equal, and the expected response time reflects that. Tenancy Services guidance (not the Act itself, which uses "reasonable time") distinguishes between urgent and non-urgent issues.

**Urgent — expect action within 24 hours:**
- No hot water (particularly in winter — there's a [specific brief](/briefs/how-long-does-landlord-have-to-fix-hot-water-nz/) on this)
- Heating failure when outdoor temperatures are cold
- Security breach: a broken lock on an external door or window
- Burst pipe or significant water leak
- Loss of power to the property
- Blocked or overflowing sewage

These are situations where the property becomes unsafe or uninhabitable if not fixed immediately. Waiting 14 days for a response to a burst pipe is not a reasonable interpretation of "reasonable time." If your landlord takes a week to respond to a heating failure in June, that's a Tribunal case.

**Non-urgent — 14 days is a reasonable starting expectation:**

A broken dishwasher, a dripping tap that isn't wasting significant water, a sticky oven door — these still need to be fixed, but the 24-hour expectation doesn't apply. Tenancy Services guidance suggests approximately 14 days as a reasonable timeframe for a first request on non-urgent issues. The Act doesn't define a specific number of days — it requires action within a "reasonable time" given the nature and impact of the problem.

The practical rule: the more the issue affects habitability or health, the shorter the reasonable timeframe.

## Step 1: Put It in Writing

This is the step most tenants skip, and it costs them later.

A verbal request to a property manager is not a formal request. It won't create a paper trail for the Tribunal. It won't trigger the landlord's legal obligations in any documented way. A spoken "can you get that fixed?" is easy to misremember, delay, or ignore. Email is not.

Put it in writing the first time.

> **Template: First written maintenance request**
>
> Hi [Landlord/Property Manager name],
>
> I am writing to notify you of a maintenance issue at [property address]:
>
> [Describe the issue clearly and specifically — what it is, where it is, when you first noticed it, and how it is affecting your use of the property.]
>
> Under section 45(1)(b) of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, I am requesting that this be remedied as soon as reasonably practicable. Given the nature of this issue, I consider a reasonable timeframe to be [14 days / within 24 hours for urgent issues].
>
> Please confirm receipt and let me know when repairs will be scheduled.
>
> [Your name]
> [Date]

Send it via email or another method that creates a written record with a timestamp. Text is acceptable for urgent issues, but follow up with email. Keep the sent copy.

If you're submitting via a property management portal (some managers use apps for maintenance requests), submit through the portal AND send a parallel email. Portals have a habit of losing records when tenants change or managers shift.

---

## Step 2: Follow Up and Set a Deadline

If you haven't had a response within a few days, follow up in writing:

> **Template: Follow-up**
>
> Hi [name], following up on my email of [date] regarding [issue]. I haven't received a response. Could you please confirm when this will be attended to?

Keep it factual. Keep it documented. Two or three weeks of unanswered follow-ups on a maintenance issue is evidence at the Tribunal — not just frustration.

---

## Step 3: Notice to Remedy

If the issue hasn't been fixed within the timeframe you set, and your requests are being ignored or strung along, it's time to issue a formal Notice to Remedy.

A Notice to Remedy is a written notice that the landlord is in breach of their obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, with a specified deadline to fix it. If they don't meet the deadline, you can apply to the Tenancy Tribunal.

The notice doesn't follow a prescribed template — it needs to clearly:
1. State the specific breach (what is broken, which obligation it violates)
2. Reference the relevant law (section 45(1)(b) RTA 1986, or the specific Healthy Homes standard)
3. Set a deadline (14 days for non-urgent issues; 24 hours for urgent)
4. State that you will apply to the Tenancy Tribunal if not remedied by that date

> **Template: Notice to Remedy**
>
> To: [Landlord/Property Manager name]
> Re: Property address: [full address]
> Date: [date]
>
> **Notice to Remedy — Breach of Landlord Obligations**
>
> This is a formal Notice to Remedy under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986.
>
> I am notifying you that you are in breach of your obligations under section 45(1)(b) of the Residential Tenancies Act 1986 in relation to the following issue at the above property:
>
> [Describe the issue specifically: what it is, where it is, when it was first reported, and to whom.]
>
> This issue has not been remedied despite [my written request on [date] / correspondence since [date]].
>
> I require this to be remedied by [date — 14 days from today for non-urgent / 24 hours for urgent].
>
> If this issue is not remedied by the above date, I will apply to the Tenancy Tribunal for an order to remedy and, where appropriate, a rent reduction for the period the issue has remained unaddressed.
>
> [Your name]
> [Date]

Send via email. [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) also has a Notice to Remedy template on their website if you prefer their version.

As of 30 January 2025, under the Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024, notices can be served electronically to any address recorded in the tenancy agreement, including email.

## Step 4: Mediation

Before the Tenancy Tribunal, [Tenancy Services](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/) offers free mediation — a facilitated process with both parties present, aiming to reach a voluntary agreement. Mediation is typically faster than a Tribunal hearing (weeks rather than months) and doesn't require legal representation.

Most maintenance disputes that reach formal escalation resolve at mediation. The landlord sees you're serious; the mediator helps them understand their obligations; often a repair schedule and possibly a temporary rent reduction get agreed.

Apply online via tenancy.govt.nz or phone Tenancy Services on 0800 836 262.

---

## Step 5: Tenancy Tribunal

If mediation doesn't resolve it, the Tenancy Tribunal is the next step. Applications are made online through the [justice.govt.nz Tenancy Tribunal page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/tribunals/tenancy-tribunal/apply-to-the-tenancy-tribunal/).

What the Tribunal can order:
- Repair work to be completed by a specified date
- **Rent reduction** for the period the issue has been unresolved — backdated to when the problem arose
- **Exemplary damages** — up to $7,200 for landlords with fewer than six properties; up to $50,000 for larger landlords or property management companies
- Compensation for documented costs incurred because of the breach

The full process is covered in [how to apply to the Tenancy Tribunal](/posts/tenancy-tribunal-how-to-apply-nz/).

> **Key point:** The Tribunal looks at the documentation trail. A landlord who has not responded to written requests, ignored a Notice to Remedy, and skipped mediation looks significantly worse to an adjudicator than one who engaged at every step but disputed the scope of the required repair. The paper trail you've built from Step 1 is what makes the Tribunal effective.

## If Your Tenancy Is Managed by a Property Manager

The escalation path is the same — your Notice to Remedy goes to whoever is managing the property. But there's a development worth knowing about.

In March 2026, Cabinet agreed to develop legislation establishing a registration and complaints regime for residential property managers in New Zealand. The proposed model requires property managers to register, meet training requirements, and be subject to formal complaints and disciplinary proceedings. [The Ministry of Housing and Urban Development has the current detail](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/regulation-of-residential-property-managers).

This legislation is still being developed — it isn't law yet. But the direction is clear: property managers who are unresponsive to tenant maintenance requests will face more accountability options than they do currently. In the meantime, the Notice to Remedy and Tribunal process is your mechanism.

The [property manager vs private landlord guide](/posts/property-manager-vs-private-landlord/) covers the structural differences in how accountability works depending on who holds your tenancy.

RentRant's [responsiveness ratings](https://rentrant.nz/how-it-works) track how property managers perform on maintenance across their portfolios — it's the signal that doesn't appear in a listing description but is often what determines whether the next three years are manageable or a grind.

## Documentation Checklist

Build this file from the moment you first notice an issue:

- [ ] Written record of every request — date sent, method used, what you said
- [ ] Written record of every response — date received, what was said or not said
- [ ] Photos of the issue — dated, showing the specific area affected
- [ ] Notes on any impact to your use of the property (dates you couldn't use a room, dates you had no hot water, etc.)
- [ ] Copy of the Healthy Homes compliance statement if the issue relates to a Healthy Homes Standard
- [ ] Copy of your Notice to Remedy with the date you sent it
- [ ] Confirmation of delivery — email timestamp, or postal record if sent by post

If this ends up at the Tribunal, the adjudicator reviews all of the above. Start the file from day one.
